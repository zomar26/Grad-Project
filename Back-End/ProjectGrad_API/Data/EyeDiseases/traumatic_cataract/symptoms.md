# Symptoms of Traumatic Cataract

## Common Symptoms

Symptoms depend on the location, density, and speed of cataract development, as well as on associated ocular injuries.

- **Sudden or progressive blurred vision** – The most common symptom. Blur may be:
    - Immediate (penetrating trauma with capsular rupture)
    - Develop over hours to days (blunt contusion, electric injury)
    - Occur months to years later (delayed post‑contusion cataract)
- **Unilateral involvement** – Vision loss is confined to the traumatised eye; the fellow eye remains clear (unless bilateral trauma).

- **Glare and halos** – Light scatter through the opacity causes disabling glare, especially with posterior subcapsular or intumescent cataracts.

- **Monocular diplopia or polyopia** – Double or multiple images in the affected eye due to irregular lens opacities or partial dislocation.

- **Change in refractive error** – A myopic shift (increased nearsightedness) from lens swelling or a hyperopic shift (farsightedness) if the lens subluxates.

- **Visible lens damage** (noted by patient or family):
    - A grey/white spot in the pupil (mature cataract)
    - Iridescent “oil droplet” appearance (electric cataract)
    - Irregular pupil contour (associated with iris trauma or penetrating injury)

- **Floaters or flashes** – Not caused by the cataract itself, but very common with concurrent posterior vitreous detachment, vitreous haemorrhage, or retinal tear.

## Advanced Symptoms

- **Dense white cataract (mature/intumescent)** – Vision reduced to hand motion or light perception. The pupil may appear grey/white (leukocoria).

- **Severe eye pain** – **Red flag** for complications:
    - **Phacomorphic glaucoma** – A swollen lens pushes the iris forward, acutely raising intraocular pressure. Symptoms: intense pain, nausea/vomiting, headache, hazy cornea, mid‑dilated fixed pupil.
    - **Phacoanaphylactic uveitis** – Leaked lens protein causes granulomatous inflammation (mutton‑fat keratic precipitates, hypopyon).

- **Lens subluxation/dislocation** – The lens shifts due to zonular rupture. Symptoms:
    - Tremulous iris (iridodonesis)
    - Unilateral astigmatism or high refractive error
    - Aphakic crescent (edge of lens visible in the pupil)
    - Monocular diplopia

- **Chronic uveitis** – Low‑grade inflammation from retained lens material causes persistent pain, photophobia, and gradual vision loss.

- **Secondary glaucoma** – Lens material clogging the trabecular meshwork (phacolytic glaucoma) or chronic inflammation.

- **Amblyopia (children)** – If a unilateral traumatic cataract is not treated early, the brain suppresses the blurry image, leading to permanent visual impairment even after successful surgery.

## When to Seek Medical Attention

**Emergency (same day – go to emergency department)** :

- **Ocular trauma with sudden vision loss, pain, or visible abnormality** – Especially if there is a history of a high‑velocity projectile (hammering metal, grinding, explosion) – rule out intraocular foreign body.
- **Eye pain with nausea and vomiting** after trauma – suspect phacomorphic glaucoma.
- **Sudden worsening of vision with a red, painful eye** – possible acute glaucoma or lens‑induced uveitis.
- **Flashes and a sudden shower of floaters** – possible retinal tear/detachment (common after blunt trauma).

**Urgent (within 24–48 hours – call ophthalmologist)** :

- **Known ocular trauma with new or increasing blur** – even days to weeks after injury.
- **White/grey spot appearing in the pupil** after trauma.
- **Double vision in the injured eye** (monocular diplopia).
- **Light sensitivity out of proportion to injury**.

**Routine (schedule eye exam within weeks)** :

- **Months to years after old trauma, slowly worsening vision** – delayed traumatic cataract.
- **Annual follow‑up** for known zonular weakness or lens subluxation to monitor progression.

## Symptoms That May Affect Daily Activities

| Activity | Potential Symptom Impact |
|----------|--------------------------|
| Reading | Progressive blur, glare, monocular diplopia makes text hard to follow |
| Driving (daytime) | Glare from sun/windshields; inability to read signs if vision is reduced |
| Driving (night) | Severe glare and halos from oncoming headlights – often impossible with posterior subcapsular or intumescent cataract |
| Recognising faces | If central opacities are dense, faces become blurry |
| Occupational tasks | Fine visual work (surgeon, machinist, artist, driver) becomes impossible; risk of further trauma if working with machinery |
| Sports | Depth perception impaired (monocular vision); glare interferes with ball tracking |
| Walking at night | Glare from streetlights; reduced contrast sensitivity |
| School (children) | Trouble reading blackboard, textbooks – may be misdiagnosed as learning disability |
| Using smartphones/computer | Glare from screens; need for magnification and high contrast |

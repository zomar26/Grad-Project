# Traumatic Cataract

## Definition

A traumatic cataract is an opacity (clouding) of the natural crystalline lens that develops as a direct or indirect consequence of ocular trauma. It may occur immediately after injury or develop months to years later. Traumatic cataract is a leading cause of monocular (one‑eye) visual impairment in children and working‑age adults, particularly in settings where occupational or recreational eye injuries are common.

Unlike age‑related cataracts, traumatic cataracts often present asymmetrically (affecting only the injured eye) and may be associated with other ocular injuries, including corneal lacerations, hyphema (blood in the anterior chamber), lens dislocation (ectopia lentis), glaucoma, retinal detachment, or posterior segment trauma.

## How the Eye is Affected

The lens is a transparent, avascular structure encased in a thin elastic capsule. Trauma disrupts the normal architecture of the lens, allowing aqueous humor to enter the lens substance and/or triggering abnormal lens fiber proliferation.

**Mechanisms of opacification**:

1. **Capsular rupture** – A break in the lens capsule (most often anterior but also posterior) allows aqueous humor to flood the normally dehydrated lens fibers. Water influx causes rapid swelling, hydration, and opacification.

2. **Direct lens contusion** – Blunt force (e.g., fist, ball, airbag) can cause focal lens fiber damage without overt capsular rupture, leading to localized or progressive generalised opacity.

3. **Penetrating injury** – A sharp object (needle, metal fragment, glass) directly lacerates the lens capsule and often introduces foreign material.

4. **Electric shock / lightning** – High‑voltage current denatures lens proteins, causing a distinctive stellate or feathery cataract.

5. **Ionising radiation** – Therapeutic or accidental radiation damages lens epithelial cells, causing posterior subcapsular opacities after a latency period (months to years).

6. **Chemical injury** – Alkali or strong acid burns can penetrate the eye and damage the lens.

**Secondary effects**:

- **Phacoanaphylactic (lens‑induced) uveitis** – Leaked lens proteins incite an inflammatory reaction.
- **Lens‑induced glaucoma** – Lens material obstructs the trabecular meshwork, or a swollen (intumescent) cataract blocks the pupil (phacomorphic glaucoma).
- **Lens subluxation or dislocation** – Partial or complete zonular rupture destabilises the lens.
- **Retained lens particles** – Fragments may induce chronic inflammation or glaucoma.

## Types or Stages

Traumatic cataracts are classified by morphology, mechanism, and timing.

### By Mechanism

| Type | Cause | Typical Appearance |
|------|-------|--------------------|
| **Blunt (contusion) cataract** | Blunt force (fist, ball, airbag, car crash) | Stellate (star‑shaped), rosette, or petal‑shaped anterior or posterior opacity; often delayed (weeks–years). |
| **Penetrating cataract** | Sharp object (needle, knife, metal/glass shard) | Focal or complete opacification, often rapid (hours–days). Associated with capsular laceration. |
| **Electric cataract** | High‑voltage electrical injury (>200 V) | Fine stellate or feathery anterior/subcapsular opacities; may appear early (days) or late (months). |
| **Radiation cataract** | Ionising radiation (therapeutic, nuclear accident) | Posterior subcapsular opacity, often progressive; latency of months to years. |
| **Chemical cataract** | Alkali or acid burn | Complete opacification, usually with severe anterior segment necrosis. |

### By Timing (Clinical Course)

- **Immediate** – Opacity present at the time of injury (typically penetrating trauma or severe blunt trauma with capsular rupture).
- **Acute / progressive** – Opacity develops within days to weeks (most common after blunt trauma with intact capsule).
- **Delayed (late onset)** – Cataract appears months to years after injury, often following blunt contusion without initial obvious opacity.

### By Anatomic Extent

- **Anterior subcapsular** – Confined to anterior pole (blunt trauma, electric injury).
- **Posterior subcapsular** – Associated with radiation or less commonly blunt trauma.
- **Cortical** – Wedge‑shaped peripheral opacities.
- **Total (mature)** – Complete lens opacification.
- **Intumescent** – Swollen, hydrated cataract that may cause phacomorphic glaucoma.
- **Morgagnian** – Hypermature, liquefied cortex with sinking nucleus (rare in trauma).

## Key Facts

- **Prevalence**: Traumatic cataract accounts for 30–50% of unilateral cataracts in children and young adults. Up to 5% of all ocular traumas result in cataract.

- **Blunt trauma is the most common cause** in civilian life (sports injuries, assaults, motor vehicle crashes, falls). Penetrating trauma dominates in combat and industrial settings.

- **Bilateral involvement** is rare; the uninjured eye usually remains clear (unless bilateral trauma occurs).

- **Intraocular foreign body (IOFB)** – Any penetrating injury with a retained metallic or organic fragment carries a high risk of cataract, as well as endophthalmitis.

- **Associated injuries** are the rule, not the exception: hyphema (50–70% of blunt trauma), angle recession (glaucoma risk), zonular dialysis (lens subluxation), vitreous hemorrhage, retinal detachment.

- **Phacomorphic glaucoma** – A swollen, intumescent traumatic cataract can push the iris forward, closing the angle and raising intraocular pressure to 40–60 mmHg; this is a sight‑threatening emergency.

- **Lens‑induced uveitis (phacoanaphylaxis)** – Leaked lens protein triggers a granulomatous reaction weeks to months after capsular rupture. Treat with corticosteroids and cataract extraction.

- **Surgery is the only treatment**; timing depends on the presence of inflammation, glaucoma, and concurrent ocular injuries.

- **Pediatric traumatic cataract** – Management is challenging because of high rates of posterior capsule opacification (PCO), amblyopia, and inflammatory responses. Early surgery with primary posterior capsulotomy and anterior vitrectomy is often required.

- **Prevention is effective** – Use of protective eyewear (polycarbonate lenses) in sports, construction, and military settings reduces traumatic cataract by up to 90% in high‑risk activities.

# Causes and Risk Factors of Traumatic Cataract

## Main Causes

Traumatic cataract results from mechanical, electrical, radiation, or chemical injury to the crystalline lens.

### Blunt (Contusion) Trauma – Most Common (>50% of traumatic cataracts)

- **Mechanism**: The lens is compressed and stretched; aqueous humours reverberates through the eye, striking the lens from behind (posterior capsular rupture) or causing metabolic damage to lens fibres.
- **Examples**:
    - Sports: fist, ball (baseball, cricket, basketball, squash), puck, stick
    - Assaults: punch, kick
    - Falls: striking the eye on a sharp edge
    - Motor vehicle crashes (airbag deployment)
    - Occupational: machinery recoil, falling objects
- **Typical cataract**: Rosette or stellate anterior/posterior subcapsular opacity; often delayed (weeks–years).

### Penetrating and Perforating Injuries

- **Mechanism**: Direct capsular laceration allows aqueous humour to enter the lens, causing rapid hydration and opacity.
- **Examples**:
    - Sharp objects: knife, scissors, needle, pencil
    - High‑velocity projectiles: metal or glass shards (industrial grinding, explosions), airgun pellets, BB guns, fragments from hammering metal (“metal‑on‑metal injury”)
- **Typical cataract**: Focal or total opacity, often immediate or within hours.
- **Intraocular foreign body (IOFB)** – Retained metallic or organic material often accelerates cataract and elevates endophthalmitis risk.

### Electric Cataract

- **Mechanism**: High‑voltage current (>200 V) damages lens epithelium and denatures lens proteins.
- **Sources**: Lightning strike, high‑tension power lines, electrical accidents (e.g., handling live wires).
- **Typical cataract**: Fine stellate, feathery, or vacuolar anterior subcapsular opacities. May appear acutely (days) or be delayed (months–years).

### Radiation Cataract

- **Mechanism**: Ionising radiation damages lens epithelial cells, especially at the posterior pole.
- **Sources**:
    - Therapeutic: head/neck radiotherapy for tumours, total body irradiation (bone marrow transplant)
    - Occupational: historic radiologists, nuclear workers (modern shielding reduces risk)
    - Accidental: nuclear accidents (e.g., Chernobyl)
- **Typical cataract**: Posterior subcapsular opacity; latency of months to decades, dose‑dependent.

### Chemical Cataract

- **Mechanism**: Severe alkali or acid burns penetrate the cornea and directly damage lens proteins.
- **Sources**: Industrial cleaning agents (sodium hydroxide, lime), battery acid.
- **Typical cataract**: Complete opacification, usually with extensive anterior segment necrosis.

## Risk Factors

### Demographics

- **Male sex** – Men are 3–5 times more likely to sustain ocular trauma than women (occupational, sports, assault).
- **Age** – Children (6–18 years) and young adults (20–40 years) are at highest risk.
- **Low socioeconomic status** – Lack of protective eyewear, hazardous occupations.

### Occupational Hazards

- **Construction workers** (hammering, grinding, drilling, welding)
- **Industrial workers** (lathes, presses, high‑pressure hoses)
- **Agricultural workers** (thorns, plant material, machinery)
- **Mechanics, carpenters, electricians**

### Recreational / Sports Activities

- **Contact sports** – boxing, martial arts, football, rugby, basketball
- **Ball sports** – baseball, cricket, squash, tennis, hockey
- **Shooting sports** – airguns, BB guns, paintball, pellet guns
- **High‑speed activities** – cycling (without eye protection), bungee jumping

### Other Factors

- **Illegal fireworks** – Major cause of pediatric open‑globe injuries
- **Domestic violence / assault** – frequently underreported
- **Motor vehicle crashes** – airbag deployment can cause blunt lens injuries
- **Prior ocular surgery or weak zonules** – pseudoexfoliation, Marfan syndrome – predispose to lens dislocation with even trivial trauma
- **Alcohol intoxication** – increases risk of falls and assaults

## Genetic Factors

No specific genes cause traumatic cataract, but **predisposing conditions** that weaken zonules or make the lens more vulnerable:

- **Marfan syndrome (FBN1 mutations)** – Zonular weakness → lens subluxation after minor trauma.
- **Homocystinuria (CBS mutations)** – Lens dislocation (ectopia lentis).
- **Weill‑Marchesani syndrome** – Spherophakia, lenticular myopia, zonular laxity.
- **Pseudoexfoliation syndrome (LOXL1 variants)** – Weak zonules, higher risk of lens dislocation.
- **Retinitis pigmentosa** – The lens may be more susceptible to trauma? Not established.

Genetic counselling is not required for isolated traumatic cataract unless a syndromic cause (e.g., Marfan) is suspected.

## Environmental Factors

- **Workplace safety standards** – Lack of mandatory protective eyewear increases risk.
- **Access to firearms / airguns** – Especially in children and adolescents.
- **Geographic region** – Higher rates of agricultural or industrial trauma in rural areas.
- **Fireworks availability** – Countries with unregulated fireworks have higher rates of pediatric traumatic cataract.
- **Substance use** – Alcohol and drug use preceding injury.

## Prevention Strategies

**Primary prevention (avoiding injury)** – Highly effective.

- **Protective eyewear (polycarbonate lenses)** – Mandated in many occupational and sports settings.
    - Industry: ANSI Z87.1 certified safety glasses
    - Sports: ASTM F803 certified eyewear (basketball, racquet sports, baseball, hockey)
- **Workplace engineering controls** – Machine guards, safety shields, proper training.
- **Fireworks bans / restrictions** – Public education; avoid home fireworks.
- **Proper use of tools** – Never use a hammer on metal without safety glasses (metal‑on‑metal injuries are a classic cause of penetrating trauma).
- **Airbag safety** – Sit at least 10 inches from the steering wheel.

**Secondary prevention (minimising sequelae after injury)** :

- **Immediate medical evaluation** – Any ocular trauma with vision change, pain, or visible abnormality requires same‑day exam.
- **Primary repair of corneal/scleral laceration** – Restore globe integrity.
- **Removal of intraocular foreign body** – Urgent to prevent endophthalmitis and progressive cataract.
- **Control inflammation** – Topical corticosteroids for traumatic uveitis.
- **Intraocular pressure control** – Monitor for and treat secondary glaucoma.
- **Amblyopia prevention in children** – Prompt surgery if visual axis is involved; treat the fellow eye if needed.

**Key message**: Traumatic cataract is almost entirely preventable with the use of appropriate protective eyewear. If an injury occurs, immediate ophthalmologic evaluation is critical to manage associated damage and preserve vision.

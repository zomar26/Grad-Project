# Treatment of Traumatic Cataract

## Available Treatments

The only definitive treatment for a visually significant traumatic cataract is **surgical removal of the opacified lens** with implantation of an intraocular lens (IOL), often combined with management of concurrent ocular injuries (corneal laceration, hyphema, glaucoma, vitreoretinal trauma).

**Medical management is only temporary** – to control inflammation, intraocular pressure, or to delay surgery until the eye is stable.

## Medications

### Preoperative or Non‑Surgical Medical Therapy

- **Topical corticosteroids** (prednisolone acetate 1%, difluprednate 0.05%) – Control traumatic uveitis, reduce post‑traumatic inflammation. Taper slowly.
- **Cycloplegics** (atropine 1%, cyclopentolate 1%) – Dilate pupil, relieve ciliary spasm (pain), prevent posterior synechiae.
- **Topical antibiotics** – For any penetrating injury or at time of primary wound repair.
- **Intraocular pressure (IOP)‑lowering drugs** – For traumatic glaucoma or phacomorphic glaucoma:
    - Topical beta‑blockers (timolol)
    - Alpha‑agonists (brimonidine)
    - Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (dorzolamide drops, oral acetazolamide)
- **Oral corticosteroids** – For severe phacoanaphylactic uveitis or persistent inflammation after trauma.
- **Anti‑VEGF injections** – For concurrent traumatic choroidal neovascularisation (rare).

### **No medication reverses the cataract** – lens opacity is permanent once formed.

## Surgical Options

### Timing of Surgery

| Clinical Scenario | Recommended Timing |
|------------------|--------------------|
| **Dense cataract blocking view of posterior segment** (no view of retina) | **Urgent** (within days) if suspected open‑globe or retinal detachment |
| **Phacomorphic glaucoma** (IOP uncontrolled with meds) | **Emergency** (same day) – lens extraction to break the angle‑closure |
| **Lens‑induced uveitis (phacoanaphylaxis)** unresponsive to steroids | **Urgent** (within days) |
| **Penetrating injury with lens capsular rupture and cortical material in anterior chamber** | **Primary cataract extraction at time of wound repair** (within 24–48 hours) |
| **Blunt traumatic cataract, stable eye, no inflammation/glaucoma, vision >20/50** | **Elective, often delayed** for weeks–months to allow posterior segment evaluation and stabilisation |
| **Pediatric traumatic cataract** | **Early** (weeks) to prevent amblyopia; concurrent or early secondary IOL controversial |

### Surgical Techniques

#### 1. Cataract Extraction Alone (No IOL)

- **Indications** – Uncontrolled inflammation, high risk of recurrent uveitis, young children (<2 years where IOL is avoided), inadequate capsular support for IOL.
- **Method**: Phacoemulsification or manual small‑incision cataract surgery; aspirate lens material, leave aphakic (no IOL).

#### 2. Cataract Extraction + Intraocular Lens (IOL) Implantation

- **Indications** – Stable eye, no active infection, adequate capsular support, patient age >2–4 years (in children).
- **IOL type choices**:
    - Monofocal IOL – most common.
    - Toric IOL – if pre‑existing or traumatic corneal astigmatism >1.0 D.
    - Multifocal IOL – generally **avoided** in traumatic cataract due to higher risk of postoperative inflammation and visual aberrations.
- **Capsular support considerations**:
    - **Intact capsular bag** – Standard in‑the‑bag IOL.
    - **Compromised zonules / capsular rupture** – Scleral‑fixated IOL, iris‑claw IOL, or anterior chamber IOL (ACIOL).

#### 3. Combined Procedures (Complex Trauma Cases)

- **Penetrating injury repair (corneal/scleral laceration) + cataract extraction + IOL (same sitting)**
- **Cataract + vitrectomy** – For posterior capsular rupture with vitreous in anterior chamber, or intraocular foreign body.
- **Cataract + trabeculectomy / glaucoma drainage device** – For pre‑existing or traumatic glaucoma.
- **Cataract + retinal detachment repair** – Combined vitrectomy, lensectomy, gas/silicone oil.

### Special Considerations

- **Intraocular foreign body (IOFB)** – Remove foreign body (magnet, forceps) at same time as cataract extraction.
- **Pediatric traumatic cataract** – High rate of posterior capsule opacification (PCO) and amblyopia. Often perform **primary posterior capsulotomy + anterior vitrectomy** at time of cataract surgery. IOL implantation is often delayed or performed in a second procedure for children <2–4 years.

### Postoperative Management

- Topical corticosteroids + antibiotics + cycloplegics (duration longer than routine cataract surgery – often 4–8 weeks).
- Monitor for cystoid macular edema (CME), elevated IOP, persistent uveitis, retinal detachment.
- YAG laser capsulotomy – Frequently needed if posterior capsule is left intact (especially in children).

## Lifestyle Recommendations

- **Eye protection** – After surgery, continue wearing protective eyewear during high‑risk activities.
- **Avoid rubbing the eye** – Especially important if zonules are weak or after penetrating injury.
- **Sun protection** – UV‑blocking sunglasses to reduce postoperative photophobia and light sensitivity.
- **Follow‑up compliance** – Critical to detect and treat complications (CME, glaucoma, PCO, amblyopia in children).

## Treatment Limitations

- **Irreversible vision loss from concurrent injuries** – Corneal scarring, macular damage, optic neuropathy, or retinal detachment may limit final vision even with successful cataract surgery.
- **Amblyopia (children)** – If surgery is delayed beyond the amblyopic period (usually 7–10 years), permanent subnormal vision may occur.
- **No IOL is perfect** – Aphakic patients need high‑powered spectacles or contact lenses, which may limit peripheral vision and cause image minification.
- **Chronic complications** – Post‑traumatic glaucoma, retinal detachment, persistent uveitis, corneal decompensation.
- **Multiple surgeries** – Complex trauma often requires staged procedures (primary globe repair, later cataract extraction, later IOL implantation, later glaucoma surgery).

## Prognosis

### Visual Outcomes

| Injury Severity | Typical Final Visual Acuity (with modern surgery) | Limitations |
|----------------|---------------------------------------------------|--------------|
| **Isolated blunt traumatic cataract, intact capsule, no other damage** | 20/20 – 20/40 | Excellent |
| **Penetrating injury without IOFB, successful primary repair** | 20/25 – 20/60 | Depends on corneal scar |
| **Open‑globe injury with IOFB and vitreous haemorrhage** | 20/40 – 20/200 | Often limited by retinal/optic nerve damage |
| **Severe contusion with macular hole / choroidal rupture** | 20/80 – counting fingers | Poor central vision |
| **Pediatric traumatic cataract (with early surgery, no amblyopia)** | 20/20 – 20/40 | Excellent |
| **Pediatric traumatic cataract (delayed surgery, established amblyopia)** | 20/100 – 20/400 | Poor, despite clear visual axis |

### Factors Associated with Better Prognosis

- Isolated blunt trauma without capsular rupture
- Immediate or early presentation (<24 hours)
- No intraocular foreign body
- No retinal or optic nerve damage
- Age >20 years (amblyopia not a concern)
- Good capsular support for IOL
- No secondary glaucoma

### Factors Associated with Worse Prognosis

- Open‑globe injury
- Delayed treatment (>1 week)
- Intraocular foreign body (especially organic)
- Posterior segment trauma (retinal detachment, macular hole, choroidal rupture)
- Severe contusion with angle recession → glaucoma
- Children with delayed surgery → amblyopia
- Repeated surgeries for complications

**Key message**: Traumatic cataract is highly treatable with modern microsurgery, and visual outcomes are excellent when the cataract is the only problem. However, concurrent ocular injuries (corneal scar, glaucoma, macular damage, retinal detachment) often determine the final vision. Prompt evaluation and individualised surgery are essential.

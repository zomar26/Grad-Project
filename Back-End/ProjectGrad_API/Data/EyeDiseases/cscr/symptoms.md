# Symptoms of Central Serous Chorioretinopathy (CSCR)

## Common Symptoms

The onset of acute CSCR is often sudden, and patients frequently report waking up with symptoms. Unlike many retinal diseases, CSCR is typically painless.

- **Blurred or dim central vision (unilateral)** – Almost always affects one eye at a time (bilateral involvement occurs in 10–15% over time). Vision is typically reduced to the 20/25–20/70 range in acute cases, though 20/20 may be reported despite functional deficits.

- **Metamorphopsia (visual distortion)** – A hallmark symptom. Straight lines (e.g., door frames, window blinds, telephone poles, text lines) appear:
    - Wavy, bent, or crooked
    - Smaller or larger in the affected area (micropsia or macropsia)
    - As if looking through a "rippled glass" or "water surface"

- **Central or paracentral scotoma (blind spot)** – A dark, gray, or hazy area in the central vision. Patients may describe:
    - "A grayish spot right where I'm looking"
    - "A hole in my vision"
    - Difficulty reading because the middle of words disappears

- **Hypermetropic (far-sighted) shift** – The serous detachment changes the refractive power of the eye. Patients may notice:
    - Their usual glasses or contact lenses feel "too strong" (distant blur, near vision preserved)
    - Improvement in near vision (unexpected ability to read without reading glasses)

- **Reduced color vision (dyschromatopsia)** – Colors appear less vivid, faded, or slightly yellow/brown (particularly blues and greens). Often subtle and only noticed when comparing between eyes.

- **Micropsia (objects appear smaller)** – A specific form of metamorphopsia where objects look smaller than they are. For example:
    - A coffee cup appears miniature
    - Text on a page looks compacted

- **Prolonged dark adaptation** – Difficulty adjusting when moving from bright to dim lighting (e.g., entering a movie theater, walking indoors on a sunny day). This can persist even after subretinal fluid resolves.

- **Glare sensitivity (mild)** – Bothersome from headlights or bright sunlight, though less severe than in cortical cataracts.

## Advanced Symptoms

Chronic or recurrent CSCR leads to progressive RPE and photoreceptor damage, producing additional symptoms:

- **Progressive central vision decline** – Visual acuity worsens over months to years to 20/80–20/200 range, despite intermittent fluid resolution.

- **Permanent central scotoma** – A fixed, dense blind spot in the center of vision that does not improve when fluid resolves. Caused by photoreceptor atrophy.

- **Metamorphopsia becomes fixed** – No longer fluctuates with fluid; distortion persists even after anatomical resolution due to photoreceptor disarray.

- **Reduced contrast sensitivity** – Difficulty distinguishing objects from their background, particularly in low light or low contrast situations (e.g., seeing white steps against a white wall, navigating in fog).

- **Choroidal neovascularization (CNV)** – In 2–5% of chronic CSCR, abnormal blood vessels grow beneath the retina, causing:
    - Sudden worsening of vision over days to weeks
    - New metamorphopsia
    - Subretinal hemorrhage (visible as dark spots in vision)

- **Bullous retinal detachment (rare)** – Large, shifting subretinal fluid that can involve the peripheral retina. Symptoms include:
    - Severe, widespread vision loss
    - Floaters (uncommon in typical CSCR)
    - Visual field loss beyond the central area

- **Outer retinal atrophy** – On advanced imaging, thinning of the outer nuclear layer (photoreceptor cell bodies) leads to irreversible vision loss, often with photophobia and poor night vision.

## When to Seek Medical Attention

**Urgent (within days to 1–2 weeks)** – Schedule an ophthalmology appointment promptly if you develop:

- **Sudden, painless central vision changes** in one eye (blur, distortion, or a gray spot)
- **New-onset metamorphopsia** (wavy straight lines)
- **Sudden increase in near vision** (unexpected ability to read without glasses)

**Note**: CSCR is not a medical emergency (it does not cause sudden blindness overnight), but early diagnosis allows for risk factor modification and consideration of treatment for persistent cases.

**Routine (schedule within weeks to months)** :

- Known CSCR with no improvement after 3–4 months – evaluation for treatment options
- Recurrent symptoms after prior resolution
- Follow-up imaging for chronic CSCR (every 3–6 months)

**Seek immediate (same day) emergency care** if you experience:

- **Sudden, painless vision loss** over hours (not days) – could indicate retinal artery occlusion, vitreous hemorrhage, or retinal detachment
- **New flashes of light or sudden increase in floaters** – possible posterior vitreous detachment or retinal tear (not typical of CSCR)
- **A dark curtain or shadow across vision** – potential retinal detachment

**Pregnancy note**: Pregnant patients with CSCR symptoms should inform their obstetrician and ophthalmologist; most cases are managed conservatively (no treatment until postpartum unless severe).

## Symptoms That May Affect Daily Activities

| Activity | Symptom Impact |
|----------|----------------|
| Reading | Words break in the middle (scotoma); lines appear wavy (metamorphopsia); need for larger print or magnification; text may look "crowded" or "compressed" (micropsia) |
| Driving | Difficulty reading road signs (central blur); trouble judging distances due to distortion; night driving problematic if contrast sensitivity reduced |
| Computer work | Straight lines (borders, text rows) appear wavy; icons may look misshapen; eye strain from attempting to focus through fluid |
| Recognizing faces | Central scotoma covers the nose/eyes/mouth area; patients may recognize a face only by peripheral vision (e.g., hairline, jaw) |
| Watching television | Subtitles become unreadable; actors' faces appear distorted; fine details (sports scores, clocks) are missed |
| Using smartphones/tablets | Icons appear shifted; text lines break; micropsia makes fonts look smaller than actual size |
| Reading analog clocks | Distortion makes it impossible to see where the hands point relative to numbers |
| Outdoor activities | Glare from sunlight worsens contrast loss; difficulty seeing golf balls, tennis balls, or other small objects against grass/sky |
| Occupational tasks | Pilots, surgeons, machinists, and others requiring fine central vision may be unable to perform duties; driving-based jobs (trucking, taxi) may be temporarily or permanently restricted |
| Walking on uneven ground | Reduced contrast and distortion lead to misjudging stair heights, curbs, or cracks |
| Taking medications | Difficulty reading small-print labels (scotoma); may mistake pills if labels are not magnified |
| Hobbies (sewing, painting, woodworking) | Fine detail work becomes impossible; distortion alters perceived shape of workpieces |

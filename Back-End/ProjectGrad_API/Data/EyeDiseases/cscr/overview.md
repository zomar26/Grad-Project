# Central Serous Chorioretinopathy (CSCR)

## Definition

Central serous chorioretinopathy (CSCR) is a relatively uncommon retinal disorder characterized by the accumulation of fluid (serous detachment) beneath the macula—the central portion of the retina responsible for sharp, detailed vision. The fluid accumulates in the potential space between the neurosensory retina and the underlying retinal pigment epithelium (RPE), creating a localized detachment that distorts and reduces central vision.

CSCR typically affects young to middle-aged adults (ages 30–50) and is more common in males than females by a ratio of approximately 6:1 (or higher in some studies). The condition is often self-limiting, with spontaneous resolution in 70–90% of cases within 3–6 months. However, persistent or recurrent episodes can lead to permanent vision loss due to progressive RPE and photoreceptor damage.

## How the Eye is Affected

**Primary structures involved**:
1. **Choroid** – The vascular layer beneath the retina. In CSCR, the choroid becomes abnormally thickened and hyperpermeable (leaky), a condition called "pachychoroid" (thick choroid).
2. **Retinal pigment epithelium (RPE)** – A monolayer of cells that normally acts as a pump, removing fluid from the subretinal space and transporting it into the choroid. In CSCR, the RPE develops one or more focal leaks or widespread dysfunction.
3. **Neurosensory retina** – The light-sensing layer (photoreceptors). When fluid accumulates between the RPE and neurosensory retina, photoreceptors are displaced from their normal position, causing metamorphopsia (distortion) and blurred vision.

**Mechanism** (current understanding, based on multimodal imaging):

- **Step 1**: Increased choroidal vascular permeability and congestion lead to thickening of the choroid (pachychoroid). The cause of this is not fully understood but involves glucocorticoid (stress hormone) pathways, sympathetic nervous system activation, and possibly genetic predisposition.

- **Step 2**: Increased hydrostatic pressure or direct leakage from the choroid overwhelms the pumping capacity of the RPE. The RPE normally transports fluid from the retina into the choroid; when overwhelmed, fluid accumulates in the subretinal space.

- **Step 3**: Focal RPE leaks (visible on fluorescein angiography) allow fluid to flow from the choroid into the subretinal space, creating a serous detachment of the neurosensory retina.

- **Step 4**: Persistent fluid leads to photoreceptor outer segment damage, RPE atrophy, and eventually permanent central vision loss if untreated or recurrent.

## Types or Stages

CSCR is classified based on imaging characteristics, duration, and clinical course:

### 1. Acute CSCR (typical)
- **Duration**: < 4–6 months
- **Findings**: Localized neurosensory retinal detachment on optical coherence tomography (OCT); one or more focal RPE leaks on fluorescein angiography (FA)
- **Prognosis**: 70–90% resolve spontaneously with good visual recovery

### 2. Chronic (persistent) CSCR
- **Duration**: Fluid present for > 6 months
- **Findings**: More widespread RPE changes ("gravitational tracks" or "descending tracts" of RPE atrophy), diffuse RPE leakage on FA, often without a single focal leak
- **Complications**: Progressive photoreceptor loss, RPE atrophy, permanent central vision reduction
- **Prognosis**: Less likely to resolve spontaneously; often requires treatment

### 3. Recurrent CSCR
- **Definition**: One or more episodes of acute CSCR after complete resolution
- **Risk factors**: Uncontrolled risk factors (stress, corticosteroid use, type A personality)
- **Prognosis**: Each recurrence increases risk of chronic changes and permanent vision loss

### 4. Atypical variants (less common)
- **Bullous CSCR**: Large, exudative retinal detachments mimicking uveitis or choroidal tumors; rare
- **Chronic CSCR with choroidal neovascularization (CNV)**: Abnormal blood vessels develop secondary to chronic RPE damage (2–5% of chronic cases)

### 5. Pachychoroid spectrum disorders (related diagnoses)
- **Pachychoroid pigment epitheliopathy (PPE)** – RPE changes without subretinal fluid (early form)
- **Pachychoroid neovasculopathy (PNV)** – CNV arising in pachychoroid eyes without drusen
- **Polypoidal choroidal vasculopathy (PCV)** – Aneurysmal dilations of choroidal vessels; more common in Asian populations

## Key Facts

- **Demographics**: Peak incidence age 35–45 years; male:female ratio ~6:1 (range 3:1 to 10:1 across studies)

- **Exogenous corticosteroid association**: Systemic, inhaled, topical (skin), or even epidural corticosteroids are identified as a cause or exacerbating factor in 30–50% of cases. Intranasal steroids (for allergies) are also a risk factor.

- **Endogenous hypercortisolism**: Cushing's syndrome, severe psychological stress, and type A (competitive, time-urgent) personality are associated with increased risk.

- **Spontaneous resolution rate**: 70–90% within 3–6 months for first episodes; chronic cases have lower spontaneous resolution rates (30–50% over 12 months).

- **Recurrence rate**: 30–50% within 1–3 years after resolution, particularly if risk factors are not controlled.

- **Vision outcomes**: Most acute CSCR patients return to 20/20–20/30 vision after resolution. However, 20–30% experience persistent visual symptoms (metamorphopsia, reduced contrast sensitivity, mild color desaturation) despite normal acuity.

- **Permanent vision loss risk**: Approximately 10–15% of chronic CSCR patients develop legal blindness (20/200 or worse) in the affected eye over 5–10 years due to RPE atrophy and photoreceptor loss.

- **Association with pregnancy**: CSCR can occur in pregnancy, particularly in the third trimester, likely due to hormonal and hemodynamic changes. Usually resolves postpartum but may recur in subsequent pregnancies.

- **Imaging gold standards**: OCT (detects fluid and photoreceptor integrity) and fluorescein angiography (identifies RPE leaks). Indocyanine green angiography (ICGA) reveals choroidal hyperpermeability and is essential for chronic cases.

- **No approved pharmacologic therapy** (as of 2025). Treatments include laser photocoagulation (for extrafoveal leaks), photodynamic therapy (PDT, first-line for chronic CSCR), and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (off-label).

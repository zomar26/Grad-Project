# Treatment of Central Serous Chorioretinopathy (CSCR)

## Available Treatments

Treatment is **not always necessary** for acute CSCR, given the high rate of spontaneous resolution (70–90% within 3–6 months). Intervention is indicated for:

- Persistent subretinal fluid for ≥ 4–6 months (chronic CSCR)
- Recurrent episodes causing progressive RPE damage
- Severe vision loss or significant occupational/quality-of-life impairment
- Bullous or extensive retinal detachment
- Secondary choroidal neovascularization (CNV)

Treatment options are categorized by evidence strength:

| Intervention | Indication | Evidence Level |
|--------------|------------|----------------|
| Observation (no treatment) | First episode, symptoms < 4 months | Strong (for spontaneous resolution) |
| Risk factor modification | All patients (primary and secondary prevention) | Strong |
| Focal laser photocoagulation | Extrafoveal (away from fovea) RPE leak | Strong |
| Photodynamic therapy (PDT) with verteporfin | Chronic CSCR, subfoveal leak, diffuse RPE disease | Strong (first-line for chronic) |
| Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (eplerenone, spironolactone) | Chronic CSCR (off-label) | Moderate (mixed trial results) |
| Anti-VEGF injections | CSCR with secondary CNV only | Strong for CNV; not for pure CSCR |
| Micropulse laser (subthreshold) | Chronic CSCR (alternative to PDT) | Moderate (emerging evidence) |

## Medications

### No FDA-approved medications for CSCR. The following are used off-label:

**Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs)**

- **Eplerenone (Inspra®)** – Selective mineralocorticoid receptor blocker.
    - *Dosing*: 25–50 mg daily.
    - *Mechanism*: Reduces choroidal vascular permeability; may enhance RPE fluid transport. Based on observation that CSCR resembles mineralocorticoid-induced retinopathy in animal models.
    - *Evidence*: Mixed results. Early small trials showed accelerated fluid resolution and improved vision. Subsequent larger randomized trials (e.g., VICI trial, 2020) showed **no significant benefit** over placebo for visual acuity or fluid resolution at 12 months. However, some meta-analyses suggest possible benefit in subpopulations (e.g., shorter duration CSCR).
    - *Current role*: Considered by some specialists for chronic CSCR when PDT is unavailable or declined, but efficacy is debated. Not first-line.

- **Spironolactone (Aldactone®)** – Non-selective MRA (also anti-androgen).
    - *Dosing*: 25–50 mg daily.
    - *Evidence*: Similar to eplerenone; mixed results.
    - *Side effects*: Gynecomastia (male breast enlargement), menstrual irregularities, hyperkalemia (elevated potassium).
    - *Monitoring*: Check serum potassium and renal function at baseline and 1 month after initiation.

**Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors** – Acetazolamide (Diamox®) has been used historically to reduce subretinal fluid via RPE pump stimulation. Limited evidence; not recommended as first-line due to side effects (paresthesias, fatigue, kidney stones, metabolic acidosis).

**Anti-VEGF agents** (ranibizumab, aflibercept, bevacizumab) – **Not indicated** for non-neovascular CSCR. Anti-VEGF does not reduce choroidal hyperpermeability (VEGF is not the primary driver). Only used when CNV complicates CSCR (2–5% of chronic cases).

### Corticosteroids – **Contraindicated** in CSCR. Systemic or local corticosteroids exacerbate fluid accumulation, delay resolution, and trigger recurrences. Never use intravitreal steroid implants (Ozurdex, Iluvien) for CSCR.

## Surgical Options

**Focal (conventional) laser photocoagulation**

- **Indication**: Extrafoveal (≥ 200–300 μm from foveal center) RPE leak confirmed on fluorescein angiography.
- **Procedure**: Thermal laser burns the leaking RPE site, creating a scar that seals the leak.
- **Success rate**: 80–90% resolution of subretinal fluid within 1–3 months.
- **Limitations**: Cannot be used for subfoveal leaks (laser scar would cause permanent central scotoma). Does not address underlying choroidal hyperpermeability.
- **Recurrence rate**: ~20–30% after successful treatment (often at different locations).

**Photodynamic therapy (PDT) with verteporfin (Visudyne®)**

- **Indication**: Chronic CSCR (> 4–6 months), subfoveal or juxtafoveal leaks, diffuse RPE disease, or failed/conventional laser not possible.
- **Mechanism**: Intravenous verteporfin (light-activated drug) accumulates in hyperpermeable choroidal vessels. Laser activation (689 nm) selectively closes abnormal choroidal vessels and reduces choroidal congestion without damaging the overlying retina.
- **Procedure**:
    - Verteporfin infusion over 10 minutes
    - 15 minutes later, apply 689 nm laser (50 J/cm², 83 seconds) to the area of choroidal hyperpermeability (typically 3000–6000 μm diameter)
- **Dosing**: Standard fluence (50 J/cm²) or reduced fluence (25–30 J/cm²). Reduced-fluence PDT is preferred to minimize RPE atrophy risk.
- **Outcomes**:
    - Complete fluid resolution: 70–90% after 1–3 months
    - Visual acuity improvement: Mean gain of 5–15 ETDRS letters
    - One to three sessions typically required; repeat if persistent or recurrent fluid
- **Side effects**:
    - Transient vision decrease (first week) – resolves
    - RPE atrophy (over-treatment) – minimized with reduced fluence
    - Choroidal neovascularization (<1%)
    - Photosensitivity (avoid bright light for 5 days)
- **Current status**: First-line therapy for chronic CSCR (AAO Preferred Practice Pattern, European Retina Society guidelines).

**Subthreshold (micropulse) laser**

- **Indication**: Chronic CSCR (alternative to PDT; may be safer for foveal leaks)
- **Mechanism**: 810 nm diode laser with micropulse duty cycle (5–15% on time) heats RPE without coagulative necrosis. Stimulates RPE cell repair without scarring.
- **Advantages**: No visible burn; can be applied over fovea; no risk of RPE atrophy.
- **Evidence**: Moderate. Multiple studies show 60–80% fluid resolution with good safety. Less effective than PDT in head-to-head trials (lower complete resolution rates).
- **Availability**: Not all centers have micropulse lasers.

**Verteporfin PDT vs. micropulse laser** – Most comparative studies show PDT superior for chronic CSCR (higher complete fluid resolution, faster response). Micropulse is an option for mild chronic cases or when PDT is unavailable.

## Lifestyle Recommendations

**Essential for all CSCR patients (acute and chronic)** :

- **Discontinue all corticosteroids** – Topical, inhaled, intranasal, intra-articular, oral. Work with prescribing physicians to find non-steroidal alternatives (e.g., antihistamines for allergies, leukotriene inhibitors for asthma).

- **Stress reduction** – Structured stress management: cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR), regular exercise (aerobic 30 min/day), adequate sleep (7–8 hours).

- **Blood pressure control** – Target <130/80 mmHg. If hypertensive, adhere to medications; consider adding home BP monitoring.

- **Treat obstructive sleep apnea** – If diagnosed, CPAP adherence reduces sympathetic activation.

- **Avoid shift work** – If possible; irregular sleep patterns worsen sympathetic tone.

**What to avoid** :

- Over-the-counter corticosteroid creams (hydrocortisone) for rashes, insect bites
- Decongestant sprays containing steroids (Flonase, Nasonex, Nasacort) – use saline spray or antihistamines instead
- High-stress situations (if avoidable)
- Unnecessary medical steroids (ask physicians: "Do I really need this steroid?")

## Treatment Limitations

- **No cure** – Treatments address fluid but do not reverse established RPE or photoreceptor atrophy. Some patients have persistent metamorphopsia despite anatomical resolution.

- **PDT limitations**:
    - Not available in all centers
    - Expensive (verteporfin cost: $1000–2000 per dose)
    - Requires IV access and laser equipment
    - RPE atrophy risk (reduced-fluence mitigates but does not eliminate)

- **Laser photocoagulation limitations**:
    - Only for extrafoveal leaks (approximately 20–30% of acute CSCR)
    - Scar may enlarge over years, potentially involving fovea
    - Does not prevent recurrences elsewhere

- **Medication limitations (MRAs)** :
    - Mixed trial results; many retina specialists no longer prescribe them for CSCR
    - Requires potassium monitoring; contraindicated in renal failure (eGFR <30)
    - Side effects cause discontinuation in 10–20%

- **Observation limitations** – Even with spontaneous resolution, 20–30% of acute CSCR patients have residual symptoms (metamorphopsia, reduced contrast, color desaturation) despite 20/20 acuity.

- **Recurrence risk** – 30–50% recurrence rate within 3 years, even with successful treatment. Multiple episodes increase risk of permanent vision loss.

## Prognosis

### Acute CSCR (first episode, no prior episodes)

- **Spontaneous resolution**: 70–90% within 3–6 months
- **Visual recovery**: 80–90% return to 20/20–20/30
- **Residual symptoms**: 20–30% have persistent metamorphopsia or reduced contrast sensitivity despite normal acuity
- **Recurrence risk**: 30–50% within 1–3 years (higher if risk factors persist)

### Chronic CSCR (fluid > 6 months)

- **Spontaneous resolution rate**: 30–50% over 12 months (much lower than acute)
- **With PDT treatment**: 70–90% fluid resolution, 60–80% vision improvement (average gain 2–3 lines)
- **Permanent vision loss (20/200 or worse)** : 10–15% over 5–10 years (due to RPE atrophy and photoreceptor loss)

### Factors associated with better prognosis

- Younger age (< 40 years)
- Shorter symptom duration before treatment (< 4 months)
- Normal baseline RPE (no pre-existing atrophy)
- Smaller detachment height (< 300 μm on OCT)
- Intact photoreceptor ellipsoid zone on OCT (no disrupted external limiting membrane)
- Resolution of fluid within 3 months

### Factors associated with worse prognosis

- Chronic (> 6 months) or recurrent episodes
- RPE atrophy or "gravitational tracks" on fundus exam
- Disrupted ellipsoid zone (photoreceptor damage) on OCT
- Older age (> 50 years)
- Ongoing corticosteroid use or uncontrolled hypertension
- Bilateral involvement

**Second eye involvement**: 10–15% develop CSCR in the fellow eye within 5 years. Annual monitoring recommended.

**Key message**: Even with successful treatment, close follow-up and risk factor modification are essential to prevent recurrences and progressive vision loss.

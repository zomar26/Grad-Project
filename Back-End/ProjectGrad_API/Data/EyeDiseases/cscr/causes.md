# Causes and Risk Factors of Central Serous Chorioretinopathy (CSCR)

## Main Causes

The precise cause of CSCR remains incompletely understood, but the central pathogenic mechanism is **pachychoroid**—abnormal thickening and hyperpermeability of the choroid—combined with **focal or diffuse dysfunction of the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE)** .

**Proposed mechanisms**:

1. **Choroidal vascular hyperpermeability** – The choroid becomes congested and leaky, likely due to autonomic nervous system dysregulation (sympathetic overactivity) and/or glucocorticoid effects on choroidal vessels. Indocyanine green angiography (ICGA) typically shows multifocal areas of choroidal vascular hyperpermeability.

2. **RPE pump failure** – The RPE normally transports fluid from the subretinal space into the choroid. When overwhelmed by excess choroidal fluid or when RPE cells are damaged (by corticosteroids, oxidative stress, or mechanical stretch), fluid accumulates beneath the neurosensory retina.

3. **Glucocorticoid pathway activation** – Exogenous or endogenous corticosteroids are strongly implicated. Corticosteroids:
    - Increase choroidal vascular permeability
    - Impair RPE barrier function
    - Delay resolution of subretinal fluid
    - May directly induce RPE cell apoptosis in animal models

4. **Sympathetic nervous system overactivity** – Stress, type A personality, and catecholamine excess may cause choroidal vasoconstriction followed by reactive hyperpermeability.

## Risk Factors

### Strongly Associated (Well-Established)

- **Male sex** – 6-fold higher incidence in males (range 3:1 to 10:1 across studies). The protective effect of estrogen in females is hypothesized but not proven.

- **Type A personality** – Competitive, aggressive, time-urgent, and easily stressed individuals have 3–5 fold higher risk. This is not a clinical psychiatric diagnosis but a behavioral pattern.

- **Systemic corticosteroid use** – Present in 30–50% of CSCR cases. Any route of administration is implicated:
    - **Oral**: Prednisone, methylprednisolone (most common)
    - **Intranasal**: Steroid sprays for allergies (fluticasone, mometasone)
    - **Inhaled**: Asthma/COPD inhalers (risk lower but present)
    - **Topical**: High-potency skin creams (especially with occlusion)
    - **Intra-articular**: Joint injections (e.g., for arthritis)
    - **Epidural**: For back pain
    - **Intravitreal**: Dexamethasone or fluocinolone implants (for uveitis, diabetic edema)

- **Endogenous hypercortisolism** – Cushing's syndrome (pituitary adenoma, adrenal tumor, or long-term high-dose corticosteroid use) causes CSCR in up to 5% of affected individuals.

- **Psychological stress** – Perceived high stress levels preceding symptom onset reported in 50–70% of patients. Stressful life events (job loss, divorce, bereavement, exams, military combat) are common triggers.

- **Hypertension** – Chronic high blood pressure is associated with 2–3 fold increased risk, possibly due to choroidal vascular changes.

- **Pregnancy** – Third trimester (hormonal and hemodynamic changes). Usually resolves postpartum.

### Moderately Associated

- **Obstructive sleep apnea** – Prevalence of CSCR is higher in OSA patients (odds ratio ~2–3). Mechanism: intermittent hypoxia and sympathetic activation may increase choroidal permeability.

- **Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) treatment** – Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) have been implicated in some case-control studies, but causality is debated. The association may be confounded by corticosteroid use (many GERD patients also use steroids).

- **Organ transplantation** – High-dose immunosuppression (including corticosteroids) and systemic inflammation may contribute.

- **Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)** – CSCR can occur, often during disease flares or corticosteroid treatment.

- **Catecholamine-secreting tumors** – Pheochromocytoma (rare cause, but reported).

### Weak or Unconfirmed Associations

- **Alcohol consumption** – Mixed evidence; heavy use may increase risk through liver dysfunction (altered steroid metabolism) or direct vascular effects.
- **Caffeine** – Not consistently associated; no recommendation to avoid.
- **Sildenafil (Viagra) and similar PDE5 inhibitors** – Isolated case reports; no causal link established.
- **Migraine** – Weak association; overlapping sympathetic dysregulation is possible.

## Genetic Factors

CSCR is not a classic Mendelian (single-gene) disorder but does have a **polygenic component** with moderate heritability.

- **Complement factor H (CFH) gene** – Variants in CFH (same gene implicated in age-related macular degeneration) are associated with CSCR, particularly the Y402H polymorphism. CFH regulates the alternative complement pathway; dysregulation may contribute to choroidal inflammation and hyperpermeability.

- **ARMS2/HTRA1** – Some studies show association, but weaker than for AMD.

- **Pachychoroid spectrum genes** – Research is ongoing; genes controlling choroidal vascular development and permeability are candidate loci.

- **Familial clustering** – Rare reports of multiple affected family members, suggesting possible autosomal dominant inheritance with incomplete penetrance in some families.

- **No clinical genetic testing** is currently recommended for CSCR diagnosis or management.

## Environmental Factors

- **Shift work or chronic sleep deprivation** – May dysregulate circadian rhythms and sympathetic tone.

- **High-altitude exposure** – Rare cases reported; hypoxia may increase choroidal permeability.

- **Seasonal variation** – More common in spring and summer in some studies (possibly related to sunlight exposure or seasonal allergies and their treatments).

- **Helicobacter pylori infection** – Weak association; if causal, mechanism unknown.

## Prevention Strategies

Because CSCR is strongly linked to modifiable factors, prevention focuses on risk factor control:

### Primary prevention (preventing first episode in high-risk individuals)

- **Avoid unnecessary corticosteroids** – Use non-steroidal alternatives when possible for allergies (antihistamines, nasal saline), asthma (leukotriene inhibitors, mast cell stabilizers), and inflammatory conditions. If corticosteroids are essential, use lowest effective dose and shortest duration.

- **Stress management** – Regular exercise, mindfulness, meditation, adequate sleep, and counseling for chronic stress or anxiety.

- **Control hypertension** – Maintain BP <130/80 mmHg (per guidelines). Regular monitoring and adherence to antihypertensive medications.

- **Treat obstructive sleep apnea** – CPAP therapy reduces sympathetic activation and may lower CSCR risk (though direct evidence for prevention is lacking).

- **Pregnancy monitoring** – Pregnant women with prior CSCR should inform their ophthalmologist; consider more frequent monitoring in third trimester.

### Secondary prevention (preventing recurrence after resolution)

- **Strictly avoid corticosteroids** – Patients with prior CSCR should avoid all corticosteroid preparations unless absolutely medically necessary. Even short courses (e.g., 5-day prednisone for poison ivy) can trigger recurrence.

- **Identify and stop hidden steroid sources**:
    - Intranasal sprays (some patients forget these are steroids)
    - Inhaled asthma inhalers (discuss alternatives with pulmonologist)
    - Topical skin creams (check labels; hydrocortisone, triamcinolone, etc.)
    - Intra-articular injections (inform all physicians of prior CSCR)

- **Lifestyle modifications** – Stress reduction, regular sleep schedule, avoid shift work if possible.

- **Treat underlying conditions** – Control hypertension, manage OSA with CPAP, screen for Cushing's syndrome if clinical features present (moon facies, buffalo hump, purple striae).

- **Regular monitoring** – Annual dilated eye exams with OCT (more frequently if symptomatic). Patient education on Amsler grid use (monthly testing).

### What does NOT prevent CSCR (no evidence)

- Avoiding caffeine or alcohol (moderate intake likely safe)
- Vitamin or antioxidant supplements
- Dietary modifications (no specific diet proven protective)
- Aspirin or other antiplatelet agents

# Treatment of Posterior Subcapsular Cataract

## Available Treatments

The only definitive treatment for posterior subcapsular cataract (PSC) is **surgical removal of the opacified lens** with implantation of an intraocular lens (IOL). No medications, eye drops, or supplements reverse or halt PSC progression.

Because PSCs cause disabling symptoms early (glare, near vision loss) even with good distance acuity, **surgery is often indicated at an earlier stage** than for nuclear or cortical cataracts (e.g., 20/30–20/40 vision with significant symptoms).

## Medications

**No FDA-approved medications for treating PSC.** The following are **not effective**:

- **Aldose reductase inhibitors** – No benefit for PSC.
- **N-acetylcarnosine eye drops** – No efficacy; not recommended.
- **Antioxidant supplements (AREDS/AREDS2)** – No benefit for PSC.
- **Topical non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)** – Do not reverse established PSC; may slow progression in uveitic PSC by controlling inflammation, but not a treatment for the cataract itself.
- **Corticosteroids** – Contraindicated for treating PSC (cause the condition); use only for underlying uveitis.

**Off-label medications for symptom management**:
- **Tinted lenses** – Yellow, amber, or polarized glasses (not medications) reduce glare and improve contrast.
- **Pharmacologic pupil dilation (rare)** – Dilating drops (e.g., tropicamide 1% or phenylephrine 2.5%) can temporarily dilate the pupil, allowing light to pass around the central opacity, improving vision for short periods. Not practical for long-term use due to photophobia and near blur.

## Surgical Options

**Phacoemulsification with intraocular lens (IOL) implantation** is the standard of care.

### Indications for Early Surgery (PSC-specific)

Due to the disproportionately disabling nature of PSC, surgery is indicated for:

- **Disabling glare** interfering with night driving, outdoor activities, or work
- **Inability to read** despite updated glasses (near vision loss)
- **Contrast sensitivity loss** affecting safety (walking, stairs, curbs)
- **Visual acuity 20/30–20/40** with significant symptoms (earlier than for other cataract types)
- **Patient quality of life** significantly impacted (e.g., can no longer work at computer, read menus, enjoy hobbies)
- **Rapid progression** of PSC (e.g., vision declines from 20/30 to 20/80 within 6–12 months)

**Do NOT delay surgery** for PSC until vision reaches 20/50–20/70, as is often done for nuclear cataracts. Early surgery is both safe and appropriate.

### Procedure Overview

1. **Anesthesia** – Topical (eye drops) or local anesthetic (peribulbar/retrobulbar block). General anesthesia rarely needed.
2. **Corneal incision** – 2–3 mm self-sealing incision.
3. **Capsulorhexis** – Circular opening in the anterior lens capsule.
4. **Hydrodissection** – Fluid injected to separate the lens from the capsule.
5. **Phacoemulsification** – Ultrasonic probe breaks and aspirates the lens (nucleus, cortex, and PSC plaque).
6. **IOL implantation** – Foldable IOL inserted into the capsular bag.
7. **Incision hydration** – No sutures required.

**Special considerations for PSC**:
- The PSC plaque is typically soft and easily aspirated with the cortex, requiring less ultrasound energy than dense nuclear cataracts.
- However, the posterior capsule may be more fragile if the PSC has caused capsular wrinkling or fibrosis.
- Higher risk of posterior capsule rupture if the PSC plaque is densely adherent to the capsule.

### Intraocular Lens (IOL) Options

| IOL Type | Features | Considerations for PSC Patients |
|----------|----------|----------------------------------|
| **Monofocal IOL** | Single focus (distance). Best distance vision; requires glasses for near (reading) or intermediate (computer). | Excellent contrast sensitivity; no glare/halos. Good choice if night driving is essential. |
| **Multifocal IOL** | Multiple rings for distance, intermediate, near. Reduced or eliminated need for glasses. | **May increase postoperative glare/halos** (ironic, given PSC patients already have severe glare). Many PSC patients are poor candidates due to pre-existing glare sensitivity. |
| **Extended depth of focus (EDOF) IOL** | Continuous range from distance to intermediate; some near. Fewer halos than multifocal. | Better intermediate vision (computer). Acceptable for PSC patients who want less dependence on glasses but are sensitive to halos. |
| **Toric IOL** | Corrects pre-existing corneal astigmatism (>0.75–1.0 D). | Reduces need for post-op distance glasses. Often a good choice for PSC patients. |
| **Blue-blocking IOL** | Filters blue light (similar to yellow-tinted glasses). | May reduce glare and improve contrast in bright light; some patients report improved comfort postoperatively. Evidence modest. |

**Shared decision-making**: Discuss glare sensitivity (PSC patients are often very bothered by glare, so multifocal IOLs may be poorly tolerated). Balance desire for spectacle independence against risk of postoperative dysphotopsias (halos, starbursts).

### Postoperative Course

- **Day of surgery**: Eye patched or shielded; vision blurry.
- **Days 1–7**: Rapid vision improvement. Use prescribed antibiotic and anti-inflammatory drops (typically 4 weeks). Avoid rubbing eye, heavy lifting (>20 lbs), swimming, or getting water in eye.
- **Weeks 1–4**: Vision stabilizes. Glasses prescription can be updated after 4 weeks.
- **Complication rates**: Serious complications (endophthalmitis, retinal detachment) occur in <0.5% of healthy eyes.

### Special Consideration: Posterior Capsule Opacification (PCO) Risk

**PCO is significantly more common after PSC surgery** (30–50% at 2–3 years vs. 20–30% for nuclear cataracts). The same residual lens epithelial cells that caused the original PSC can proliferate on the posterior capsule, causing secondary opacification.

- **YAG laser capsulotomy** – Outpatient procedure (5 minutes, painless) to open the opaque posterior capsule. Effective and safe.
- **Timing**: Typically performed 6–24 months post-surgery.
- **Complications**: Rare (<1% risk of retinal detachment, IOL damage, or cystoid macular edema).
- **Prevention**: Some surgeons polish the posterior capsule during surgery or use special IOL designs (square-edged optics) to reduce PCO risk.

## Lifestyle Recommendations

**Before surgery (non-surgical management)** :

- **Tinted lenses for glare** – Yellow, amber, or orange-tinted glasses (e.g., NoIR, EagleEyes) reduce glare and improve contrast. Fit-over sunglasses work over prescription glasses.
- **Polarized sunglasses** – Essential for outdoor activities; reduce glare from reflections (windshields, water, snow).
- **Anti-reflective (AR) coating** – On prescription glasses to reduce internal reflections.
- **Dark mode on all devices** – Use white text on black background to reduce screen glare.
- **Reduce overhead lighting** – Use task lighting (gooseneck lamps) instead of bright overhead fluorescents.
- **Wear sunglasses indoors** – For patients with severe glare, wearing lightly tinted glasses indoors (e.g., 20% tint) can improve comfort.
- **Night driving avoidance** – Most patients with moderate PSC cannot drive safely at night. Stop night driving early.
- **Brighter task lighting for reading** – Paradoxically, PSC patients need **bright** light to read (despite glare) because they need increased illumination to overcome the opacity. Use adjustable gooseneck LED lamps.

**After surgery** :

- **Discontinue tinted glasses** (if no longer needed).
- **Use prescribed eye drops** – Complete full 4-week course.
- **Expect dramatic glare reduction** – Most patients are amazed by the reduction in glare and halos.
- **But be aware of possible PCO** – If vision becomes hazy again after months to years, it is likely PCO, treatable with YAG laser (not a cataract recurrence).

## Treatment Limitations

- **No pharmacologic treatment** – Surgery is the only cure.
- **Higher PCO rate** – 30–50% require YAG laser within 2–3 years. This is an additional procedure (though quick and safe).
- **Surgery does not reverse pre-existing retinal or optic nerve damage** – If concurrent macular degeneration, diabetic retinopathy, glaucoma, or retinitis pigmentosa is present, vision may remain limited post-surgery.
- **IOL choice matters** – Multifocal IOLs may worsen glare in PSC patients; careful patient selection required.
- **Underlying disease progression** – In uveitic or retinitis pigmentosa patients, the underlying disease may continue to progress after cataract surgery, limiting visual gains.

## Prognosis

### Without surgery

- Rapid progression (months to 2–3 years) from mild to advanced.
- Near vision loss early; distance vision eventually declines.
- Disabling glare severely limits night driving, outdoor activities, and work under bright lights.
- Eventually interferes with all activities of daily living.

### With cataract surgery

| Outcome | Percentage (Healthy Eye) | Notes |
|---------|-------------------------|-------|
| Visual acuity 20/40 or better | >95% | With monofocal IOL |
| Visual acuity 20/25 or better | >85% | Assuming no other ocular pathology |
| Glare and halos eliminated | >90% | Dramatic improvement post-surgery |
| Patient satisfaction | >90% | Improved quality of life, reading, driving |
| YAG laser capsulotomy for PCO | 30–50% by 2–3 years | Higher than other cataract types |
| Retinal detachment (post-YAG) | <1% | Rare |
| Cystoid macular edema | 1–3% | Usually temporary |

### Factors affecting prognosis

| Factor | Better Prognosis | Worse Prognosis |
|--------|------------------|-----------------|
| Underlying ocular disease | Healthy eye | Uveitis, retinitis pigmentosa, glaucoma, AMD |
| PSC severity | Mild to moderate | Severe with posterior capsule fibrosis |
| Steroid-induced PSC | After steroid discontinuation | Ongoing steroid use (continued progression) |
| Age | Older (>70) | Younger (<50) – higher PCO and inflammation risk |
| IOL selection | Monofocal or EDOF | Multifocal (in PSC patients) |
| Surgical technique | Uncomplicated | Capsular rupture, vitreous loss |

**Key message**: Posterior subcapsular cataracts are highly treatable with excellent outcomes, but they warrant **earlier surgery** than other cataract types due to disabling symptoms. Postoperative PCO is common but easily treated with YAG laser. Do not delay surgery if your quality of life is affected.

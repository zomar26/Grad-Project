# Posterior Subcapsular Cataract

## Definition

A posterior subcapsular cataract (PSC) is a type of age-related or secondary cataract that forms as an opacity in the **posterior cortex**, directly beneath the posterior lens capsule (the thin membrane enclosing the back of the lens). Unlike nuclear cataracts (central lens core) or cortical cataracts (peripheral spokes), PSCs develop in the axial (central) region of the lens at the visual axis, making them disproportionately disabling for their size.

PSCs are often called "the cataract that blinds more than its size would suggest" because even a small 2–3 mm opacity located directly at the nodal point of the eye can cause severe visual disability—particularly glare, halos, and near vision loss—while distance vision may remain relatively preserved in early stages.

## How the Eye is Affected

**Affected structure**: The posterior subcapsular region—the layer of lens epithelial cells located at the posterior pole of the lens, just anterior to the posterior capsule. These cells normally remain dormant throughout life, but under certain conditions (aging, corticosteroids, inflammation, radiation), they proliferate, migrate, and undergo fibrous metaplasia, forming a plaque-like opacity.

**Mechanism of opacification**:

1. **Trigger**: Corticosteroid exposure (endogenous or exogenous), chronic inflammation (uveitis), radiation, or aging causes abnormal proliferation of residual lens epithelial cells at the posterior pole.

2. **Cell migration and fibrosis**: These cells migrate posteriorly (toward the capsule), transform into myofibroblast-like cells, and deposit extracellular matrix (collagen, fibronectin), forming a dense, granular, or multilamellar plaque.

3. **Location at nodal point**: The opacity forms directly along the visual axis, the path of incoming light. Even a small opacity (1–2 mm) scatters light passing through the central pupil, causing disabling glare, halos, and reduced contrast sensitivity. Under bright light (when the pupil constricts), the opacity is more visually significant (paradoxical photophobia).

4. **Light scatter**: PSCs scatter light forward (toward the retina) and backward (toward the examiner), producing an **after-cataract** appearance on slit-lamp examination.

5. **Near vision loss**: Because the opacity lies at the posterior pole, it blocks the converging light rays used for near focus more severely than parallel light rays for distance, explaining the early loss of reading vision.

**Effect on vision**:

- **Disabling glare and halos**: Worse than any other cataract type. Bright lights (sunlight, oncoming headlights, overhead fluorescents) cause severe scattering and disability.
- **Near vision loss out of proportion to distance vision**: Patients lose reading ability early while still seeing reasonably well at distance.
- **Diminished vision in bright light (paradoxical photophobia)**: Pupil constriction in bright light brings more of the opacity into the visual axis. Patients see better in dim light (pupil dilation allows light past the opacity).
- **Reduced contrast sensitivity**: The central opacity reduces ability to distinguish objects from background, especially in low-contrast situations.
- **Monocular diplopia or polyopia**: Multiple images (ghosting) due to light diffraction through the irregular plaque.

## Types or Stages

Posterior subcapsular cataracts are typically classified by morphology and severity, though a formal staging system (like LOCS III for nuclear cataracts) is less commonly used.

**By morphology**:

- **Granular PSC**: Early stage; fine, granular dust-like opacities just beneath the posterior capsule. Often subtle on examination.
- **Plaque-like PSC**: Dense, discoid, multilamellar white or iridescent plaque (like "crushed eggshells" or "crackled glass"). Most visually significant.
- **Cystic PSC**: Fluid-filled vacuoles or cysts at the posterior pole; less common, often associated with uveitis or retinitis pigmentosa.
- **Mixed PSC + other cataract types**: Frequently coexists with nuclear sclerosis or cortical changes, especially in older patients.

**By location and extent**:

| Grade | Description | Visual Acuity (Typical) | Symptoms |
|-------|-------------|-------------------------|----------|
| **Trace** | Isolated fine granules, <1 mm diameter | 20/20–20/25 | Minimal symptoms; glare possible in very bright light |
| **Mild** | Small plaque, 1–2 mm, involving visual axis | 20/25–20/40 | Glare, halos; difficulty reading small print; night driving problematic |
| **Moderate** | Larger plaque, 2–4 mm, dense white | 20/40–20/80 | Disabling glare; near vision severely impaired; distance vision still reasonable |
| **Severe** | Large plaque (>4 mm) or confluent, may involve entire posterior capsule | 20/80–20/200 | Severe near and distance blur; disabling glare in all light conditions; functional blindness for reading |

**By etiology**:

- **Idiopathic (age-related) PSC**: Most common; presents after age 50–60.
- **Corticosteroid-induced PSC**: Classic association; may occur at younger ages (30–50 years) and progress rapidly.
- **Radiation-induced PSC**: After ocular or head/neck radiation (e.g., for tumors).
- **Uveitic PSC**: Secondary to chronic intraocular inflammation (e.g., juvenile idiopathic arthritis, sarcoidosis, Fuch's heterochromic iridocyclitis).
- **Traumatic PSC**: After blunt or penetrating ocular trauma.
- **PSC in inherited retinal diseases**: Retinitis pigmentosa, gyrate atrophy, Stickler syndrome.

## Key Facts

- **Disproportionate disability**: A 2 mm PSC causes more visual disability than a nuclear cataract covering the entire lens. Location (visual axis) matters more than size.

- **Paradoxical photophobia**: Patients see **better** in dim light (pupil dilates, light passes around the opacity) and **worse** in bright light (pupil constricts, opacity sits directly in light path). Opposite of normal pupil response.

- **Near vision affected first**: Reading difficulty often precedes distance blur by months to years. Patients complain of needing "new glasses" but acuity may be 20/20–20/30.

- **Corticosteroid link**: Systemic, topical (skin), inhaled, intranasal, or intra-articular steroids cause PSC. The risk is **dose- and duration-dependent**. Even long-term use of over-the-counter hydrocortisone creams can contribute.

- **Rapid progression**: PSCs often progress faster than nuclear cataracts (months to 2–3 years vs. 5–10 years). Corticosteroid-induced PSCs may become visually significant within 6–18 months.

- **High prevalence in retinal diseases**: 30–50% of patients with retinitis pigmentosa develop PSC by age 40–50.

- **Glare > blur**: Glare from headlights, sunlight, and indoor lighting is often more disabling than the blur itself.

- **Surgery is curative**: Phacoemulsification with IOL implantation completely removes the PSC. Success rate >95%.

- **Postoperative complications**: Higher risk of posterior capsule opacification (PCO) after PSC surgery due to residual lens epithelial cell proliferation (same cells that caused the original PSC). YAG laser capsulotomy rate ~30–50% at 2–3 years.

- **No medical treatment**: No eye drops or supplements reverse or halt PSC progression. Early cataract surgery is often indicated due to disabling symptoms, even with 20/30–20/40 acuity.

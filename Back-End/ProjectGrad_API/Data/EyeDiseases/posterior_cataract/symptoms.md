# Symptoms of Posterior Subcapsular Cataract

## Common Symptoms

Posterior subcapsular cataracts produce a distinct symptom profile that differs from nuclear and cortical cataracts. The hallmark is **glare and near vision loss out of proportion to distance acuity**.

- **Disabling glare (photophobia)** – The most prominent and characteristic symptom. Patients report being "blinded" or "dazzled" by:
    - Oncoming headlights at night (making night driving impossible)
    - Sunlight reflecting off windshields, water, or light-colored pavement
    - Overhead fluorescent or LED lights in stores, offices, or homes
    - Bright computer screens or smartphones
    - Reading under bright task lighting

- **Halos around lights** – Colored or white rings surrounding car headlights, streetlights, decorative lights, or even the moon. More bothersome at night.

- **Near vision loss** – Patients complain that they "can't read anymore" or that their reading glasses "stopped working." Small print (menus, pill bottles, smartphone text) becomes illegible. This occurs even when distance vision is 20/20–20/30.

- **Better vision in dim light** – The **paradoxical photophobia** phenomenon. Patients see better in dimly lit restaurants, movie theaters, or at dusk than in bright sunlight. Reason: In dim light, the pupil dilates, allowing light to pass around the central opacity. Many patients instinctively wear sunglasses indoors or prefer dim lighting.

- **Blurred distance vision (late symptom)** – Distance vision is relatively preserved early but declines as the plaque enlarges. Patients may not notice distance blur until the cataract is moderately advanced.

- **Reduced contrast sensitivity** – Trouble distinguishing objects from background, particularly in low-contrast situations:
    - Seeing white steps against a white wall
    - Reading gray text on a white page
    - Seeing facial expressions in shadow
    - Distinguishing a curb from the road in twilight

- **Monocular diplopia or polyopia** – Seeing double (or multiple) images with one eye. The ghost images may be faint and overlapping. Caused by light diffraction through the irregular plaque.

- **Frequent eyeglass prescription changes** – Less common than in nuclear cataracts (which cause myopic shift), but patients may report that their glasses "don't help" with glare or near vision.

- **Difficulty with bright digital screens** – Glare from phones, tablets, and computers. Patients often reduce screen brightness or use dark mode.

## Advanced Symptoms

As the PSC plaque enlarges (3–5 mm or more) or becomes denser, additional symptoms develop:

- **Severe near vision loss** – Inability to read any print, including large-print books or smartphone text set to the largest font. Patients rely entirely on audiobooks or text-to-speech.

- **Moderate to severe distance blur** – Visual acuity declines to 20/50–20/200. Road signs, faces across a room, and television become difficult or impossible to see.

- **Constant disabling glare** – Even in moderately bright indoor lighting (offices, grocery stores), the patient experiences disabling glare. They may wear dark glasses indoors.

- **Dense central scotoma (rare)** – A dark or hazy spot in the central vision from a very dense plaque. Differentiates PSC from nuclear cataract (which causes uniform blur, not a discrete scotoma).

- **Complete loss of night vision** – Oncoming headlights create complete "whiteout" or "star burst" effects. Most patients stop night driving entirely.

- **Difficulty recognizing faces** – When looking directly at a person's face, the PSC blocks central details (eyes, nose, mouth). Patients may recognize people by peripheral vision (hairline, jaw, clothing) or voice.

- **Subjective color changes** – Less prominent than nuclear cataracts (which cause yellow-brown browning). PSCs typically cause a mild washed-out appearance rather than yellowing.

- **Intolerance to bright days** – Patients may refuse to go outside on sunny days without very dark sunglasses. Some become functionally "day-blind."

- **Risk of acute angle-closure glaucoma (rare)** – If the cataract becomes intumescent (swollen), it can push the iris forward, narrowing the anterior chamber angle. Symptoms: sudden eye pain, headache, nausea, vomiting, halos, blurred vision—an emergency.

## When to Seek Medical Attention

**Routine (schedule eye exam within weeks to months)** :

- New difficulty reading small print or needing brighter light to read, especially if distance vision still seems good
- Disabling glare from headlights or sunlight that makes driving uncomfortable or unsafe
- Halos around lights that interfere with night driving
- Frequent need to update reading glasses prescription (every 6–12 months)
- Paradoxical photophobia (seeing better in dim light than bright light)
- Annual exam for patients with known PSC or risk factors (corticosteroid use, retinitis pigmentosa)

**Urgent (same day or within 24–48 hours)** – PSCs themselves do NOT require urgent care, but these symptoms suggest complications:

- **Sudden, painless vision loss** over hours or days – Not typical of PSC (which progresses over months). Suggests retinal detachment, vitreous hemorrhage, or macular degeneration.
- **New flashes of light or sudden increase in floaters** – Possible posterior vitreous detachment or retinal tear.
- **A curtain or shadow across vision** – Possible retinal detachment.

**Emergency (go to emergency department immediately)** :

- **Sudden eye pain with nausea/vomiting, headache, and blurred vision** – Possible acute angle-closure glaucoma from an intumescent (swollen) cataract. Requires immediate treatment.
- **Eye pain with redness and vision loss** – Possible phacolytic glaucoma, uveitis, or endophthalmitis.

**Special note for corticosteroid users**: If you are on long-term corticosteroids (oral, inhaled, intranasal, or topical) and develop any of the above symptoms, inform your ophthalmologist. Corticosteroid-induced PSCs can progress rapidly (over months) and may require early surgery.

## Symptoms That May Affect Daily Activities

| Activity | Symptom Impact |
|----------|----------------|
| Reading (books, phones, tablets) | Near vision loss is often the first symptom. Patients cannot read small print or need very bright light and magnification. Smartphone text becomes illegible. |
| Driving (night) | Disabling glare from oncoming headlights; halos around traffic lights; unsafe at even mild to moderate stages. Most patients stop night driving early. |
| Driving (daytime) | Glare from sun reflecting off windshields, wet roads, or light-colored pavement. Distance vision may still be 20/30–20/40, but glare makes driving hazardous. |
| Computer work | Glare from bright screen; halos around text or icons; reduced contrast sensitivity. Patients reduce brightness, use dark mode, or wear tinted glasses. |
| Watching television | Subtitles become unreadable; dark scenes lose detail; glare from bright commercials or on-screen flashes. |
| Working under fluorescent lights | Disabling glare from overhead lights in offices, stores, hospitals, and factories. Patients may need to wear dark glasses or request task lighting instead. |
| Outdoor activities (sports, gardening, walking) | Glare from sunlight severely limits outdoor activities in bright conditions. Patients may wear very dark sunglasses or avoid daytime outings. |
| Recognizing faces | In moderate to advanced stages, the central opacity blocks facial details (eyes, nose, mouth) when looking directly at someone. |
| Cooking | Difficulty reading small-print measurements; glare from stainless steel appliances or overhead lights; cannot see dial settings clearly. |
| Taking medications | Inability to read small-print prescription labels (even with reading glasses). |
| Nighttime navigation | Severe glare from streetlights and car headlights; reduced contrast sensitivity makes curbs and steps hazardous. |
| Occupational tasks | Surgeons, pilots, drivers, machinists, and anyone requiring good near vision or glare-free vision may be unable to work. Office workers under fluorescent lights struggle significantly. |
| Reading menus in restaurants | Often the first complaint—patients cannot read the menu in dimly lit restaurants but see better once food arrives (distance). |

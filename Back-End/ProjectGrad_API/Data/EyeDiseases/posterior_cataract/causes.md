# Causes and Risk Factors of Posterior Subcapsular Cataract

## Main Causes

The primary cause of posterior subcapsular cataract (PSC) is **abnormal proliferation and fibrosis of residual lens epithelial cells** at the posterior pole of the lens, triggered by various insults (corticosteroids, inflammation, radiation, aging). Unlike nuclear cataracts (oxidative protein damage), PSCs result from cellular dysregulation.

**Key pathophysiologic mechanisms**:

1. **Corticosteroid-induced**: Glucocorticoids bind to receptors on lens epithelial cells, upregulating genes involved in proliferation, migration, and extracellular matrix production (collagen, fibronectin, proteoglycans). This transforms dormant epithelial cells into myofibroblasts that deposit subcapsular plaques.

2. **Inflammatory (uveitic)**: Chronic intraocular inflammation releases cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α, TGF-β), which similarly activate lens epithelial cells. TGF-β is a potent inducer of epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT), leading to fibrous metaplasia.

3. **Radiation-induced**: Ionizing radiation (X-rays, gamma rays) damages lens epithelial cell DNA, leading to aberrant repair, apoptosis, or dysplastic proliferation. The posterior pole is particularly vulnerable due to the location of germinative lens epithelial cells.

4. **Age-related (idiopathic)**: With aging, the posterior lens capsule becomes more permeable to inflammatory and growth factors from the vitreous, and residual epithelial cells may spontaneously undergo EMT, though at a slower rate than with triggers.

## Risk Factors

### Strongly Associated (Well-Established)

- **Corticosteroid use (systemic, topical, inhaled, intranasal, intra-articular)** – The strongest modifiable risk factor. Present in 30–50% of non-idiopathic PSC cases. Risk factors:
    - **Dose**: Higher daily dose → higher risk (e.g., prednisone ≥10 mg/day).
    - **Duration**: >1 year of use significantly increases risk; >5 years → 50–80% incidence.
    - **Route**: Systemic (oral, IV) > intra-articular > inhaled > intranasal > topical skin (with occlusion).
    - **Specific agents**: Prednisone, methylprednisolone, dexamethasone (high potency); hydrocortisone (low potency but chronic use adds risk).
- **Even over-the-counter hydrocortisone creams** (e.g., for eczema, poison ivy) with chronic use and occlusion (plastic wrap) can cause PSC.
- **Inhaled corticosteroids for asthma/COPD** (fluticasone, budesonide, beclomethasone) – Risk lower than systemic but significant with high-dose, long-term use (e.g., >1000 mcg/day fluticasone for >3 years).
- **Intranasal corticosteroids** (fluticasone, mometasone, triamcinolone) – Low but detectable risk, especially with concomitant inhaled or systemic steroids.

- **Chronic uveitis (intraocular inflammation)** – Any cause of chronic uveitis can produce PSC, including:
    - **Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA)-associated uveitis** – Very high risk; up to 50% develop PSC within 5–10 years.
    - **Sarcoidosis** – 20–30% develop cataract (PSC common).
    - **Fuch's heterochromic iridocyclitis** – Characteristic PSC in 50–70% of affected eyes.
    - **Behçet's disease, Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada (VKH) disease, ankylosing spondylitis, psoriatic arthritis**.
    - **Herpetic uveitis** (HSV, VZV, CMV).

- **Radiation exposure** – History of ocular or periocular radiation for:
    - **Intraocular tumors** (retinoblastoma, choroidal melanoma) – near 100% incidence if lens included in field.
    - **Orbital or head/neck tumors** (lymphoma, nasopharyngeal carcinoma).
    - **Total body irradiation** (bone marrow transplant).
    - **Occupational radiation** (radiologists, nuclear workers) with inadequate eye protection (historical risk; modern standards reduce it).
- **Latency**: Typically 6 months to 5 years after radiation.

- **Retinitis pigmentosa (RP) and inherited retinal degenerations** – 30–50% of RP patients develop PSC, typically by age 40–50. Mechanism: chronic vitreous inflammation and TGF-β release from degenerating retina.

- **Trauma (blunt or penetrating)** – Contusion injury to the lens causes focal damage to lens epithelial cells, triggering proliferation and PSC formation months to years later.

### Moderately Associated

- **Diabetes mellitus** – Increases PSC risk 1.5–2 fold, particularly in poorly controlled diabetes. Weaker association than for nuclear cataracts.

- **Atopic dermatitis (eczema)** – Risk from combination of chronic topical corticosteroid use and systemic inflammation (elevated IgE, eosinophils). **Atopic cataracts** are classically shield-like PSCs (anterior or posterior).

- **Myotonic dystrophy** – Characteristic Christmas-tree cataracts (multicolored iridescent opacities) with PSC component.

- **High myopia** – Weak association; PSC may develop earlier than in emmetropes.

- **Alcohol use disorder** – Possible increased risk, but confounded by nutritional deficiencies and liver disease (altered steroid metabolism).

### Weak or Unconfirmed Associations

- **Smoking** – Strongly associated with nuclear cataracts, but weak or no association with PSC.
- **UV-B light exposure** – Major risk for cortical and nuclear cataracts, but minimal role in PSC.
- **Statin use** – No consistent association.

## Genetic Factors

- **Familial PSC** – Rare autosomal dominant forms linked to mutations in:
    - **CRYGD** (gamma-D crystallin) – Causes early-onset (childhood to young adult) PSC.
    - **CRYBB2** (beta-B2 crystallin) – Associated with PSC and nuclear cataracts.
    - **GJA8** (gap junction protein alpha-8, connexin 50) – Also causes zonular pulverulent cataracts.
    - **PITX3** (transcription factor) – Posterior polar cataracts (PSC variant).

- **Retinitis pigmentosa genes** – Numerous genes (RHO, RPGR, USH2A, etc.) cause RP, leading to secondary PSC via inflammation.

- **Genetic susceptibility to corticosteroid-induced PSC** – Variants in glucocorticoid receptor (NR3C1) and drug transporter (ABCB1) genes may explain individual variability in steroid response.

**Clinical genetic testing** – Not recommended for sporadic PSC. Only indicated for early-onset (<40 years) or familial cases with strong inheritance pattern.

## Environmental Factors

- **Ionizing radiation** – Occupational (historical: radiologists, nuclear workers) and therapeutic (radiation for cancer). Protective leaded glasses reduce risk.

- **UV light** – Not a major cause of PSC (unlike cortical and nuclear cataracts).

- **High altitude** – Increased UV exposure, but PSC not clearly linked.

## Prevention Strategies

### Primary prevention (preventing onset)

- **Avoid unnecessary corticosteroids** – Use non-steroidal alternatives when possible:
    - For allergies: antihistamines (oral or topical), nasal saline, cromolyn sodium (mast cell stabilizer).
    - For asthma: leukotriene inhibitors (montelukast), long-acting beta-agonists, mast cell stabilizers.
    - For eczema: topical calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus), emollients, phototherapy.
    - For inflammatory arthritis: disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs: methotrexate, TNF inhibitors).

- **If corticosteroids are essential**:
    - Use lowest effective dose and shortest duration.
    - Prefer non-systemic routes (topical, inhaled, intranasal) over systemic—but these still carry risk.
    - Consider steroid-sparing agents (e.g., in uveitis: methotrexate, mycophenolate, adalimumab).

- **Aggressive control of uveitis** – Treat underlying inflammatory disease to prevent chronic intraocular inflammation. Regular ophthalmology follow-up (every 3–12 months) for early detection.

- **Radiation shielding** – For patients requiring ocular or periocular radiation: use leaded eye shields (when possible) or optimize treatment planning to minimize lens dose.

- **Manage diabetes** – Maintain HbA1c <7% to reduce risk of all cataract types.

### Secondary prevention (slowing progression)

- **Discontinue corticosteroids** – If PSC is detected and steroid use is non-essential, stopping steroids may slow or halt progression, though existing opacity does not reverse.

- **Control inflammation** – In uveitic PSC, aggressive immunosuppression (steroid-sparing agents) can slow cataract progression and reduce need for early surgery.

- **Treat underlying disease** – Control diabetes, manage atopic dermatitis, treat myotonic dystrophy symptoms (no proven effect on cataract progression).

- **Optimize lighting and glasses** – Tinted lenses (yellow, amber, or polarized) for glare reduction; updated glasses prescription for residual refractive error.

- **Early surgery** – Given rapid progression and disabling symptoms, PSC often warrants surgery earlier than other cataract types (even with 20/30–20/40 acuity).

**What does NOT prevent PSC**:
- Antioxidant supplements (vitamins C/E, lutein/zeaxanthin) – No benefit.
- UV-protective sunglasses – Not proven effective for PSC (unlike cortical/nuclear cataracts).
- Dietary modifications – No specific diet prevents PSC.

**Key message**: Posterior subcapsular cataracts are often preventable by avoiding unnecessary corticosteroids and aggressively treating intraocular inflammation. If you require long-term steroids, have regular dilated eye exams (every 6–12 months) for early detection.

# Stargardt Disease

## Definition

Stargardt disease (also known as Stargardt macular dystrophy or juvenile macular degeneration) is the most common inherited form of macular degeneration in children and young adults. It is characterized by progressive degeneration of the macula (the central part of the retina responsible for sharp, detailed vision), typically leading to significant central vision loss while peripheral vision is relatively preserved.

First described by German ophthalmologist Karl Stargardt in 1909, the disease is usually caused by mutations in the **ABCA4 gene**, leading to accumulation of toxic lipofuscin (a waste product) in the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE), which ultimately causes photoreceptor cell death. Stargardt disease is a form of macular dystrophy and is sometimes referred to as **fundus flavimaculatus** (a broader term that may include later-onset or milder phenotypes).

## How the Eye is Affected

**Primary structures involved**: Retinal pigment epithelium (RPE), photoreceptors (particularly cones in the macula), and to a lesser extent, the choroid.

**Mechanism of degeneration**:

1. **Genetic defect**: Mutations in the **ABCA4 gene** (located on chromosome 1p22) lead to dysfunction of the ABCA4 protein, a transporter protein located on the outer segments of photoreceptors.

2. **Lipofuscin accumulation**: Normally, ABCA4 helps clear **all-trans-retinal** (a byproduct of the visual cycle) from photoreceptor outer segments. In Stargardt disease, dysfunctional ABCA4 causes accumulation of all-trans-retinal, which forms toxic **lipofuscin** (bisretinoid compounds, including **A2E** and its precursors) within RPE cells.

3. **RPE toxicity**: Accumulated lipofuscin is autofluorescent (visible on fundus autofluorescence imaging) and toxic to RPE cells, causing their gradual death.

4. **Photoreceptor degeneration**: RPE loss leads to secondary death of overlying photoreceptors, particularly cones in the macula (responsible for central vision and color vision). Rods may be affected later, contributing to night blindness in some patients.

5. **Yellow-white flecks**: Characteristic **pisciform (fish-shaped) flecks** appear at the level of the RPE, representing deposits of lipofuscin. These flecks are most numerous in the macula and posterior pole but can extend to the mid-periphery.

6. **"Beaten-bronze" macular atrophy**: The end-stage macula appears atrophic, with a metallic, beaten-bronze appearance due to RPE loss and choroidal vessel visibility.

**Distinctive features on examination**:

- **Yellow-white flecks** (pisciform) in the macula and posterior pole (fundus flavimaculatus).
- **Macular atrophy** with a "beaten-bronze" appearance (late stage).
- **Dark choroid sign** on fluorescein angiography (blocked fluorescence due to lipofuscin).
- **Increased autofluorescence** on fundus autofluorescence (FAF) imaging in areas of lipofuscin accumulation, followed by decreased autofluorescence in areas of RPE atrophy.

## Types or Stages

### By Genetic Form

| Type | Gene | Inheritance | Phenotype | Proportion |
|------|------|-------------|-----------|------------|
| **STGD1** | ABCA4 | Autosomal recessive | Classic Stargardt (most common) | 95–98% |
| **STGD2** | ELOVL4 | Autosomal dominant | Rare, later onset | <1% |
| **STGD3** | PROM1 | Autosomal dominant | Rare | <1% |
| **STGD4** | – | Autosomal dominant | Very rare | <1% |

**Note**: The majority of this knowledge base refers to **STGD1 (ABCA4-related Stargardt disease)** , which accounts for >95% of cases.

### By Phenotype (Clinical Presentation)

- **Classic Stargardt disease (juvenile onset)** : Onset before age 20; rapid central vision loss; characteristic flecks and macular atrophy.
- **Late-onset Stargardt disease (fundus flavimaculatus)** : Onset after age 30–40; slower progression; flecks extend to mid-periphery; macular atrophy develops later.
- **Attenuated phenotype** : Mild flecks, slow progression, good central vision preserved into middle age.

### By Disease Severity (Functional Staging)

| Stage | Visual Acuity | Fundus Findings | FAF Findings |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|--------------|
| **Early** | 20/20–20/40 | Few flecks, normal macula | Normal or mild hyperautofluorescence |
| **Moderate** | 20/40–20/100 | Multiple flecks, early macular atrophy | Hyperautofluorescent flecks; early hypoautofluorescent atrophy |
| **Advanced** | 20/100–20/400 | Confluent macular atrophy ("beaten-bronze"), flecks fading | Large area of hypoautofluorescence (atrophy) |
| **End-stage** | 20/400 to CF | Extensive atrophy, pigmentation, choroidal sclerosis | Complete hypoautofluorescence |

**Note**: Peripheral vision is typically preserved until late stages, even when central vision is severely reduced.

### By Age of Onset

- **Early-onset** : <20 years – more rapid progression, early central vision loss.
- **Intermediate-onset** : 20–40 years – variable progression.
- **Late-onset** : >40 years – slower progression, resembles age-related macular degeneration (but distinct genotype).

## Key Facts

- **Prevalence**: 1 in 8,000 to 10,000 people worldwide (approximately 30,000 affected in the US). It is the most common inherited macular dystrophy in young people.

- **Inheritance**: Autosomal recessive in >95% (STGD1). Both parents are typically unaffected carriers. Autosomal dominant forms (ELOVL4, PROM1) are very rare.

- **Age of onset**: Typically childhood to early adulthood (6–20 years), but late-onset forms (30–60 years) also occur. The earlier the onset, the faster the progression.

- **Central vision loss**: The hallmark symptom. Most patients lose central vision to 20/200 or worse by the third to fifth decade of life. Peripheral vision is preserved, so patients rarely become totally blind.

- **Color vision**: Red-green color defects are common; blue-yellow discrimination may also be affected.

- **Night blindness**: Not a prominent early symptom (unlike retinitis pigmentosa), but may occur in late stages or in some genotypes.

- **Fluorescein angiography (FA)**: Shows a characteristic **"dark choroid" sign** (blocked choroidal fluorescence due to lipofuscin) in 70–80% of patients – nearly pathognomonic.

- **Fundus autofluorescence (FAF)** : Essential for monitoring disease progression. Hyperautofluorescence = lipofuscin accumulation (active disease); hypoautofluorescence = RPE atrophy (irreversible).

- **Genetic testing**: Recommended to confirm diagnosis and guide family counseling. ABCA4 sequencing detects mutations in 70–80% of clinically diagnosed Stargardt patients (remaining may have deep intronic mutations or other genes).

- **No FDA-approved treatments** as of 2026, but several clinical trials are ongoing (gene therapy, pharmacological chaperones, visual cycle modulators, stem cells). The most advanced is **tinlarebant (ALK-001)** , a deuterated vitamin A derivative (Phase 3 completed, results pending).

- **Avoid high-dose vitamin A supplementation**: Vitamin A is a precursor to the toxic all-trans-retinal; supplementation may accelerate disease progression. Patients should avoid vitamin A supplements unless medically necessary (pregnancy, deficiency). Low vitamin A diets are not proven beneficial; normal dietary intake is safe.

- **Sunlight protection**: Some evidence suggests blue light exposure may accelerate lipofuscin formation; wearing UV/blue-blocking sunglasses and hats is recommended.

- **Low vision rehabilitation** is the mainstay of management (magnifiers, screen readers, mobility training).

# Symptoms of Stargardt Disease

## Common Symptoms

The onset and progression of symptoms vary significantly based on age of onset and specific genetic mutations. Most patients present in childhood or early adolescence, though late-onset forms may not be diagnosed until the 30s or 40s.

### Early Symptoms (Childhood – Teens)

- **Gradual, painless central vision loss** – The earliest and most consistent symptom. Initially, vision may be 20/25–20/40, with slow worsening over years. Patients report:
    - Difficulty reading small print (e.g., textbooks, smartphone texts)
    - Trouble recognizing faces at a distance (e.g., across a classroom)
    - Needing brighter light to read
    - "Blurry spot" in the center of vision (scotoma)
- **Often noticed at school** – Child may complain of not seeing the blackboard clearly, despite sitting in the front row. Reading speed slows, and they may lose place frequently.

- **Metamorphopsia (visual distortion)** – Less common than in macular pucker but can occur. Straight lines (text lines, door frames) may appear wavy, bent, or crooked.

- **Reduced color vision (dyschromatopsia)** – Subtle early on, often red-green defects. Parents may notice the child struggles with color-coded tasks (e.g., sorting colored candies, matching clothes). Progresses to more severe color deficits.

- **Central or paracentral scotoma** – A dark, gray, or hazy spot in the center or slightly off-center of vision. The patient may not notice initially but will "wiggle" text to read around it (eccentric viewing).

- **Slow dark adaptation** – Difficulty transitioning from bright to dim lighting (e.g., walking indoors on a sunny day, adjusting to a movie theater). Less severe than in retinitis pigmentosa.

- **Glare and light sensitivity (photophobia)** – Mild to moderate; worse in bright sunlight or under fluorescent lights.

- **Normal peripheral vision** – A key distinguishing feature from retinitis pigmentosa. Patients do not bump into things or have night blindness (in early stages).

### Moderate Symptoms (Adolescence – Young Adulthood)

- **Progressive central vision decline** – Vision worsens to 20/50–20/200. Reading becomes very difficult without magnification. Recognizing faces is limited to very close distances (1–2 feet).

- **Enlargement of central scotoma** – The blind spot expands, covering more of the central visual field. Patients learn to use eccentric viewing (looking slightly to the side of the object).

- **Reading difficulty** – Even with large print, reading is slow and fatiguing. Audiobooks and screen readers become necessary.

- **Color vision loss becomes noticeable** – Patients describe colors as "washed out" or "faded." Reds and greens are most affected; blues may also be dull.

- **Worsening photophobia** – Bright sunlight may be disabling. Patients wear tinted glasses or wide-brimmed hats outdoors.

- **Possible night blindness (late moderate stage)** – Some patients notice difficulty seeing in dim light, though not as severe as in RP. Occurs when rods are affected in the mid-periphery.

- **Yellow-white flecks visible on eye exam** – Flecks in the macula and posterior pole may be visible to the examiner, but patients are typically unaware of them.

### Advanced Symptoms (Adulthood, Age 30–60)

- **Severe central vision loss (legal blindness)** – Visual acuity 20/200 to counting fingers or worse in both eyes. Central scotoma is dense, covering the fixation point.

- **Reading impossible** – Cannot read any print, even with maximum magnification. Relies entirely on audiobooks, text-to-speech, and screen readers.

- **Cannot recognize faces** – Even at close range, faces are a blur or missing the central features (eyes, nose, mouth). Recognizes people by voice, clothing, or gait.

- **Color vision severely impaired** – Colors appear gray or monochromatic; may only distinguish bright colors (red vs. green difficult).

- **Preserved peripheral vision** – Patients can navigate, walk, and perform daily activities (cooking, cleaning) using side vision. They rarely become totally blind (no light perception).

- **Possible nyctalopia (night blindness)** – More common in late-stage or certain genotypes; affects outdoor navigation at dusk.

- **Metamorphopsia may resolve** – As the macula becomes completely atrophic, distortion may decrease because the retina is not being "pulled" anymore.

## When to Seek Medical Attention

**Routine (schedule eye exam within weeks to months)** :

- **Child or young adult with progressive central vision loss** – Difficulty reading, seeing the blackboard, or recognizing faces.
- **Family history of Stargardt disease or inherited macular dystrophy** – Even if asymptomatic, baseline examination and genetic counseling recommended.
- **New difficulty with color vision** – Especially red-green confusion.
- **Annual monitoring** – For patients with confirmed Stargardt disease (every 6–12 months for OCT, FAF, visual field testing).
- **New central scotoma or worsening of existing scotoma** – Document progression.

**Urgent (same day or within 24–48 hours)** – Stargardt disease itself does not require urgent care, but these symptoms are not typical and warrant evaluation:

- **Sudden worsening of central vision** over days (not months or years) – Possible choroidal neovascularization (CNV) – rare but reported in Stargardt.
- **New metamorphopsia (wavy distortion)** – Could indicate CNV or cystoid macular edema (rare).
- **Sudden increase in floaters or flashes** – Possible retinal tear or detachment (uncommon but possible).
- **A dark curtain or shadow** across vision – Possible retinal detachment.

**Emergency (go to emergency department immediately)** :

- Sudden, painless vision loss with a "curtain" – Retinal detachment.
- Eye pain with redness and vision loss (unusual for Stargardt) – Uveitis, acute glaucoma, or infection.

## Symptoms That May Affect Daily Activities

| Activity | Symptom Impact |
|----------|----------------|
| Reading (books, phones, computers) | Progressive difficulty starting in childhood; by young adulthood, large print and magnification needed; later, audiobooks and screen readers required |
| Recognizing faces | Central scotoma covers eyes/nose/mouth; may recognize only by voice or clothing; difficulty in social situations |
| Driving | Central vision loss (20/50–20/200) fails legal acuity requirements in most jurisdictions; driving usually impossible by young adulthood |
| Watching television | Subtitles unreadable; fine details (facial expressions, sports scores) missed; may prefer audio description services |
| Using smartphones/tablets | Standard font too small; need magnification, large text, or screen reader (VoiceOver/TalkBack) |
| Walking on uneven ground | Peripheral vision preserved → mobility usually intact unless late-stage atrophy or night blindness |
| Nighttime activities | Some patients develop night blindness (late stage); using dimly lit restaurants, walking at dusk may be difficult |
| Cooking | May need magnification to read measurements; central scotoma could cause spills if not using eccentric viewing |
| Taking medications | Difficulty reading small-print labels; request large-print labels from pharmacy |
| School (children/teens) | Difficulty seeing blackboard, reading textbooks, completing worksheets; need preferential seating, large-print materials, assistive technology |
| Occupational tasks | Driving-based jobs impossible; surgeons, pilots, artists, graphic designers affected when central vision declines; many office jobs feasible with screen readers |
| Sports | Difficult to track small balls (tennis, baseball), see golf ball against grass; but many patients play adapted sports (goalball, blind soccer) |
| Color-dependent tasks | Matching clothes, identifying ripe fruit, interpreting color-coded charts (medical labs, graphs) – difficult or impossible |
| Social situations | Anxiety about not recognizing people (appears rude), bumping into things; may avoid large gatherings |

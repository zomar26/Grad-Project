# Treatment of Stargardt Disease

## Available Treatments

**There is no cure for Stargardt disease**, and no FDA-approved medical therapy that halts or reverses progression. Treatment focuses on:

1. **Slowing progression (investigational)** – Lifestyle modifications (avoid vitamin A supplements, blue-light protection), clinical trial participation.
2. **Managing symptoms** – Low vision rehabilitation, assistive technology.
3. **Treating rare complications** – Choroidal neovascularization (CNV) with anti-VEGF.
4. **Addressing refractive error** – Glasses or contact lenses for residual vision.

## Medications

### No FDA-approved medications for Stargardt disease.

**Investigational drugs (clinical trials only)** – Do not seek outside of trials:

- **Tinlarebant (ALK-001)** – Deuterated vitamin A derivative. Reduces bisretinoid formation by slowing the rate of vitamin A dimerization. Phase 3 (TEASE study) completed 2024; results pending regulatory submission.
- **Fenretinide** – Oral retinoid antagonist that reduces serum vitamin A and its delivery to the retina. Phase 2 trials did not meet primary endpoint; not further developed.
- **Emixustat** – Visual cycle modulator (RPE65 inhibitor). Phase 2/3 trials showed no significant benefit; development discontinued.
- **Metformin** – May reduce lipofuscin accumulation (preclinical). Clinical trial completed (no benefit on macular atrophy progression). Not recommended.

**Off-label treatments (limited evidence)** :

- **Anti-VEGF injections (ranibizumab, aflibercept, bevacizumab)** – For **choroidal neovascularization (CNV)** , a rare complication of Stargardt (2–5% of patients). CNV causes sudden worsening of vision, metamorphopsia, hemorrhage. Anti-VEGF can stabilize or improve vision in these cases (off-label). **Do not use anti-VEGF for typical Stargardt without CNV** (no benefit).

- **Intravitreal corticosteroids** – Not effective; avoid due to cataract and glaucoma risk.

- **Acetazolamide or dorzolamide** – For cystoid macular edema (very rare in Stargardt; if present, may trial topical dorzolamide).

### Medications to AVOID

- **High-dose vitamin A supplements** – May accelerate bisretinoid formation. Avoid multivitamins with >100% RDA of vitamin A (700–900 mcg RAE). Also avoid cod liver oil, retinyl palmitate.
- **Isotretinoin (Accutane)** – A vitamin A derivative; potentially accelerates disease (theoretical risk). Use alternative acne medications (topical retinoids likely safe due to low systemic absorption, but caution).
- **Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil)** – Retinal toxicity; relatively contraindicated in inherited retinal dystrophies (may cause additive damage).
- **Tamoxifen, thioridazine, deferoxamine** – Known retinal toxicity; avoid.

## Surgical Options

### No surgery for Stargardt disease itself.

**Cataract surgery** – Posterior subcapsular cataracts may develop earlier than in the general population (but less common than in retinitis pigmentosa).

- **Indication**: Visually significant cataract (lens opacity reduces vision).
- **Benefits**: Can improve vision if cataract is contributing to vision loss beyond the macular atrophy.
- **Risks** (higher than in healthy eyes):
    - Postoperative cystoid macular edema (CME) – treatable.
    - Progression of underlying macular atrophy – unclear risk.
    - Retinal detachment (rare).
- **Technique**: Standard phacoemulsification with IOL. Consider use of NSAIDs pre- and post-operatively to reduce CME risk.

**Vitrectomy** – Not indicated for Stargardt disease unless complications (epiretinal membrane, macular hole – rare).

**Stem cell or gene therapy** – Investigational only (see Clinical Trials). Do not pay for unregulated "stem cell clinics" (dangerous, no proven benefit).

## Lifestyle Recommendations

### Essential (Strongly Recommended)

- **Avoid high-dose vitamin A supplements** – Check all supplements (multivitamins, cod liver oil, specific vitamin A). RDA for adults: 700–900 mcg RAE (2,330–3,000 IU). Avoid products exceeding this unless medically necessary (pregnancy, deficiency under doctor's supervision).

- **Normal dietary vitamin A is safe** – Do NOT restrict vitamin A-rich foods (carrots, sweet potatoes, leafy greens, dairy, eggs). Diet does not need to change.

- **Blue-light blocking sunglasses** – Wear **100% UV-blocking** and **blue-blocking** (amber/orange tint) sunglasses outdoors. Brands: NoIR, EagleEyes, Zeiss (BlueGuard), Essilor (Blue UV Capture). Dark tint (80–90%) preferred outdoors.

- **Wide-brimmed hat** – Adds protection from sunlight (especially UV).

- **Avoid smoking** – Strongly advise cessation; smoking increases oxidative stress.

### Recommended (Moderate Evidence)

- **Healthy diet** – Rich in antioxidants (fruits, vegetables, leafy greens), omega-3 fatty acids (fish, flaxseed). No specific supplements have proven benefit, but a healthy diet supports retinal health.

- **Maintain healthy weight, exercise** – May reduce systemic oxidative stress.

- **Regular eye exams** – Every 6–12 months for OCT, fundus autofluorescence (FAF), visual field testing.

- **Amsler grid monitoring** – Monthly (or weekly if high-risk for CNV). Report new distortion, scotoma, or vision changes to retina specialist.

### Not Recommended

- **Low-vitamin A diet** – No evidence of benefit; may cause vitamin A deficiency (night blindness, immune dysfunction). Normal diet is safe.

- **Lutein/zeaxanthin supplements** – No proven benefit for Stargardt (unlike age-related macular degeneration). Not harmful, but not effective.

- **Vitamins C, E, zinc (AREDS2)** – No evidence for Stargardt.

- **Fish oil (DHA)** – No proven benefit, but safe.

## Treatment Limitations

- **No cure** – Progression continues inexorably.
- **No FDA-approved therapy** – All potential treatments are investigational; patients must enroll in clinical trials (not widely available).
- **Gene therapy challenges** – ABCA4 is a large gene (6.8 kb) exceeding AAV packaging capacity (4.7 kb). Dual AAV, lentiviral, and non-viral vectors are in development but not yet clinically available.
- **Stem cell therapy** – Phase 1/2 trials show safety but limited efficacy (no restoration of vision).
- **Low vision rehabilitation** – Essential but does not restore lost vision; requires patient adaptation and training.
- **Complications** – CNV (2–5%) treatable with anti-VEGF, but others (retinal detachment, cataract surgery complications) add morbidity.

## Prognosis

### Natural History (ABCA4-related Stargardt)

Progression varies dramatically based on genotype (number of null alleles) and age of onset.

| Genotype (Severity) | Typical Onset | Visual Acuity at 20 years | Legal Blindness (20/200) Age | Progression Rate |
|---------------------|---------------|---------------------------|------------------------------|------------------|
| **Two null mutations** (severe) | 6–10 years | 20/50–20/100 | 20–30 years | Rapid |
| **One null + one missense** (moderate) | 12–20 years | 20/40–20/80 | 30–45 years | Moderate |
| **Two mild missense** (mild/late) | 25–40 years | 20/25–20/40 | 50–70 years (or not in lifetime) | Slow |

### Functional Outcomes (Typical)

- **Reading ability**:
    - Most patients can read with magnification until central scotoma >5° (~20/100–20/200).
    - By age 30–50, most require screen readers or audiobooks for extended reading.
- **Face recognition**:
    - Dense central scotoma (5–10°) makes face recognition impossible by age 30–50 in severe forms; later in mild forms.
- **Driving**:
    - Most patients lose legal driving acuity (<20/40) by age 20–30 (severe) or 30–50 (mild). Peripheral vision preserved, but central vision too poor for licensure.
- **Peripheral vision**:
    - Remains intact until very late stages (60+ years). Patients can navigate, walk, and perform daily activities independently.
- **Night blindness**:
    - Not a prominent early symptom; some patients develop mild nyctalopia in late stages, but rarely disabling (unlike RP).
- **Legal blindness** (20/200 or worse):
    - 70–80% of patients with early-onset (<20 years) become legally blind by age 40–50.
    - Late-onset (>40 years) may never reach 20/200.

### Factors Associated with Better Prognosis

- **Late-onset (>30 years)** – Slow progression, often retains useful central vision into 60s–70s.
- **Mild missense mutations** (e.g., p.Asn1868Ile).
- **No CNV** (choroidal neovascularization) – if CNV develops, adds additional vision loss (even with treatment).
- **No cystoid macular edema** – rare but reversible.

### Factors Associated with Worse Prognosis

- **Early-onset (<10–15 years)** – Rapid progression, legal blindness by 20–30s.
- **Two null mutations** (e.g., nonsense, frameshift, large deletions).
- **Male sex** – Some studies suggest faster progression (controversial).
- **High-dose vitamin A supplementation** – May accelerate disease (avoid).

### Impact of Lifestyle Modifications (Theoretical)

- **Avoiding vitamin A supplements** – May slow progression by reducing bisretinoid formation, but no clinical trial proof.
- **Blue-light protection** – May slow RPE damage, but unproven.

**Key message**: Stargardt disease is a relentlessly progressive macular dystrophy, but the rate varies widely. Many patients maintain functional peripheral vision and independent mobility for life, even if central vision is severely impaired. Low vision rehabilitation is the most impactful intervention available today. Clinical trials (gene therapy, tinlarebant, stem cells) offer hope for future treatments.

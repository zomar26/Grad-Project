# Symptoms of Pathologic Myopia

## Common Symptoms

Symptoms typically begin in early to middle adulthood (30s–50s) and progress slowly over decades. Early symptoms are often subtle and may be attributed to "worsening nearsightedness."

- **Progressive decline in best-corrected visual acuity** – Vision gradually worsens from 20/20–20/40 to 20/80–20/200 over years to decades, even with the strongest glasses or contact lenses. This distinguishes pathologic from simple myopia (where vision is fully correctable).

- **Metamorphopsia (visual distortion)** – Straight lines (door frames, window blinds, text lines, telephone poles) appear wavy, bent, or crooked. This is an early sign of myopic choroidal neovascularization (mCNV) or myopic traction maculopathy (foveoschisis). Often the first symptom prompting ophthalmology referral.

- **Central or paracentral scotoma (blind spot)** – A dark, gray, or hazy area in the center or slightly off-center of vision. May be small initially ("a speck") and enlarge over months to years. Caused by macular atrophy or mCNV scarring.

- **Reduced contrast sensitivity** – Difficulty distinguishing objects from their background, particularly in low-light conditions (twilight, fog, dimly lit rooms). Patients report trouble seeing steps, curbs, or faces in shadow.

- **Glare and light sensitivity** – Bothersome or disabling glare from oncoming headlights, bright sunlight, or overhead fluorescent lights. Caused by light scatter through atrophic or scarred macular tissue.

- **Floaters** – Sudden or gradual increase in floaters (spots, cobwebs, or strands in vision). Common in pathologic myopia due to posterior vitreous detachment (occurs earlier than in non-myopic eyes) or vitreous liquefaction. **A sudden shower of floaters with flashes of light** may indicate retinal tear or detachment—a medical emergency.

- **Photopsia (flashing lights)** – Brief, spontaneous flashes of light in the peripheral vision (often described as "lightning streaks" or "arcs"). Caused by vitreous traction on the retina.

## Advanced Symptoms

- **Severe central vision loss (legal blindness)** – Visual acuity 20/200 to counting fingers or worse in the affected eye. Caused by:
    - Geographic macular atrophy (end-stage RPE and photoreceptor loss)
    - Subfoveal mCNV scar (disciform scar)
    - Full-thickness macular hole
    - Macular hole retinal detachment

- **Dense, stable central scotoma** – Patient cannot read any words, recognize faces directly ahead, or see fine details. Peripheral vision remains intact (preserves mobility).

- **Enlargement of blind spot over months to years** – Patchy atrophic lesions slowly enlarge and coalesce, progressively enlarging the central or paracentral scotoma.

- **Micropsia (objects appear smaller)** – More common in myopic traction maculopathy (foveoschisis) or posterior staphyloma. Text may look "compressed" or "crowded." Icons on a phone may appear miniaturized.

- **Charles Bonnet syndrome (CBS)** – Complex visual hallucinations (geometric patterns, faces, animals, entire scenes) in patients with significant central vision loss. The patient is aware the images are not real. Occurs in 10–20% of advanced pathologic myopia.

- **Sudden, painless vision loss over hours to days** – Indicates:
    - Acute mCNV hemorrhage (blood under the fovea)
    - Macular hole formation
    - Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment (peripheral break with macula-off detachment)
    - Vitreous hemorrhage (from mCNV or retinal tear)

- **Peripheral field loss (not just central)** – In retinal detachment or advanced glaucoma (a common comorbidity). Described as a "curtain" or "shadow" coming across the vision.

- **Diplopia (double vision) in one eye (monocular diplopia)** – Rare; can occur with large posterior staphyloma causing retinal distortion.

## When to Seek Medical Attention

**Urgent (same day or within 24–48 hours)** – Call your ophthalmologist immediately if you have known pathologic myopia and experience:

- **Sudden onset of metamorphopsia** (new waviness of straight lines) – Possible active myopic CNV; requires urgent OCT and fluorescein angiography.
- **Sudden increase in floaters, especially a "shower" of many small floaters** – Possible posterior vitreous detachment with retinal tear.
- **Flashes of light** (photopsia) – Especially if persistent or in one eye only.
- **A dark curtain, shadow, or veil across any part of the visual field** – Until proven otherwise, this is retinal detachment until proven otherwise.
- **Sudden, painless central vision loss** – Possible mCNV hemorrhage, macular hole, or retinal detachment involving the macula.

**Emergency (go to emergency department immediately)** :

- Sudden vision loss accompanied by eye pain (unusual for pathologic myopia alone; suggests acute angle-closure glaucoma, uveitis, or other emergency).
- Trauma to the eye (patients with pathologic myopia have thinner sclera and higher risk of rupture).

**Routine (schedule within weeks to months)** :

- Gradual worsening of central vision over months to years
- New difficulty reading smaller print despite updated glasses
- Enlargement of known scotoma (gradual, not sudden)
- Annual comprehensive dilated eye exam for all patients with pathologic myopia (even without new symptoms)

**Special note**: If you have high myopia but no diagnosed degenerative changes, schedule a baseline dilated exam and then every 1–2 years (more frequently if progression is noted).

## Symptoms That May Affect Daily Activities

| Activity | Potential Symptom Impact |
|----------|--------------------------|
| Reading | Metamorphopsia makes lines wavy; scotoma causes words to break in the middle; micropsia makes text look compressed. Need for large print (18+ point) or magnification. |
| Recognizing faces | Central scotoma covers the nose/eyes/mouth area; patient may recognize people only by voice or peripheral cues (hair, clothing). |
| Driving | Central vision loss makes reading road signs impossible; metamorphopsia distorts distances; glare from headlights (night) or sun (day) disabling; peripheral field loss from glaucoma or detachment creates blind spots. Most patients with advanced pathologic myopia cannot drive legally or safely. |
| Watching television | Subtitles and scores unreadable; fine details (expressions, small objects) are missed; peripheral field loss may make patient unaware of action on one side. |
| Computer work | Wavy text (metamorphopsia), scotoma covers icons or cursor, micropsia makes fonts appear smaller than actual size. |
| Walking on uneven ground | Reduced contrast sensitivity and central scotoma increase fall risk, especially on stairs, curbs, or poorly lit paths. |
| Cooking | Difficulty seeing dial settings on stoves; cannot read small-print measurements; risk of burns/cuts from unseen hazards at counter edge. |
| Taking medications | Cannot read small-print labels; may mistake pills. |
| Occupational tasks | Surgeons, pilots, machinists, drivers, and anyone requiring fine central vision may be unable to work. Consider low vision rehabilitation or vocational retraining. |
| Sports | Difficulty tracking balls (tennis, baseball), seeing golf balls against grass, or avoiding obstacles (skiing, cycling). |
| Nighttime activities | Reduced contrast sensitivity and glare make twilight/night outdoor activities hazardous. |
| Social situations | Difficulty recognizing faces leads to anxiety and social withdrawal; fear of not seeing someone waving or approaching. |

# Living with Pathologic Myopia

## Reading and Computer Use

Central vision loss (scotoma), metamorphopsia (waviness), and reduced contrast are the main challenges.

**For mild to moderate vision loss (20/30–20/80)** :

- **Magnification** – Handheld magnifiers (3–5×) for short tasks (menus, pill bottles). Electronic video magnifiers (CCTV) for extended reading.
- **Large print** – Large-print books (18+ point font) or e-readers with adjustable font (Kindle, iPad). Increase font size until words are clear despite scotoma.
- **High contrast** – Black text on white background. Avoid low-contrast combinations (gray text on white, colored text).
- **Computer accessibility**:
    - Screen magnification (Windows Magnifier, macOS Zoom) – 2–4× often helpful.
    - High contrast mode (white text on black) – reduces glare and improves readability for some.
    - Screen readers (VoiceOver, Narrator, NVDA) if reading becomes too slow.
- **Dark mode** – On phones, tablets, computers; reduces glare and may reduce perceived distortion.
- **Audiobooks** – Audible, Libby (library), Bard (free for low vision).
- **Text-to-speech apps** – Seeing AI (scans printed text aloud), KNFB Reader.

**For severe vision loss (20/100–20/400)** :

- **Video magnifiers** with higher magnification (10–20×) and contrast enhancement.
- **Screen readers** as primary computer interface (VoiceOver on Apple devices is excellent).
- **Voice dictation** for writing (Dragon NaturallySpeaking, built-in options on macOS/Windows).
- **Talking devices** (clocks, scales, thermometers).

**For metamorphopsia (wavy text)** :

- Use single-word reading with a "window card" (paper with a cutout).
- Increase font size dramatically; waviness becomes less noticeable per letter.
- Consider low vision rehabilitation for eccentric viewing training (using peripheral retina to read).

## Mobility and Navigation

**Central scotoma and reduced contrast sensitivity** – The main mobility limitations. Peripheral vision remains intact until end-stage disease.

- **Central scotoma** – Patients cannot see obstacles directly ahead but will see them peripherally. Learn to scan the environment (move head and eyes constantly) rather than fixating.
- **Eccentric viewing training** – Use peripheral retina to "look" at objects. Example: If scotoma is in the right central field, turn your head slightly to the left so the object falls on the left paracentral retina.
- **Reduced contrast sensitivity** – Difficulty seeing steps, curbs, pavement changes, and dark clothing against dark backgrounds.
    - Use high-contrast tape on step edges (yellow or white on dark steps, black on light steps).
    - Install handrails on both sides of staircases.
    - Keep pathways clear and well-lit.

- **Nighttime navigation** – Contrast sensitivity worsens in dim light. Use bright LED flashlights (200+ lumens). Motion-sensor night lights in hallways, bathrooms, kitchen.

- **White cane** – Not just for total blindness. Patients with central scotoma benefit from a cane to detect curbs, steps, and obstacles in the lower visual field (often missed due to scotoma).

- **Orientation and mobility (O&M) training** – Essential for those with vision loss affecting safe navigation. Teaches cane use, route planning, auditory cues, and public transit navigation.

## Driving Considerations

**Most patients with pathologic myopia and degenerative changes cannot drive safely or legally** due to:

- Central scotoma (cannot read street signs, see traffic lights, or detect pedestrians ahead)
- Metamorphopsia (distances misjudged; road markings appear wavy)
- Reduced contrast sensitivity (difficulty seeing road edges, debris, or pedestrians in low light)
- Glare (disabling from oncoming headlights at night)
- Peripheral field loss (if glaucoma or retinal detachment is present)
- Legal visual acuity requirements (often 20/40 or better; many patients fall below this)

**When to stop driving** :

- Visual acuity worse than 20/40 in the better eye (check local laws; some jurisdictions allow 20/70 with restrictions).
- Central scotoma that covers traffic lights or road signs.
- Difficulty seeing pedestrians or curbs at intersections.
- Any night driving difficulty (stop night driving first, then consider day driving).
- If you have had a retinal detachment or macular hole involving the macula.

**Alternatives to driving** :

- Family and friends (arrange regular rides)
- Ride-sharing (Uber, Lyft) – many offer senior/low vision discounts
- Paratransit services (door-to-door, lower cost; apply through local transit authority)
- Public transit (with O&M training)
- Volunteer driver programs (e.g., ITNAmerica, local senior centers, Lions Club)

**If you continue driving** (early disease, good central vision):

- Get a driving evaluation from a low vision driving rehabilitation specialist (occupational therapist with advanced training).
- Avoid night driving, highway driving, and unfamiliar routes.
- Use bioptic telescopes (telescopic lenses mounted on glasses) – legal in some states, but requires specialized training; not suitable for scotomas.
- Reassess annually – vision can decline unpredictably.

## Home Adaptations

**Lighting** :

- Bright, even illumination throughout the home (LED daylight bulbs 5000–6500K, 800+ lumens).
- Task lighting at seating areas, kitchen counters, desks.
- Motion-sensor night lights in hallways, bathrooms, bedroom.
- Position lights to avoid glare (use shades or indirect lighting).

**Contrast enhancement** :

- **Steps and stairs** – High-contrast tape on step edges (yellow on dark, black on light). Mark top and bottom steps with contrasting color.
- **Doors and walls** – Paint door frames contrasting color (e.g., white door on dark wall). Mark light switches with black marker on white plates.
- **Dining** – White dishes on dark placemats; dark cups on light coasters. Clear glasses on dark coaster.
- **Bathroom** – White soap on dark soap dish; dark towels on white racks. Colored toilet seat (red or blue) on white porcelain.

**Safety** :

- Remove throw rugs and low coffee tables (trip hazards).
- Run electrical cords along walls under cord covers.
- Keep furniture in fixed positions; warn patient before rearranging.
- Install grab bars in showers and next to toilets.
- Non-slip mats in bathtubs/showers.

**Kitchen** :

- Contrasting cutting boards (white board on dark counter, dark on light).
- Tactile dots on stove dials, microwave keypads, washer/dryer settings.
- Audible timers, talking kitchen scales, large-print measuring cups.
- Pour liquids by placing finger over rim of cup to feel level.
- Label leftovers with large-print masking tape and bold marker.

**Medication management** :

- Request large-print labels from pharmacy.
- Use weekly pill organizers with large compartments (or monthly blister packs from pharmacy).
- Magnifier app or handheld magnifier for reading labels.
- Talking pill reminder alarms (smartphone or dedicated device).
- Consider automatic pill dispensers (e.g., MedMinder, Hero).

## Emotional and Psychological Impact

Pathologic myopia often affects middle-aged to older adults, but vision loss can begin in the 30s–40s, causing significant psychological distress.

**Common emotional responses** :

- **Grief** – Loss of driving, reading, face recognition, hobbies, and sometimes career.
- **Anxiety** – Fear of future vision loss, fear of becoming blind (reassure: total blindness is rare; peripheral vision usually remains intact).
- **Depression** – Prevalence ~25–30% in advanced pathologic myopia. Risk factors: recent vision loss, bilateral involvement, loss of driving, social isolation.
- **Guilt** – Parents may feel guilty for passing on myopia genes. Patients may blame themselves ("I read too much as a child").
- **Frustration** – Vision fluctuates (worse when tired, in bright light, or under stress); planning is difficult.
- **Anger at eye care system** – "Why didn't anyone tell me I was at risk?" "Why wasn't my child treated with atropine earlier?"
- **Charles Bonnet syndrome (CBS)** – Visual hallucinations (geometric patterns, faces, animals) in 10–20% with central vision loss. Patients often hide this due to fear of mental illness. Reassurance is treatment; explain that it is common and not a sign of dementia or psychosis.

**Coping strategies** :

- **Low vision rehabilitation** – Occupational therapy, mobility training, assistive technology training. Single most effective intervention for quality of life.
- **Support groups** – Foundation Fighting Blindness chapters, online communities (Reddit r/myopia, Facebook "Pathologic Myopia Support"). Peer support is invaluable.
- **Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)** – Addresses catastrophic thinking ("I'll be totally blind") and helps develop adaptive coping.
- **Treat depression** – SSRIs (sertraline, escitalopram) are safe and effective. Do not avoid due to "not wanting to take pills."
- **Focus on preserved abilities** – Peripheral vision, hearing, cognition, and independence in many activities remain intact.

**For Charles Bonnet syndrome** :

- Reassure: "This is a normal response of the brain to vision loss. You are not 'going crazy.'"
- Keep a log of hallucinations; they tend to decrease when you acknowledge them rather than fight them.
- Try "eye movement" techniques (look away and back) or increasing lighting.
- If distressing, consider low-dose atypical antipsychotics (quetiapine) – rarely needed.

## Tips for Family Members and Caregivers

**Understanding the condition** :

- Pathologic myopia is **not the same as simple myopia** that can be fully corrected with glasses. It causes progressive, irreversible structural damage to the retina.
- Vision loss is often central, not total. The patient sees the edges but misses the middle (like looking through a donut hole).
- The patient's intelligence, personality, and cognition are intact. Do not assume cognitive decline if they bump into things or miss details.

**Practical support** :

- **Drive** – If the patient cannot drive, offer rides to appointments, shopping, social events. Be reliable; uncertainty about rides causes anxiety.
- **Read small print** – Medication labels, mail, restaurant menus, instructions.
- **Help with home adaptations** – Install contrasting tape, night lights, grab bars. Remove trip hazards.
- **Organize medications** – Fill weekly pill organizers; request large-print labels from pharmacy.
- **Accompany to eye appointments** – Take notes, ask questions, drive home (dilation and injections impair vision temporarily).

**Communication** :

- **Identify yourself** – "Hi Mom, it's Sarah." Do not expect recognition by silhouette or gait (central scotoma makes face recognition unreliable).
- **Describe the environment** – "The curb is 6 inches down," "There's a pole 3 feet to your left."
- **Use clock face for object location** – "Your coffee cup is at 2 o'clock, about 12 inches away."
- **Do not rearrange furniture** – The patient memorizes layouts. Moving items disorients them and increases fall risk.
- **Ask before guiding** – "Would you like to take my elbow?" Offer your arm; do not grab the patient's hand or arm.

**Emotional support** :

- **Validate their experience** – "I can see why wavy text makes reading so frustrating." "It must be scary to not recognize faces."
- **Do not minimize** – "At least you're not blind" or "My aunt has that and she's fine" dismisses their struggle.
- **Encourage low vision rehab** – Many patients resist ("I'm not that bad yet"). Emphasize it's for maximizing independence, not admitting defeat.
- **Be patient** – Vision loss is exhausting. The patient may be irritable or depressed. This is the disease, not a personal attack.

**Caregiver self-care** :

- Caregiver burnout is common, especially if the patient has advanced vision loss and requires significant assistance.
- Respite care – In-home aides for a few hours weekly, adult day programs (some specifically for low vision).
- Join a caregiver support group (low vision organizations, Foundation Fighting Blindness).
- Set boundaries – You can help without being a 24/7 caregiver.
- Remind yourself: Vision loss is a family disease; your emotional health matters too.

**Key message to patients and families** : Pathologic myopia is a challenging, progressive disease, but most patients retain useful peripheral vision and independence for decades. With low vision rehabilitation, assistive technology, and a supportive environment, quality of life can remain high. Modern treatments (anti-VEGF, vitrectomy) have dramatically improved outcomes for complications. Do not lose hope—adaptation is possible, and research continues.

# Causes and Risk Factors of Pathologic Myopia

## Main Causes

Pathologic myopia results from **excessive, progressive axial elongation** of the eye during growth (and sometimes into adulthood), leading to mechanical stretching and degeneration of the posterior ocular structures. The fundamental cause is a combination of genetic predisposition and environmental factors that influence ocular growth regulation.

**Key pathophysiologic steps**:

1. **Genetic susceptibility** – Multiple genes (MYP1–MYP25 loci) regulate scleral extracellular matrix remodeling, retinal dopamine signaling, and axial length determination.

2. **Environmental factors** – High educational pressure, near work (prolonged reading, screen use), and lack of outdoor time during childhood accelerate axial elongation in genetically susceptible children.

3. **Biomechanical failure** – The posterior sclera becomes abnormally thin and weak due to altered collagen crosslinking and reduced collagen type I. Under normal intraocular pressure, the weakened sclera progressively expands (creep).

4. **Choroidal thinning and hypoxia** – Mechanical stretching thins the choroid, reducing oxygen delivery to the outer retina. Chronic hypoxia upregulates VEGF and other growth factors.

5. **Bruch's membrane rupture** – Mechanical stress causes linear breaks (lacquer cracks) in Bruch's membrane, allowing choroidal neovascularization (myopic CNV).

6. **Vitreous traction** – Abnormal vitreous adhesion to the macula, combined with posterior staphyloma, creates tractional forces leading to foveoschisis, foveal detachment, and macular holes.

## Risk Factors

### Strongly associated risk factors

- **High myopia in childhood** – Children with > -6.00D by age 6–8 have a 30–50% risk of progressing to pathologic myopia with degenerative changes by middle age. Rapidly progressing myopia (> -1.00D/year) is a red flag.

- **Family history** – Having a first-degree relative (parent, sibling) with pathologic myopia increases risk 3–10 fold. If both parents have high myopia, the child's risk is ~30–50%.

- **East Asian ethnicity** – Prevalence of pathologic myopia is 5–10% in East Asian populations (Japan, China, South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore) vs. 1–3% in Caucasians and <1% in African-derived populations. Genetic and environmental (education intensity) factors contribute.

- **High axial length** – Axial length >26.5 mm (normal ~23–24 mm) strongly predicts pathologic myopia. Each 1 mm increase in axial length increases risk of myopic macular degeneration by ~2–3 fold.

- **Age** – Pathologic changes typically begin after age 40–50; prevalence increases with age. By age 70–80, 30–50% of patients with high myopia have some degree of myopic macular degeneration.

### Moderately associated risk factors

- **Female sex** – Women have slightly higher risk of pathologic myopia progression and myopic CNV (female:male ratio ~1.5:1). Possible hormonal influences on scleral remodeling.

- **High educational attainment** – Each year of education increases myopia risk by ~1.5 diopters. University graduates have 3–5× higher pathologic myopia risk than non-graduates. Mechanism: near work load during critical developmental years.

- **Occupational near work** – Microscopists, surgeons, computer programmers, and other professions requiring prolonged near focus may have accelerated progression (effect most pronounced in childhood/adolescence).

- **Low time outdoors in childhood** – Children who spend <1–2 hours/day outdoors have 2–4× higher risk of developing high myopia and progression to pathologic myopia. Bright light and dopamine release in the retina suppress axial elongation.

## Genetic Factors

Pathologic myopia is a complex polygenic disorder with high heritability (70–90% for refractive error).

**Major genetic loci (MYP genes)** :

- **MYP1 (Xq28)** – Rare; causes X-linked high myopia (early onset, very high myopia > -10D).
- **MYP2 (18p11.31)** – Associated with early-onset high myopia.
- **MYP3 (12q21–23)** – Identified in autosomal dominant high myopia families.
- **MYP11 (4q22–q27)** – Linkage to ZNF644 gene (zinc finger protein).
- **MYP20 (1p36.12)** – Involves BMP4 (bone morphogenetic protein 4).

**Genes involved in scleral extracellular matrix** :

- **COL1A1, COL2A1, COL5A1** – Collagen genes; variants associated with scleral thinning and axial elongation.
- **LAMA2, LAMA4** – Laminin genes influencing Bruch's membrane integrity.
- **TGFB1, TGFB2, TGFB3** – Transforming growth factor-beta involved in scleral remodeling.

**Genes involved in ocular growth regulation** :

- **ZNF644** – Transcription factor regulating scleral gene expression.
- **SCO2** – Mitochondrial protein; mutations cause progressive myopia and optic atrophy.
- **ARR3** – Arrestin-3; X-linked locus associated with high myopia in females.

**Genes associated with myopic CNV** :

- **CFH** (complement factor H) – Same variant (Y402H) associated with AMD and myopic CNV.
- **ARMS2/HTRA1** – Age-related maculopathy susceptibility locus also linked to myopic CNV.
- **VEGF** – Vascular endothelial growth factor polymorphisms influence CNV risk and treatment response.

**Clinical genetic testing** – Not currently recommended for routine diagnosis of pathologic myopia. Research use only.

## Environmental Factors

- **Near work** – High cumulative hours of reading, studying, screen use (computers, smartphones, tablets, video games) during childhood and adolescence. Effect size: each additional 10 hours/week of near work increases myopia progression by ~0.1–0.2D/year.

- **Schooling** – Earlier school entry (age 4–5 vs. 6–7) and high-pressure education systems (e.g., "cram schools" in East Asia) increase myopia burden.

- **Lack of outdoor light** – Children who spend <30 minutes/day outdoors have much higher myopia progression than those with 2+ hours/day. Bright outdoor light (10,000+ lux vs. 500 lux indoors) stimulates retinal dopamine release, which inhibits axial elongation.

- **Urban environment** – Urban vs. rural living increases myopia risk, likely due to less outdoor time, more near work, and higher educational pressure.

- **Premature birth** – Low birth weight and retinopathy of prematurity (ROP) increase risk of high myopia and myopic degeneration later in life.

- **Nutrition** – Limited evidence; high glycemic index diets may modestly increase myopia progression; omega-3 fatty acids may be protective (weak).

## Prevention Strategies

### Primary prevention (preventing development of high myopia and pathologic changes)

**Early childhood intervention (most effective)** :

- **Increase time outdoors** – At least 90 minutes per day (preferably 2+ hours) outdoors during daylight. School-based programs (recess outdoors, outdoor classes) reduce myopia incidence by 20–30%.

- **Reduce near work breaks** – 20-20-20 rule: every 20 minutes, look 20 feet away for 20 seconds. Not proven to reduce progression but reduces eye strain.

- **Monitor axial length** – In children with early myopia (age 6–10), annual axial length measurement (not just refraction) predicts future risk.

**Pharmacologic prevention (atropine)** :

- **Low-dose atropine (0.01–0.05% eye drops)** – Reduces myopia progression by 50–70% in children with progressive myopia. Given nightly. Minimal side effects (mild photophobia, near blur at 0.05%).
- **Indication** : Children age 6–12 with myopia progression > -0.50D/year or baseline myopia > -2.00D.

**Optical treatments** :

- **Orthokeratology (Ortho-K)** – Rigid gas-permeable contact lenses worn overnight temporarily reshape the cornea, reducing myopia. Slows axial elongation by ~30–50% in children.
- **Multifocal soft contact lenses** – Peripheral defocus design slows progression by ~25–40%.
- **Spectacle lenses with peripheral defocus** (e.g., MiyoSmart, Stellest) – Limited data; may reduce progression by 20–30%.

### Secondary prevention (preventing progression to vision-threatening lesions in established high myopia)

**Once high myopia or early pathologic changes are present** :

- **Regular dilated eye exams** – Every 6–12 months for OCT, fundus photography, and monitoring for lacquer cracks, mCNV, and foveoschisis.

- **Amsler grid home monitoring** – Monthly testing in each eye separately; report new distortion or scotoma immediately (possible mCNV).

- **Control modifiable risk factors** – Near work reduction, outdoor time (still beneficial in adults? limited evidence, but safe).

- **Avoid eye trauma** – Contact sports (boxing, martial arts) increase risk of retinal detachment and scleral rupture (thinner sclera). Protective eyewear recommended for high-risk activities.

- **Blood pressure control** – Hypertension may worsen myopic CNV progression (limited evidence, but standard cardiovascular health advice).

### Tertiary prevention (preventing severe vision loss from established complications)

- **Prompt treatment of mCNV** – Anti-VEGF injections within 4–6 weeks of symptom onset to prevent disciform scar.

- **Vitrectomy for myopic traction maculopathy** – Before macular hole or detachment develops.

- **Laser prophylaxis for peripheral retinal breaks** – Photocoagulation of atrophic holes and lattice degeneration to prevent retinal detachment (controversial; not all breaks require treatment).

**Key message for patients**: Pathologic myopia cannot be reversed, but you can slow progression in children (outdoor time, atropine, orthokeratology) and preserve vision in adults (regular monitoring, prompt treatment of complications, low vision rehabilitation).

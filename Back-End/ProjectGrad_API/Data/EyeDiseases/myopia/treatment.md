# Treatment of Pathologic Myopia

## Available Treatments

**There is no cure for pathologic myopia**—the axial elongation and scleral thinning cannot be reversed. Treatment focuses on:

1. **Refractive correction** – Glasses, contact lenses, or refractive surgery to optimize vision from remaining healthy retina.
2. **Preventing or slowing progression** (in children and young adults) – Atropine, orthokeratology, multifocal lenses.
3. **Treating complications** – Anti-VEGF for myopic choroidal neovascularization (mCNV), vitrectomy for myopic traction maculopathy (MTM), laser or surgery for retinal detachment.
4. **Low vision rehabilitation** – Maximizing functional use of remaining vision.

## Medications

### Anti-VEGF agents (first-line for myopic CNV)

**Indication**: Active myopic choroidal neovascularization (mCNV) – presence of subretinal fluid, hemorrhage, or leakage on fluorescein angiography.

**Agents** (all injected intravitreally):

- **Ranibizumab (Lucentis®)** – Monoclonal antibody fragment. Monthly until fluid resolves, then treat-and-extend (T&E). FDA-approved for myopic CNV.
- **Aflibercept (Eylea®)** – VEGF receptor-Fc fusion protein. Monthly loading (3 doses), then T&E (typically every 8–12 weeks). Off-label for mCNV but widely used.
- **Bevacizumab (Avastin®)** – Full-length antibody. Off-label, equally effective at lower cost ($50 vs. $2000 per dose). Not FDA-approved for mCNV.

**Treatment protocol** (based on RADIANCE and MYRROR trials):

- **Loading phase**: 1–3 monthly injections (typically until fluid resolves).
- **Maintenance**: Treat-and-extend – extend interval by 2 weeks if stable (up to 12 weeks). Many patients achieve quiescence after 1–3 injections.
- **Prognosis**: 90–95% of eyes have improved or stabilized vision at 1 year; 50–60% gain ≥3 lines (15 ETDRS letters).
- **Duration**: Some patients need only short-term treatment (1–4 injections); others require long-term monitoring (30–40% recur within 1–2 years).

**Adverse effects**: Endophthalmitis (0.05–0.1% per injection), retinal detachment (rare), cataract acceleration (minimal with small-gauge injections), systemic thromboembolic events (very rare, no increased risk over control in mCNV trials).

### Atropine (for slowing progression in children)

- **Indication**: Children age 6–12 with progressive myopia (> -0.50D/year) or baseline myopia > -2.00D.
- **Dose**: 0.01% to 0.05% eye drops nightly.
- **Mechanism**: Blocks muscarinic receptors on scleral fibroblasts, reducing axial elongation (mechanism not fully understood).
- **Efficacy**: Reduces myopia progression by 50–70% over 2–3 years.
- **Side effects**: Photophobia (dilated pupil) and near blur (loss of accommodation) – dose-dependent. 0.01% has minimal side effects but lower efficacy; 0.05% more effective but more side effects.
- **Duration**: Typically continued until age 15–18 or progression stabilizes.

### No role for other medications

- **Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors** – No benefit.
- **Antioxidant supplements (AREDS2, vitamins)** – Not shown to slow myopic macular degeneration progression (unlike AMD).
- **Corticosteroids** – May worsen CNV; contraindicated.
- **Statins** – No evidence.

## Surgical Options

### 1. Vitrectomy for Myopic Traction Maculopathy (MTM)

**Indications** (progressive or vision-threatening):

- **Foveoschisis with progressive vision loss or metamorphopsia**
- **Foveal detachment** (with risk of macular hole)
- **Full-thickness macular hole** (especially with retinal detachment)
- **Macular hole retinal detachment (MHRD)**

**Procedure**:

- 23–25 gauge pars plana vitrectomy
- Posterior vitreous detachment induction (if not already present)
- Internal limiting membrane (ILM) peeling (releases tangential traction)
- Gas tamponade (SF6 or C3F8) for macular holes or detachments
- Possible face-down positioning post-operatively (for holes/detachments)

**Outcomes**:

- Foveoschisis: 80–90% anatomical improvement; 60–70% visual improvement
- Macular hole: closure rate 80–95%; visual improvement modest (average gain 2–3 lines)
- MHRD: anatomical success 80–90%; functional success variable (dependent on duration of detachment)

**Complications**: Cataract progression (100% within 1–2 years; requires combined phacoemulsification or subsequent surgery), retinal detachment (1–5%), macular hole reopening (5–10%), endophthalmitis (<0.1%).

### 2. Laser Photocoagulation

**Indications**:

- **Peripheral retinal breaks (atrophic holes, operculated tears)** – To prevent rhegmatogenous retinal detachment.
- **Lattice degeneration with atrophic holes** – Controversial; many prophylactically treat if high-risk (fellow eye detachment history, symptomatic, large holes).

**Procedure**: Argon or diode laser applied around the break, creating chorioretinal adhesion.

**Limitations** – Does NOT treat mCNV (use anti-VEGF). Does NOT treat macular pathology (no laser for myopic traction maculopathy or subfoveal CNV).

### 3. Scleral Buckling and Pars Plana Vitrectomy for Retinal Detachment

- **Indication**: Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment in pathologic myopia.
- **Challenges**: Thinner sclera (higher risk of perforation during buckling), posterior staphyloma (makes break location difficult). Often requires vitrectomy + gas/silicone oil + laser.
- **Outcomes**: Anatomical success 80–90%; functional success limited by pre-existing macular pathology.

### 4. Refractive Surgery (for stable high myopia)

**Indications**: Stable refraction for ≥12 months, no active myopic macular degeneration, healthy cornea.

**Options**:

- **Phakic intraocular lenses (pIOL)** – Implantable Collamer Lens (ICL). Preferred for very high myopia (-8D to -20D) with normal cornea. Reversible. Risk of cataract, glaucoma, endothelial cell loss.
- **Clear lens extraction (CLE) / Refractive lens exchange (RLE)** – Removal of natural lens with IOL implantation. Similar to cataract surgery. Indicated for patients with early cataract or presbyopia. Risks: retinal detachment (higher in myopes), posterior capsule opacification.
- **LASIK / PRK** – Limited to -8D to -10D maximum; not suitable for high myopia > -10D. Corneal thinning, ectasia risk.

**Contraindications**: Progressive myopia, active mCNV, foveoschisis, posterior staphyloma. Discuss with retina specialist before proceeding.

## Lifestyle Recommendations

- **Regular monitoring** – Annual dilated eye exams with OCT; Amsler grid monthly.
- **Protective eyewear** – UV-blocking sunglasses (theoretical benefit; not proven to slow degeneration). Polycarbonate lenses for impact protection (reduces eye injury risk).
- **Avoid eye trauma** – Boxing, martial arts, and high-impact sports may increase risk of scleral rupture and retinal detachment.
- **Blood pressure and cardiovascular health** – Control hypertension; may reduce progression of myopic CNV (limited evidence).
- **Smoking cessation** – Smoking may worsen choroidal ischemia; definite benefit for general vascular health.
- **Nutrition** – No specific diet proven effective, but healthy diet (rich in antioxidants, omega-3s) may support retinal health.

## Treatment Limitations

- **No cure** – Axial elongation and scleral thinning cannot be reversed.
- **Atrophy irreversible** – Once geographic atrophy develops (macular atrophy), no treatment restores vision.
- **Anti-VEGF for mCNV** – Does not prevent recurrences (30–40% at 2 years). Does not treat underlying choroidal atrophy.
- **Vitrectomy for MTM** – Does not restore atrophic photoreceptors; vision may remain limited even after successful anatomical repair.
- **Refractive surgery** – Improves spectacle independence but does not prevent progression of degenerative changes.
- **Retinal detachment risk persists** – Even after prophylactic laser, new breaks can form in other areas.

## Prognosis

### Visual outcomes by lesion type

| Lesion/Complication | Without Treatment | With Current Treatment |
|---------------------|------------------|------------------------|
| Myopic CNV | 80–90% progress to legal blindness (20/200) by 5–10 years | 90–95% stabilize or improve; 50–60% gain ≥3 lines |
| Foveoschisis (MTM) | 20–30% progress to foveal detachment or macular hole over 5 years | 80–90% anatomical improvement; 60–70% visual gain |
| Macular hole | Vision 20/200 to counting fingers | Closure 80–95%; vision improves to 20/50–20/100 |
| Geographic macular atrophy | Progressive enlargement (~0.2 mm²/year); eventual central scotoma | No treatment; vision declines to 20/200 |
| Retinal detachment (macula-off) | Permanent severe vision loss | Anatomical success 80–90%; final vision 20/200 or worse in 50% |

### Long-term prognosis (natural history)

- **Age 40–50** : Most patients with high myopia still have good central vision (20/20–20/40).
- **Age 60–70** : ~30% develop myopic CNV or foveoschisis; vision 20/50–20/100.
- **Age 80+** : ~50–60% develop macular atrophy; vision 20/200 or worse (legal blindness) in the worse eye; 20–30% bilateral legal blindness.

**Factors associated with better prognosis**:

- Early detection and treatment of mCNV (within 4–6 weeks of symptoms)
- Smaller mCNV lesion size (<1 disc area)
- Younger age (<50 years) at mCNV onset
- Absence of pre-existing patchy or macular atrophy
- Good response to anti-VEGF (fluid resolution after 1–2 injections)

**Factors associated with worse prognosis**:

- Large posterior staphyloma (especially macular involvement)
- Pre-existing lacquer cracks in the fovea
- Delayed treatment of mCNV (>3 months after symptoms)
- Recurrent mCNV with subretinal fibrosis
- Development of full-thickness macular hole
- Bilateral disease (30–40% of patients)

**Key message**: With modern anti-VEGF therapy and vitrectomy techniques, many patients with pathologic myopia retain functional vision for decades. However, geographic atrophy remains untreatable, and regular monitoring is essential to intervene before irreversible vision loss occurs.

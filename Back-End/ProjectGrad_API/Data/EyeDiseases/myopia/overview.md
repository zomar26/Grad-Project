# Pathologic Myopia

## Definition

Pathologic myopia (PM), also known as degenerative myopia or malignant myopia, is a severe, progressive form of nearsightedness characterized by excessive and abnormal elongation of the eyeball (axial length typically >26.5 mm or refractive error greater than -6.00 to -8.00 diopters) combined with degenerative changes to the posterior segment of the eye—including the sclera, choroid, retinal pigment epithelium (RPE), and retina. Unlike simple (school) myopia, pathologic myopia is a distinct disease entity that leads to irreversible vision loss from progressive structural damage, independent of refractive correction.

Pathologic myopia is a leading cause of blindness worldwide, particularly in East Asian populations, where prevalence is highest. It ranks among the top three causes of irreversible vision loss in many developed countries, along with age-related macular degeneration and diabetic retinopathy.

## How the Eye is Affected

The fundamental abnormality in pathologic myopia is **excessive axial elongation**—the eyeball grows too long, typically throughout adolescence and into early adulthood, and may continue to elongate slowly throughout life. This mechanical stretching causes progressive thinning and degeneration of the posterior ocular tissues.

**Affected structures and mechanisms**:

1. **Sclera** – The outer collagen shell of the eye becomes abnormally thin and weak due to altered extracellular matrix (decreased collagen type I, increased proteoglycans). Biomechanically, the posterior sclera progressively expands under normal intraocular pressure, driving further axial elongation. **Posterior staphyloma**—a circumscribed outpouching of the thinned sclera—is the hallmark structural lesion of pathologic myopia, present in 30–80% of cases.

2. **Choroid** – The vascular layer thins progressively (choroidal thinning). Choriocapillaris loss leads to outer retinal hypoxia. **Lacquer cracks** (linear breaks in Bruch's membrane) develop due to mechanical stress, appearing as yellowish lines on fundoscopy.

3. **Retinal pigment epithelium (RPE)** – Stretching and ischemia cause diffuse and patchy atrophy, visible as **diffuse chorioretinal atrophy** (yellowish-white, mottled appearance) and **patchy atrophy** (sharply demarcated, gray-white lesions). The atrophic areas enlarge over time.

4. **Bruch's membrane** – Breaks (lacquer cracks) allow ingrowth of abnormal choroidal vessels, leading to **myopic choroidal neovascularization (mCNV)** —the most vision-threatening complication.

5. **Retina** – Mechanical stretching causes thinning of the neurosensory retina. **Myopic traction maculopathy (MTM)** develops from vitreoretinal traction or posterior staphyloma, including foveoschisis (splitting of retinal layers), foveal detachment, and macular hole. Peripheral retinal degeneration (lattice degeneration) increases risk of retinal detachment.

**Why vision is lost** : Progressive vision loss in pathologic myopia results from:
- Atrophy of the macula (central RPE and photoreceptor loss)
- Myopic choroidal neovascularization (leakage, hemorrhage, fibrosis)
- Myopic traction maculopathy (foveoschisis, macular hole)
- Glaucoma (increased risk due to structural changes)
- Retinal detachment (from peripheral breaks)

## Types or Stages

Pathologic myopia is a progressive continuum, but several classification systems exist. The most widely used for clinical research is the **META-PM (Meta-Analysis for Pathologic Myopia) classification**:

| Category | OCT/Fundus Findings | Visual Prognosis |
|----------|---------------------|------------------|
| **No pathologic lesions** | High myopia without posterior staphyloma or degenerative changes | Good (correctable to 20/20–20/40) |
| **Tessellated fundus** | Visible choroidal vessels due to RPE thinning; no atrophy | Good |
| **Diffuse chorioretinal atrophy** | Yellowish-white, ill-defined atrophy of posterior pole | Moderate vision loss possible |
| **Patchy atrophy** | Sharply demarcated, gray-white atrophic lesions (usually >several disc areas) | Moderate to severe vision loss |
| **Macular atrophy** | Atrophy involving the fovea directly | Severe central vision loss |
| **Myopic choroidal neovascularization (mCNV)** | Active or scarred CNV beneath the fovea | Severe vision loss without treatment |
| **Posterior staphyloma** | Circumscribed outpouching of the sclera (diagnosed by OCT or ultrasound) | Variable; worsens other lesions |

**Simplified staging for patients** :

- **Early (pre-pathologic)** – High myopia (> -6D) without degenerative changes. At risk for progression.
- **Moderate (established)** – Diffuse atrophy, lacquer cracks, posterior staphyloma. Vision typically 20/30–20/100.
- **Advanced (severe)** – Patchy or macular atrophy, myopic CNV scarring, foveoschisis or macular hole. Vision 20/200 to counting fingers.
- **End-stage** – Geographic macular atrophy, large posterior staphyloma, dense central scotoma. Legal blindness.

**Myopic traction maculopathy (MTM)** classification (based on OCT):

- **Stage 1**: Foveoschisis (intraretinal splitting) without detachment
- **Stage 2**: Foveoschisis with foveal detachment
- **Stage 3**: Full-thickness macular hole
- **Stage 4**: Macular hole with retinal detachment

## Key Facts

- **Prevalence**: Pathologic myopia affects approximately 1–3% of the general population in Western countries and 5–10% in East Asian populations (Japan, China, South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore). The global prevalence is rising rapidly due to the "myopia epidemic."

- **Blinding disease**: Pathologic myopia is the leading cause of legal blindness in East Asian adults (25–50% of cases) and a top 3 cause in Caucasians (5–15% of blindness).

- **Age of onset**: Degenerative changes typically begin in the third to fifth decade of life but can progress slowly; vision loss often occurs between ages 40 and 70.

- **Posterior staphyloma**: Present in 30–80% of pathologic myopia; often multiple (compound staphylomas). Pathognomonic for the disease when present.

- **Myopic CNV**: Occurs in 5–10% of pathologic myopia, usually between ages 40 and 60. Without treatment, 80–90% of mCNV eyes progress to legal blindness (20/200 or worse) within 5–10 years. Anti-VEGF therapy has dramatically improved outcomes.

- **Myopic traction maculopathy (MTM)**: Affects 10–30% of eyes with posterior staphyloma; can be stable or progressive. Vitrectomy surgery improves vision in selected cases.

- **Glaucoma risk**: Pathologic myopia increases glaucoma risk 2–3 fold, often with normal intraocular pressure (normal-tension glaucoma). Difficult to diagnose due to large optic discs and peripapillary atrophy.

- **Retinal detachment**: Lifetime risk ~1–2% (5–10× higher than non-myopic population). Often atrophic holes in lattice degeneration, not just traumatic tears.

- **No cure**: Pathologic myopia cannot be reversed. Treatment focuses on preventing and managing complications (mCNV, MTM, retinal detachment, glaucoma).

- **Genetic basis**: Highly heritable (heritability 70–90% for refractive error). Multiple genetic loci identified (MYP genes), but no clinical genetic testing currently recommended.

- **Prevention of progression in children**: Atropine eye drops, orthokeratology (special contact lenses), and increased outdoor time slow axial elongation in progressive childhood myopia, potentially reducing risk of pathologic myopia later in life.

# Hypertensive Retinopathy

## Definition

Hypertensive retinopathy (HR) is a condition in which chronically elevated systemic blood pressure causes damage to the small blood vessels (arterioles, venules, and capillaries) of the retina—the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye. It is a common end-organ manifestation of hypertension, reflecting the impact of high blood pressure on the body's microvasculature.

The retina is uniquely suited for evaluating hypertensive damage because its blood vessels can be directly visualized non-invasively during a dilated eye examination. Consequently, the severity of hypertensive retinopathy correlates with the duration and degree of blood pressure elevation and serves as a marker for hypertensive damage in other vital organs, including the brain, kidneys, and heart.

## How the Eye is Affected

Hypertension damages the retinal microvasculature through two primary mechanisms: **vasospasm** (acute, reversible narrowing) and **arteriosclerosis** (chronic, structural thickening of vessel walls).

**Acute effects (vasospasm)** – In response to sudden or severe blood pressure elevation, the retinal arterioles constrict autoregulatorily (attempting to protect the capillary bed from high pressure). This causes:

- **Generalized arteriolar narrowing** – Diffuse reduction in arteriolar caliber
- **Focal arteriolar constriction** – Segmental ("sausage-like") narrowing

**Chronic effects (arteriosclerosis)** – Sustained hypertension leads to structural changes in the vessel wall:

- **Intimal thickening** – Proliferation of smooth muscle cells and deposition of hyaline material
- **Media hypertrophy** – Thickening of the muscular layer
- **Arteriovenous nicking (AV nicking)** – Thickened arteriolar wall compresses the underlying venule at crossing points, causing venous indentation, tapering, or banking (seen as "nicking" on fundoscopy)
- **Copper wire and silver wire arterioles** – Progressive thickening and opacification of the arteriolar wall leads to increased light reflex (copper wire) and eventually complete opacification (silver wire)

**Advanced damage (exudative and ischemic)** – When autoregulation fails or blood pressure is extremely high (e.g., hypertensive emergency), the retinal circulation becomes compromised:

- **Retinal hemorrhages** – Rupture of small vessels, typically flame-shaped (superficial) or dot-blot (deep)
- **Hard exudates** – Lipid deposits from chronic leakage; yellow, sharply demarcated
- **Cotton-wool spots (soft exudates)** – Microinfarcts of the retinal nerve fiber layer due to arteriolar occlusion; fluffy white, irregular borders
- **Optic disc edema (papilledema)** – Swelling of the optic nerve head due to elevated intracranial pressure or ischemia; hallmark of malignant hypertension

**Choroidal involvement** – In severe hypertension, the choroidal circulation (supplying the outer retina) can also be affected, leading to Elschnig spots (choroidal infarcts) and Siegrist streaks (hyperpigmented lines along choroidal vessels).

## Types or Stages

Hypertensive retinopathy is most commonly classified using the **Keith-Wagener-Barker (KWB) system** (modified by Scheie), which grades severity from normal to malignant hypertension.

| Grade | Funduscopic Findings | Clinical Correlation |
|-------|---------------------|----------------------|
| **Grade I** | Mild generalized arteriolar narrowing; arteriolar tortuosity; increased arteriolar light reflex (mild "copper wire") | Mild to moderate hypertension; often asymptomatic |
| **Grade II** | Definite focal arteriolar constriction; more pronounced generalized narrowing; arteriovenous (AV) nicking | Moderate hypertension; duration likely years |
| **Grade III** | Grade II changes PLUS retinal hemorrhages (flame or dot-blot), hard exudates, and/or cotton-wool spots | Severe hypertension; hypertensive urgency (BP usually >180/110 without end-organ failure) |
| **Grade IV** | Grade III changes PLUS optic disc edema (papilledema) | Malignant hypertension (hypertensive emergency with end-organ damage); requires immediate hospitalization |

**Alternative simplified classification** (more recent, AAO-aligned):

- **Mild HR** – Arteriolar narrowing, AV nicking, arteriolar wall changes (copper/silver wire)
- **Moderate HR** – Mild changes + retinal hemorrhages, hard exudates, cotton-wool spots
- **Severe (malignant) HR** – Moderate changes + optic disc edema

**Chronic vs. acute (accelerated) HR** :

- **Chronic HR** – Gradual arteriosclerotic changes (AV nicking, copper/silver wire) developing over years; vision usually unaffected
- **Accelerated/malignant HR** – Rapid onset of hemorrhages, exudates, and disc edema over weeks; associated with severe, acute BP elevation; vision may be reduced

## Key Facts

- **Prevalence**: Hypertensive retinopathy is present in approximately 40–70% of individuals with chronic hypertension, depending on duration, severity, and control.

- **Correlation with systemic damage**: The presence of moderate to severe HR (Grade III–IV) strongly correlates with hypertensive damage to other organs—left ventricular hypertrophy, chronic kidney disease, and silent cerebral infarcts.

- **Asymptomatic in early stages**: Grade I–II HR typically causes NO visual symptoms. Many patients are unaware of retinal changes until advanced disease.

- **Hypertensive emergency warning**: Optic disc edema (Grade IV) with vision loss is a medical emergency requiring immediate hospitalization for blood pressure control (risk of stroke, myocardial infarction, kidney failure).

- **Reversibility**: Early arteriolar narrowing (Grade I) may reverse with blood pressure control. Arteriosclerotic changes (AV nicking, silver wire) are largely irreversible. Hemorrhages, exudates, and cotton-wool spots typically resolve over weeks to months after BP normalization.

- **Macular involvement**: Hard exudates and edema can develop in the macula (macular star), causing central vision loss. This is a late complication of chronic severe hypertension.

- **Risk of retinal vein occlusion**: Chronic hypertension is the leading risk factor for retinal vein occlusions (branch or central), which can cause sudden, permanent vision loss.

- **Screening recommendation**: All hypertensive patients should have a dilated retinal examination at diagnosis and annually thereafter (or more frequently if retinopathy is present).

- **Not a stand-alone diagnosis**: Hypertensive retinopathy is a marker of systemic vascular disease. Its presence should prompt aggressive blood pressure management and evaluation for other end-organ damage (renal, cardiac, cerebrovascular).

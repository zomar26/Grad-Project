# Causes and Risk Factors of Hypertensive Retinopathy

## Main Causes

The sole direct cause of hypertensive retinopathy is **systemic arterial hypertension**—chronically elevated blood pressure. Both the **magnitude** (how high) and **duration** (how long) of hypertension determine the severity of retinal damage.

**Pathophysiology in brief**:

1. **Elevated systemic BP** increases perfusion pressure in the retinal arterioles.
2. **Autoregulatory vasoconstriction** attempts to protect the capillary bed from pressure-induced damage (causes generalized and focal arteriolar narrowing—Grade I–II).
3. **Chronic pressure overload** leads to arteriosclerotic structural changes (intimal thickening, media hypertrophy, hyalinization)—manifesting as AV nicking, copper/silver wire (Grade II–III).
4. **Failure of autoregulation** or extreme BP elevation (e.g., >180/120) causes:
   - **Breakdown of blood-retinal barrier** → retinal edema, hard exudates
   - **Arteriolar rupture** → flame/dot-blot hemorrhages
  5. **Arteriolar occlusion** (fibrinoid necrosis) → cotton-wool spots (microinfarcts)
   - **Optic nerve head ischemia** → optic disc edema (Grade IV)

**Acute (malignant) hypertension** – A distinct syndrome defined by:
- Severe hypertension (typically diastolic >130–140 mmHg)
- Retinal hemorrhages, exudates, and optic disc edema (Grade IV)
- Concomitant renal, cardiac, or cerebral microvascular damage
- Occurs in <1% of hypertensive patients, more common in young adults with uncontrolled hypertension or secondary hypertension (renal artery stenosis, pheochromocytoma)

## Risk Factors

The risk and severity of hypertensive retinopathy depend on factors influencing blood pressure control and individual vascular susceptibility.

### Hypertension-related factors (strongest)

- **Elevated systolic blood pressure (SBP)** – SBP >140 mmHg increases risk linearly; SBP >160–180 mmHg dramatically accelerates retinopathy. Isolated systolic hypertension (common in elderly) also causes retinopathy.

- **Elevated diastolic blood pressure (DBP)** – DBP >90 mmHg is associated with retinopathy; DBP >110–120 mmHg strongly predicts advanced grades.

- **Long duration of hypertension** – Patients with hypertension for >10 years have significantly higher prevalence of AV nicking, copper/silver wire, and exudative changes than those with recent diagnosis.

- **Poor blood pressure control** – Even with similar peak BP levels, patients with poorly controlled hypertension (frequent elevated readings, missed medications) develop more severe retinopathy than those with well-controlled BP.

- **Nocturnal non-dipping** – Absence of the normal 10–20% BP drop during sleep (common in diabetes, chronic kidney disease) accelerates target organ damage, including retinopathy.

### Demographic factors

- **Age** – Older adults (≥60 years) have greater cumulative exposure to hypertension and more arteriosclerotic changes; however, malignant hypertension is more common in younger adults (30–50 years).

- **Race/ethnicity** – Black/African American individuals have higher prevalence and severity of hypertensive retinopathy at similar BP levels, likely due to earlier onset, higher average BP, and genetic susceptibility to hypertensive end-organ damage.

- **Male sex** – Slightly higher risk of malignant hypertension and retinopathy in men, though women also commonly affected.

### Comorbid conditions (synergistic risk)

- **Diabetes mellitus** – Diabetic retinopathy and hypertensive retinopathy coexist in >50% of patients with both diseases. Hypertension accelerates diabetic retinopathy progression and increases risk of macular edema. Conversely, diabetic microvascular disease may worsen hypertensive changes.

- **Chronic kidney disease (CKD)** – Hypertension is both a cause and consequence of CKD. Retinopathy severity correlates with CKD stage and proteinuria.

- **Hyperlipidemia** – Dyslipidemia accelerates arteriosclerosis and promotes hard exudate formation in the retina.

- **Obesity (BMI ≥30)** – Contributes to hypertension and metabolic syndrome; associated with more severe retinopathy in some studies.

### Lifestyle factors

- **High sodium intake** – Worsens hypertension control.
- **Sedentary lifestyle** – Associated with higher BP and vascular stiffness.
- **Excessive alcohol consumption** – >2–3 drinks/day raises BP.
- **Cigarette smoking** – Causes endothelial dysfunction and accelerates arteriosclerosis; smoking + hypertension have additive vascular damage.

## Genetic Factors

Hypertensive retinopathy is not a monogenic disorder, but genetic polymorphisms influence individual susceptibility to hypertensive end-organ damage.

- **ACE gene (I/D polymorphism)** – The DD genotype (associated with higher ACE activity and angiotensin II levels) is linked to more severe hypertensive retinopathy and nephropathy in some populations.

- **AGT (angiotensinogen) gene** – Variants associated with higher angiotensinogen levels may increase retinopathy risk.

- **NOS3 (endothelial nitric oxide synthase)** – Polymorphisms affecting nitric oxide bioavailability correlate with arteriolar narrowing and endothelial dysfunction.

- **CFH (complement factor H)** – Some studies suggest CFH variants (also linked to AMD) influence susceptibility to retinal arteriolar damage, though evidence is mixed.

- **Familial aggregation** – First-degree relatives of patients with malignant hypertension have higher risk of severe retinopathy, even accounting for shared BP levels, suggesting heritable vascular reactivity.

**Clinical genetic testing** – Not recommended or available for hypertensive retinopathy; management focuses on BP control regardless of genetic profile.

## Environmental Factors

- **Chronic stress** – Persistent psychological stress raises sympathetic tone and BP; occupational stress (e.g., high-demand jobs) associated with higher retinopathy prevalence.

- **Low socioeconomic status** – Associated with poor BP control (medication access, healthcare utilization) and higher retinopathy rates.

- **Altitude** – Not a direct cause, but high altitude can exacerbate hypertensive retinopathy due to hypoxia-induced vasodilation increasing retinal perfusion pressure.

- **Seasonal variation** – BP is typically higher in winter months, which may contribute to seasonal fluctuations in retinopathy severity.

## Prevention Strategies

Because hypertensive retinopathy is a direct consequence of elevated BP, prevention focuses entirely on **blood pressure control**.

### Primary prevention (preventing retinopathy in hypertensive patients)

- **Achieve and maintain target BP** (per American Heart Association/ACC guidelines):
  - General hypertensive patients: <130/80 mmHg
  - Diabetes or CKD: <130/80 mmHg
  - Elderly (≥65 years, frail): individualize, typically <140/90

- **Regular BP monitoring** – Home BP monitoring (morning and evening) improves control. Keep a log for physician review.

- **Medication adherence** – Take antihypertensive medications as prescribed; do not skip doses even when feeling well.

- **Lifestyle modifications** (see below).

- **Annual dilated eye exams** – Detects early retinopathy (often asymptomatic), allowing reinforcement of BP control before vision loss occurs.

### Secondary prevention (preventing progression to vision-threatening disease)

- **Intensify BP control** – If retinopathy is present (especially Grade II–III), lower BP targets (e.g., <130/80) are warranted.

- **Treat comorbidities** – Optimize glycemic control (if diabetic), lipid management (statins), weight reduction.

- **More frequent eye exams** – Every 6–12 months depending on retinopathy severity.

- **Identify secondary hypertension** – Consider evaluation for renal artery stenosis, primary aldosteronism, pheochromocytoma in patients with resistant hypertension or malignant retinopathy.

- **Patient education** – Explain that retinopathy is a "warning sign" of systemic vascular damage; vision preservation is possible with BP control, but damage to other organs (heart, brain, kidneys) may have already occurred.

### Tertiary prevention (preventing vision loss from established advanced retinopathy)

- **Emergent BP reduction** – Malignant hypertension (Grade IV with disc edema) requires hospitalization for IV antihypertensives (e.g., nicardipine, labetalol) to lower BP gradually (reduce by ~25% over 8–24 hours, not to normal immediately—risk of cerebral hypoperfusion).

- **Treat macular edema** – If macular star or edema persists despite BP control, consider:
  - Anti-VEGF injections (off-label; limited evidence)
  - Focal laser photocoagulation (for chronic macular edema)
  - Intravitreal corticosteroids (rare, cautious due to BP effects)

- **Manage complications** – Branch retinal vein occlusion: consider anti-VEGF or laser; central retinal vein occlusion: anti-VEGF for macular edema.

### Modifiable lifestyle factors (for prevention and control)

- **Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH diet)** – High in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, low-fat dairy; low in saturated fat, cholesterol, and sodium. Reduces SBP by 8–14 mmHg.

- **Sodium restriction** – <1500–2300 mg/day. One teaspoon of salt = 2300 mg.

- **Regular aerobic exercise** – 150 minutes/week moderate intensity (brisk walking, cycling, swimming) reduces BP by 5–8 mmHg.

- **Weight reduction** – Each 1 kg weight loss reduces SBP by ~1 mmHg; target BMI <25.

- **Moderate alcohol** – ≤1 drink/day (women) or ≤2 drinks/day (men).

- **Smoking cessation** – Reduces arteriosclerosis progression and cardiovascular risk.

- **Stress management** – Mindfulness, meditation, yoga (adjunctive; not a substitute for medication).

**Key message**: Hypertensive retinopathy is largely preventable with good BP control. Its presence on eye exam is a call to action—not for eye treatment, but for systemic blood pressure management.

# Treatment of Hypertensive Retinopathy

## Available Treatments

**The primary treatment for hypertensive retinopathy is systemic blood pressure control.** There is no direct "eye treatment" that reverses hypertensive retinopathy independent of lowering blood pressure. Retinal changes (hemorrhages, exudates, cotton-wool spots, and disc edema) will typically resolve over weeks to months after BP is normalized, though arteriosclerotic changes (AV nicking, silver wire) are permanent.

Treatment is divided into:
1. **Systemic BP management** (core treatment)
2. **Ocular treatments for complications** (macular edema, retinal vein occlusion, neovascularization—rare)

## Medications

### Antihypertensive medications (systemic)

**No eye drops treat hypertensive retinopathy.** Oral or intravenous antihypertensives are the cornerstone.

**First-line classes for chronic hypertension** (affect retinopathy by lowering BP):

- **ACE inhibitors (e.g., lisinopril, enalapril)** – Also have reno-protective effects; reduce progression of diabetic retinopathy (by BP-dependent and possibly independent mechanisms).
- **Angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs) (e.g., losartan, valsartan)** – Similar efficacy to ACE inhibitors; fewer side effects (no cough).
- **Calcium channel blockers (e.g., amlodipine, nifedipine)** – Effective for isolated systolic hypertension and in Black/African American patients.
- **Thiazide diuretics (e.g., chlorthalidone, hydrochlorothiazide)** – First-line for uncomplicated hypertension; particularly effective in salt-sensitive patients.
- **Beta-blockers (e.g., metoprolol, carvedilol)** – Not first-line unless compelling indications (heart failure, post-myocardial infarction, arrhythmias).

**For hypertensive emergency (malignant hypertension with disc edema, Grade IV)** :

- **IV nicardipine (Clevidipine)** – Calcium channel blocker; titratable, rapid onset.
- **IV labetalol** – Combined alpha/beta-blocker; preferred in many settings.
- **IV nitroprusside** – Potent arterial vasodilator; risk of cyanide toxicity; reserved for refractory cases.
- **Goal** : Reduce mean arterial pressure by 20–25% over first 8–24 hours (NOT to normal immediately—risks cerebral ischemia). Avoid oral nifedipine (short-acting) due to risk of hypotension and stroke.

**Important**: Do not lower BP too rapidly in chronic severe hypertension (especially elderly with long-standing hypertension). Autoregulatory curves have shifted; rapid normalization can cause cerebral or retinal hypoperfusion (watershed infarcts).

### Ocular medications (for complications only)

**Anti-VEGF injections (ranibizumab, aflibercept, bevacizumab)** :

- **Indications** :
  - Macular edema from branch or central retinal vein occlusion (common complication of hypertension)
  - Diabetic macular edema in patients with comorbid hypertension and diabetes
  - Choroidal neovascularization (rare in hypertensive retinopathy alone)
- **Not indicated** for pure hypertensive retinopathy without vein occlusion or neovascularization.
- **Regimen** : Monthly to bimonthly (treat-and-extend protocol).

**Intravitreal corticosteroids (dexamethasone implant [Ozurdex], triamcinolone)** :

- **Indication** : Refractory macular edema from retinal vein occlusion after anti-VEGF failure.
- **Caveats** : Can raise intraocular pressure (glaucoma) and accelerate cataract; monitor BP (systemic absorption minimal).

**Focal/grid laser photocoagulation** :

- **Indication** : Chronic macular edema (hard exudates involving fovea) persisting >3–6 months despite BP control.
- **Procedure** : Laser burns applied to areas of leakage (not directly to fovea).
- **Evidence** : Limited; used less frequently since anti-VEGF era.

## Surgical Options

**There is no surgery for hypertensive retinopathy itself.** Surgery may be indicated for complications:

- **Pars plana vitrectomy** – For non-resolving vitreous hemorrhage from retinal vein occlusion (rare) or tractional retinal detachment.
- **Retinal vein cannulation (experimental)** – Not standard of care.
- **Optic nerve sheath fenestration** – For vision-threatening chronic papilledema (rare in malignant hypertension, more common in idiopathic intracranial hypertension).

## Lifestyle Recommendations

Essential for both prevention and management:

- **DASH diet** – High in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, low-fat dairy; low in saturated fat, cholesterol, sodium.
- **Sodium restriction** – <1500 mg/day (≈⅔ teaspoon salt). Read labels; avoid processed foods.
- **Regular aerobic exercise** – 150 minutes/week moderate-intensity (brisk walking, swimming, cycling).
- **Weight reduction** – Target BMI 18.5–24.9; even 5–10% weight loss reduces BP.
- **Alcohol moderation** – ≤1 drink/day (women), ≤2 drinks/day (men).
- **Smoking cessation** – Essential for vascular health.
- **Stress reduction** – Mindfulness, meditation, yoga (adjunctive).
- **Sleep hygiene** – Treat obstructive sleep apnea if present (CPAP lowers BP).

## Treatment Limitations

- **Irreversible changes** – Arteriosclerotic findings (AV nicking, copper/silver wire, arteriolar narrowing) do not reverse, even with perfect BP control. These are structural remodelings of the vessel wall.

- **Delayed resolution** – Even after BP normalization, hemorrhages and exudates take weeks to months to clear. Cotton-wool spots resolve over 6–12 weeks. Disc edema in malignant hypertension may take months to fully resolve.

- **Risk of permanent vision loss** – If macular edema or ischemia persists, photoreceptor damage leads to permanent central scotoma and legal blindness (especially after central retinal vein occlusion).

- **Rebound after rapid BP reduction** – In chronic severe hypertension, overly aggressive BP lowering can worsen retinal ischemia (due to shifted autoregulatory curve). This is a treatment-limiting consideration.

- **Comorbidities** – Coexisting diabetic retinopathy, glaucoma, or macular degeneration complicates management and worsens prognosis.

- **Medication side effects** – Systemic antihypertensives can cause hypotension, fatigue, renal impairment, electrolyte disturbances, and drug interactions.

- **Lack of specific ocular therapies** – Unlike diabetic retinopathy (with anti-VEGF for edema), pure hypertensive retinopathy has no FDA-approved ocular pharmacotherapy.

## Prognosis

### Retinopathy resolution after BP control

| Retinal Finding | Time to Resolution after BP Normalization | Reversibility |
|----------------|-------------------------------------------|---------------|
| Generalized arteriolar narrowing (acute) | Days to weeks (if BP controlled) | Reversible |
| Focal arteriolar constriction | Weeks | Partially reversible |
| AV nicking | Permanent (does not resolve) | Irreversible |
| Copper/silver wire arterioles | Permanent | Irreversible |
| Retinal hemorrhages | 4–12 weeks | Fully reversible |
| Hard exudates | 3–12 months; may leave residual RPE changes | Reversible (but slow) |
| Cotton-wool spots | 6–12 weeks | Reversible |
| Optic disc edema (malignant HTN) | 2–6 months | Reversible (if treated early) |
| Macular star (hard exudates) | 6–18 months; may leave persistent metamorphopsia | Partially reversible |

### Visual outcomes

**Grade I–II (no macular involvement)** :

- Vision typically remains 20/20–20/25.
- No long-term visual morbidity with BP control.

**Grade III (hemorrhages, exudates, cotton-wool spots, no disc edema)** :

- Vision usually returns to baseline after BP control if macula spared.
- If hard exudates involve fovea: vision may remain 20/30–20/60 with persistent metamorphopsia.
- 5–10% develop chronic macular edema requiring treatment.

**Grade IV (malignant hypertension with disc edema)** :

- Without treatment: 50% die within 1 year (cardiovascular, renal, cerebral events).
- With treatment: 80–90% survive; visual prognosis depends on macular involvement.
- 30–50% have persistent visual deficits (central scotoma, reduced contrast, field loss) due to macular ischemia or optic atrophy.

**Retinal vein occlusion (complication)** :

- **Branch retinal vein occlusion (BRVO)** : 50–60% achieve 20/40 or better with anti-VEGF; ~10% have permanent 20/200 or worse.
- **Central retinal vein occlusion (CRVO)** : 30–40% achieve 20/40; ~20% have permanent 20/200 or worse; ischemic CRVO has poor prognosis (no recovery of central vision).

### Systemic prognosis

- Presence of hypertensive retinopathy (especially Grade III–IV) predicts:
  - 2–4× higher risk of stroke (cerebral microvascular disease)
  - 2–3× higher risk of heart failure and left ventricular hypertrophy
  - 3–5× higher risk of chronic kidney disease progression
- Retinopathy is a **marker, not the cause**—it signals that hypertension has already damaged other organs.

**Key message**: Aggressive BP control can reverse most exudative and hemorrhagic changes, prevent vision loss, and improve systemic outcomes. However, arteriosclerotic changes are permanent reminders of past uncontrolled hypertension.

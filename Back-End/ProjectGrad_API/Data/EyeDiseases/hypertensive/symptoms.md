# Symptoms of Hypertensive Retinopathy

## Common Symptoms

**Most patients with early to moderate hypertensive retinopathy (Grades I–II, and many Grade III) have NO visual symptoms.** The condition is typically discovered incidentally on routine dilated eye examination.

When symptoms do occur, they are usually due to advanced or acute disease affecting the macula (central retina) or optic nerve.

**Mild, non-specific symptoms (occasional in Grade II–III)** :

- **Transient visual obscurations** – Brief (seconds to minutes) episodes of blurred or darkened vision, often triggered by changes in posture (standing up quickly) or blood pressure fluctuations. Caused by transient ischemia of the optic nerve or retina.

- **Mild, variable blurred vision** – Patients may notice that vision is "off and on" or worse during times of high stress or elevated blood pressure.

- **Headache (non-ocular)** – Occipital or bifrontal headache associated with high blood pressure is not directly caused by hypertensive retinopathy but reflects systemic hypertension.

- **Floaters** – Not a typical feature of hypertensive retinopathy unless hemorrhage occurs into the vitreous (rare, usually only in severe hypertension with bleeding from optic disc or retinal neovascularization—very uncommon).

## Advanced Symptoms

Symptoms develop when hypertension causes macular edema (fluid in the central retina), macular hard exudates (lipid deposition in the fovea), optic disc edema, or retinal vein occlusion.

**Moderate to severe hypertensive retinopathy (Grade III–IV, with macular involvement)** :

- **Progressive central vision blurring** – Over weeks to months, vision declines to 20/40–20/200 range. Patients report difficulty reading, recognizing faces, or watching television.

- **Metamorphopsia (visual distortion)** – Straight lines (door frames, text lines, window blinds) appear wavy or bent. This indicates macular edema or exudative detachment (macular star).

- **Central or paracentral scotoma** – A dark, gray, or hazy spot in the center or slightly off-center of vision. Caused by ischemic damage to the macula or hard exudates involving the fovea.

- **Reduced color vision (dyschromatopsia)** – Colors appear washed out or faded, particularly reds and greens. Often noted when comparing between eyes.

**Optic disc edema (papilledema) – Grade IV (malignant hypertension)** :

- **Transient visual obscurations** – Brief (seconds) episodes of complete or partial vision blackout, often occurring with changes in posture or Valsalva (coughing, straining). Pathognomonic for optic disc swelling.
- **Enlarged blind spot** – Noticed as a "missing area" in the peripheral field when tested monocularly (e.g., when closing the other eye, the patient notices a blank area in the temporal field).
- **Photopsias (flashing lights)** – Brief, spontaneous flashes of light in the peripheral vision, caused by mechanical stimulation of the optic nerve.
- **Progressive peripheral vision loss** – If disc edema persists, peripheral field constriction occurs. Advanced papilledema can cause severe, permanent vision loss.

**Complications (retinal vein occlusion)** :

- **Sudden, painless vision loss** – Over hours to days, vision drops to counting fingers or worse (20/200 to hand motion).
- **Dense central scotoma** – Central retinal vein occlusion (CRVO) causes severe central vision loss with relative sparing of peripheral field.
- **Sectoral field loss** – Branch retinal vein occlusion (BRVO) causes a wedge-shaped (altitudinal) field defect corresponding to the affected quadrant.

**Macular star (macular exudates)** :

- **Distinctive appearance** – Patients may not describe specific symptoms, but examination reveals star-like pattern of hard exudates radiating from the fovea. Vision is typically 20/50–20/200 with metamorphopsia.

**Chronic, irreversible changes** :

- **Permanent central scotoma** – From chronic macular ischemia or foveal atrophy.
- **Legal blindness (20/200 or worse)** – Occurs in a minority of patients with untreated malignant hypertension or recurrent retinal vein occlusions.

## When to Seek Medical Attention

**Routine (schedule eye exam within weeks)** :

- Known hypertensive patient who has not had a dilated eye exam in the past 1–2 years.
- New diagnosis of hypertension—baseline retinal exam recommended.
- Gradual vision changes (blurring, distortion, difficulty reading) developing over weeks to months.

**Urgent (same day or within 24–48 hours)** :

- **Sudden, painless vision loss** in one eye (or both)—possible retinal vein occlusion, arterial occlusion, or optic nerve damage.
- **New-onset metamorphopsia** (wavy vision)—suggests macular edema from malignant hypertension.
- **Flashes of light or new floaters**—though less common in pure HR, these can indicate posterior vitreous detachment or retinal tear.

**Emergency (go to emergency department immediately)** :

- **Optic disc edema with very high blood pressure** (e.g., >180/120) plus vision changes. This is malignant hypertension with end-organ damage; requires hospital admission for IV blood pressure reduction.
- **Severe headache, confusion, chest pain, or shortness of breath** accompanying vision changes—these indicate hypertensive emergency affecting the brain or heart, not just the eye.

**Note**: The eye examination itself is not an emergency, but the underlying blood pressure may be life-threatening. If you have very high blood pressure readings (systolic >180 or diastolic >110) AND new vision changes, go to an emergency department—not just an ophthalmology clinic.

## Symptoms That May Affect Daily Activities

| Activity | Potential Symptom Impact (Advanced HR Only) |
|----------|---------------------------------------------|
| Reading | Central blur, metamorphosis, or scotoma from macular edema or hard exudates makes text unreadable; need for magnification or large print |
| Driving | Sudden vision loss from retinal vein occlusion may make driving permanently impossible; central scotoma creates blind spot for road signs and pedestrians |
| Recognizing faces | Macular involvement (edema, exudates) distorts or blocks central vision of facial features |
| Watching television | Central blur prevents seeing details (subtitles, scores, expressions) |
| Computer work | Wavy text (metamorphopsia) makes reading on screens difficult; scotoma covers icons or cursor |
| Walking on uneven ground | Reduced contrast sensitivity and central scotoma increase fall risk (late stages) |
| Taking medications | Difficulty reading small-print labels if central vision is compromised |
| Occupational tasks | Driving-based jobs (trucking, taxi), fine visual work (surgeon, machinist, artist) may be affected in advanced disease |
| Nighttime activities | Reduced contrast sensitivity and prolonged dark adaptation (from chronic ischemic changes) |

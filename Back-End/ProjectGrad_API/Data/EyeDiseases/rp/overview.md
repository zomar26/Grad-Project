# Retinitis Pigmentosa

## Definition

Retinitis pigmentosa (RP) is a group of inherited retinal disorders characterized by progressive degeneration of the photoreceptors (rod and cone cells) and the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE), leading to gradual vision loss. The term "retinitis" is a historical misnomer, as the condition is not primarily inflammatory; "retinopathy" would be more accurate, but the name persists.

RP is the most common inherited retinal dystrophy and a leading cause of blindness in working-age adults worldwide. The disease typically affects rods first (causing night blindness and peripheral vision loss), followed by cones (central vision loss). The rate of progression varies significantly depending on the specific genetic mutation and other modifying factors.

## How the Eye is Affected

**Primary structures involved**: Photoreceptors (rods and cones), retinal pigment epithelium (RPE), and ultimately the entire retina, choroid, and optic nerve.

**Mechanism of degeneration** (varies by genetic cause, but common pathways include):

1. **Photoreceptor dysfunction and death**: Mutations in genes essential for phototransduction, vitamin A metabolism, ciliary structure, or cellular trafficking lead to rod and cone cell death. Rods are typically affected first, explaining early night blindness.

2. **RPE atrophy**: As photoreceptors die, the underlying RPE degenerates secondarily, visible as “bone-spicule” pigmentation (migrated RPE cells clumping around blood vessels).

3. **Retinal remodeling**: Inner retinal neurons (bipolar, horizontal, amacrine, and ganglion cells) undergo reactive changes, including neurite sprouting, synapse reorganization, and eventually cell death. This remodeling limits the effectiveness of late-stage gene therapy or cell replacement.

4. **Choroidal thinning**: Reduced metabolic demand from photoreceptor loss leads to thinning of the underlying choroid.

5. **Optic nerve atrophy**: Ganglion cell loss results in pallor of the optic disc.

**Distinctive features on examination**:

- **Bone-spicule pigmentation**: Clumps of melanin in the mid-peripheral retina, classically along blood vessels.
- **Waxy optic disc pallor**: Atrophy of the optic nerve head.
- **Arteriolar attenuation**: Narrowing of retinal arteries.
- **Cystoid macular edema (CME)**: Fluid accumulation in the macula, affecting 10–30% of RP patients, causing additional central vision loss.
- **Posterior subcapsular cataract (PSC)**: Common in RP, often at a younger age than the general population.

## Types or Stages

### By Mode of Inheritance

| Inheritance | Proportion | Typical Onset | Progression | Key Features |
|-------------|-----------|---------------|-------------|--------------|
| **Autosomal dominant (adRP)** | 30–40% | Variable (20–40 years) | Slower, more variable | Often milder; later blindness; linked to rhodopsin, PRPF31, RP1 genes |
| **Autosomal recessive (arRP)** | 50–60% | Early (childhood–20s) | Moderate to severe | Most common worldwide; associated with USH2A, PDE6B, CRB1, MERTK |
| **X-linked (XLRP)** | 5–15% | Earliest (childhood) | Most severe | Rapid progression; early blindness by age 30–40; RPGR (70–80%) and RP2 genes |
| **Digenic/oligogenic** | Rare | Variable | Variable | Two or more genes involved |
| **Mitochondrial** | Extremely rare | Variable | Variable | Rare |

### By Clinical Staging (Functional)

| Stage | Night Blindness | Visual Field | Visual Acuity | Fundus Findings |
|-------|----------------|--------------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Early** | Present (childhood–teens) | Normal or mild peripheral constriction | 20/20–20/25 | Normal or mild vessel attenuation, few bone spicules |
| **Moderate** | Severe | Tunnel vision (10–30° field) | 20/25–20/50 | Bone spicules mid-periphery, arteriolar attenuation, waxy disc |
| **Advanced** | Absolute | <10° field | 20/50–20/200 | Extensive bone spicules, disc pallor, arteriolar thinning, macular atrophy |
| **End-stage** | Absolute | <5° or light perception only | Hand motion to NLP | Complete retinal atrophy, optic nerve pallor, absent vessels |

### By Syndromic Association (RP plus systemic features)

- **Usher syndrome** (RP + hearing loss): Most common syndromic RP (30–50% of arRP). Types 1, 2, and 3 (differing in hearing and vestibular severity).
- **Bardet-Biedl syndrome** (RP + obesity, polydactyly, renal anomalies, hypogonadism, learning disability).
- **Alström syndrome** (RP + childhood obesity, dilated cardiomyopathy, sensorineural hearing loss, type 2 diabetes).
- **Other rare syndromes**: Refsum disease (phytanic acid accumulation), Kearns-Sayre syndrome (mitochondrial), abetalipoproteinemia (Bassen-Kornzweig), and others.

### By Genetic Subtype (Emerging)

With next-generation sequencing, over 80 RP-causing genes have been identified. Major categories:

- **Phototransduction cascade genes**: Rhodopsin (RHO), PDE6A, PDE6B, CNGA1, CNGB1.
- **Ciliary/transport genes**: RPGR (X-linked), RP2 (X-linked), RP1, RPGRIP1, CEP290.
- **Splicing factor genes**: PRPF3, PRPF8, PRPF31, SNRNP200.
- **Retinoid cycle genes**: RPE65, LRAT, RBP3.
- **Photoreceptor structure genes**: CRB1, PRPH2, ROM1.
- **Outer segment phagocytosis genes**: MERTK.

## Key Facts

- **Prevalence**: 1 in 3,000–4,000 people worldwide (approximately 2–3 million affected globally). Carrier frequency ~1 in 100 for autosomal recessive forms.

- **Age of onset**: Typically childhood to early adulthood (night blindness often noticed by age 10). However, some forms (e.g., dominant RP) may present later (age 20–40).

- **Progression rate**: Highly variable, even within families. X-linked RP is most severe (legal blindness by age 30–40), dominant RP often slowest (blindness by age 50–70).

- **Bilateral and symmetric**: RP almost always affects both eyes symmetrically, though progression may vary slightly.

- **No cure exists**, but treatments are emerging: gene therapy (Luxturna® for RPE65 mutations), retinal implants (Argus II, discontinued), stem cell trials, and neuroprotective agents (investigational).

- **Vitamin A controversy**: High-dose vitamin A palmitate (15,000 IU/day) was suggested to slow progression based on one randomized trial (Berson et al., 1993). However, subsequent studies raised concerns about long-term hepatotoxicity, and the AAO no longer recommends routine supplementation. Do not take high-dose vitamin A without medical supervision.

- **Cystoid macular edema (CME) occurs in 10–30%**: May be treatable with topical dorzolamide or oral acetazolamide, sometimes improving central vision.

- **Cataracts are common**: Posterior subcapsular cataracts develop earlier than in the general population (often 30s–50s). Cataract surgery can improve vision but may have higher complication rates (capsular rupture, CME exacerbation).

- **Hearing loss should be screened**: Usher syndrome accounts for 30–50% of autosomal recessive RP. Early diagnosis allows supportive services.

- **Genetic testing is recommended**: Identifies the causative gene, enabling:
    - Confirmation of diagnosis
    - Eligibility for gene-specific therapies (e.g., RPE65)
    - Prognostic information (severity, progression)
    - Family counseling and prenatal/preimplantation genetic diagnosis

- **Low vision rehabilitation is essential**: Maximizes remaining vision; benefits far exceed any medical treatment currently available.

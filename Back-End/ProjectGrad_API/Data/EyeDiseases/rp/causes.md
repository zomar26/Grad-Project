# Causes and Risk Factors of Retinitis Pigmentosa

## Main Causes

Retinitis pigmentosa is a **genetic disorder** caused by mutations in over 80 different genes. The unifying pathophysiologic mechanism is **progressive photoreceptor cell death** (primarily rods initially, then secondarily cones), triggered by the specific genetic defect.

**Key molecular pathways affected** (by gene category):

1. **Phototransduction cascade** – Mutations in rhodopsin (RHO), PDE6A, PDE6B, CNGA1, CNGB1 disrupt the conversion of light into electrical signals, leading to rod dysfunction and death.

2. **Ciliary structure and transport** – Mutations in RPGR (X-linked), RP2, CEP290, RPGRIP1, and others affect the connecting cilium of photoreceptors, disrupting protein transport from inner to outer segments.

3. **Splicing factor genes** – Mutations in PRPF3, PRPF8, PRPF31, SNRNP200 affect pre-mRNA splicing, leading to generalized cellular dysfunction (mechanism unclear but retina-specific).

4. **Retinoid (vitamin A) cycle** – Mutations in RPE65, LRAT, RBP3 impair the recycling of chromophore (11-cis-retinal), essential for visual pigment regeneration.

5. **Photoreceptor structural genes** – Mutations in CRB1, PRPH2, ROM1 affect outer segment structure and disc morphogenesis.

6. **Phagocytosis** – Mutations in MERTK disrupt the RPE's ability to engulf shed outer segments, leading to toxic debris accumulation.

7. **Transcription factors** – Mutations in NR2E3, NRL, CRX alter photoreceptor-specific gene expression.

**Consequences of photoreceptor death**:

- Rod death → night blindness, peripheral field loss.
- Cone death (secondary to rod loss or direct gene effect) → central vision loss, color vision deficits.
- Retinal remodeling → inner retinal neurons undergo reactive changes, limiting potential for late-stage therapies.

## Risk Factors

**Primary risk factor**: **Family history of RP** (genetic inheritance). There are no lifestyle or environmental risk factors for developing RP.

**By inheritance pattern**:

- **Autosomal dominant RP (30–40%)** – Each child of an affected parent has a 50% risk of inheriting the mutation. Both sexes equally affected.
- **Autosomal recessive RP (50–60%)** – Parents are unaffected carriers (25% risk per child). Consanguinity (parents related by blood) increases risk.
- **X-linked RP (5–15%)** – Affects males almost exclusively; carrier females are usually asymptomatic but may have mild retinal changes. All daughters of affected males are carriers; sons of carrier females have 50% risk.

**Other risk factors for progression** (modifiers):

- **Nutritional status** – Severe vitamin A deficiency exacerbates RP (rare in developed countries). High-dose vitamin A supplements were controversially suggested to slow progression (see Prevention).
- **Smoking** – Smoking increases oxidative stress and may accelerate cone degeneration; most specialists advise cessation.
- **Sunlight exposure** – Some studies suggest bright sunlight exposure may accelerate macular degeneration in RP; wearing UV-blocking sunglasses is prudent.
- **Poorly controlled systemic disease** – Diabetes, hypertension (general vascular health) may indirectly affect retinal health.

**No other environmental or behavioral risk factors** are known to cause or prevent RP.

## Genetic Factors

**Over 80 RP-causing genes** have been identified. The most common mutations vary by population:

| Gene | Inheritance | Frequency (All RP) | Typical Onset | Severity |
|------|-------------|--------------------|---------------|----------|
| **RHO** (rhodopsin) | AD (rarely AR) | 10–15% of ADRP | Variable (childhood–adult) | Moderate |
| **USH2A** | AR | 20–30% of arRP (also Usher type 2) | Childhood | Moderate–severe |
| **RPGR** | X-linked | 70–80% of XLRP | Early childhood | Severe (blind by 30–40) |
| **PRPF31** | AD | 5–10% of ADRP | Adult (20–40) | Variable |
| **RP1** | AD (rarely AR) | 5% of ADRP | Adult | Moderate |
| **PDE6B** | AR | 5–10% of arRP | Childhood | Moderate–severe |
| **CRB1** | AR | 5% of arRP | Early childhood | Severe (often early macular involvement) |
| **RPE65** | AR | 1–2% of arRP | Early childhood | Severe (gene therapy available) |
| **MERTK** | AR | <1% | Early childhood | Severe |

**Genetic testing**:

- **Panel-based testing**: Targeted sequencing of known RP genes (most common, cost-effective, 60–80% detection rate).
- **Whole exome sequencing (WES)** : For negative panel results (additional 10–15% detection).
- **Whole genome sequencing (WGS)** : For negative exome (detects deep intronic, structural variants).

**Benefits of genetic testing**:
- Confirms diagnosis (differentiates RP from other retinopathies).
- Provides prognostic information (e.g., XLRP = severe, early blindness).
- Determines eligibility for gene-specific therapies (RPE65, others in trials).
- Guides family planning (carrier testing, prenatal/preimplantation diagnosis).
- Identifies syndromic forms (e.g., Usher syndrome → hearing screening).

**Carrier frequency**: For autosomal recessive RP, carrier frequency is ~1 in 100 in most populations, but varies by gene (e.g., USH2A carrier frequency 1 in 120–200).

## Environmental Factors

**None known** to cause RP. However, certain exposures may accelerate degeneration:

- **Smoking** – Increases oxidative stress; may accelerate cone loss.
- **High-dose vitamin A (controversial)** – One 1993 trial suggested 15,000 IU/day slowed ERG decline, but subsequent studies raised hepatotoxicity concerns. AAO no longer recommends routine supplementation. If taken, monitor liver function.
- **Sunlight** – Theoretical risk; wearing UV-blocking sunglasses is prudent.

**Protective factors** (possible, not proven):
- **Omega-3 fatty acids** – One trial showed benefit of 1200 mg/day DHA on ERG; confirmatory trials lacking.
- **Lutein/zeaxanthin** – Inconclusive.

## Prevention Strategies

### Primary prevention (preventing inheritance)

- **Genetic counseling** – For families with RP, discuss inheritance risks, carrier testing, and reproductive options.
- **Preimplantation genetic testing (PGT)** – Embryos created via IVF can be tested for the familial mutation before implantation.
- **Prenatal diagnosis** – Chorionic villus sampling or amniocentesis determines if a fetus carries the mutation.
- **Carrier screening** – For at-risk relatives (e.g., sisters of XLRP probands).

### Secondary prevention (slowing progression) – Emerging

**No proven therapy** slows RP progression. The following are **investigational or controversial**:

- **Vitamin A palmitate (15,000 IU/day)** – Based on a single 1993 randomized trial (Berson et al.) showing 20% slower decline in ERG amplitude. **Current AAO guideline** (2013): Does not recommend routine supplementation due to:
    - Hepatotoxicity risk (especially if combined with other liver-toxic drugs)
    - Teratogenicity (birth defects if taken in pregnancy)
    - Inconsistent evidence (no confirmatory large trial)
    - Lack of patient-centered outcomes (no effect on visual acuity or fields in the trial)
- **Do NOT take high-dose vitamin A without medical supervision**. If prescribed, monitor liver enzymes every 6 months.

- **Docosahexaenoic acid (DHA) (1200 mg/day)** – One trial showed slower decline in ERG; another found no benefit. Not routinely recommended.

- **Lutein/zeaxanthin** – Inconclusive.

- **Valproic acid** – An open-label study suggested benefit, but a subsequent randomized trial showed no effect and potential harm (retinal toxicity). **Not recommended**.

- **Acetazolamide or dorzolamide** – For **cystoid macular edema** (CME), not for slowing progression. May improve central vision if CME is present.

- **Anti-VEGF injections** – For CME in RP (off-label, limited evidence).

### Tertiary prevention (maximizing remaining vision)

- **Low vision rehabilitation** – Single most effective intervention for quality of life.
- **Regular monitoring** – Every 6–12 months to detect treatable complications (CME, cataracts).
- **Cataract surgery** – Improves vision if cataract is visually significant; risks include CME exacerbation, capsular rupture.
- **Gene therapy** – For RPE65 mutations (Luxturna®) can improve light sensitivity and visual field; not a cure but may slow progression.
- **Retinal implant (Argus II)** – Discontinued; no longer available.
- **Clinical trials** – Several gene-specific therapies (e.g., RPGR, USH2A, RHO) in Phase I–III trials; discuss with retina specialist.

**Key message**: RP is a genetic disease; you did nothing to cause it. Prevention focuses on genetic counseling for family planning and managing complications (CME, cataracts). No proven medical therapy slows progression, but low vision rehabilitation is transformative. Do not take high-dose vitamin A without medical oversight.

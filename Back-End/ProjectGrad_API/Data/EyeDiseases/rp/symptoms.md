# Symptoms of Retinitis Pigmentosa

## Common Symptoms

The classic triad of RP symptoms is: **night blindness, peripheral vision loss, and eventual central vision loss**. Onset is typically in childhood or adolescence, though some forms present later.

### Early Symptoms (Childhood – Teens)

- **Night blindness (nyctalopia)** – The earliest and most universal symptom. Patients report:
    - Difficulty seeing in dim lighting (e.g., navigating a movie theater, walking from a brightly lit room into a dark hallway)
    - Trouble playing outdoor games at dusk or after dark
    - Prolonged dark adaptation (taking >30 minutes to adjust after coming indoors from sunlight)
    - Bumping into furniture or walls at night
    - Fear of going out after sunset
- **Often noticed by age 10** in X-linked and autosomal recessive RP; may be later (20s–30s) in dominant forms.

- **Peripheral vision loss (constriction)** – Begins as a **ring scotoma** (blind area in the mid-periphery, sparing central and far peripheral vision). Patients may not notice initially but will:
    - Bump into door frames, furniture, or people on their sides
    - Trip over objects not directly in front of them
    - Seem unaware of people approaching from the side
    - Have difficulty with sports requiring wide field awareness (basketball, soccer)
    - Knock over drinks placed to the side

- **Difficulty with dark adaptation** – Prolonged time to recover vision after exposure to bright light (e.g., walking indoors on a sunny day, recovering after a camera flash).

### Moderate Symptoms (Adolescence – Young Adulthood)

- **Tunnel vision (concentric field constriction)** – Progressive narrowing of the visual field to a small central island of vision (10–30° diameter). Patients describe seeing "as if looking through a paper towel tube" or "a narrow straw."

- **Difficulty with mobility in low-contrast situations** – Steps, curbs, uneven pavement, and dark clothing against dark backgrounds become hard to distinguish, even in good light.

- **Glare and light sensitivity (photophobia)** – Some patients (especially those with cone involvement) experience discomfort in bright light, paradoxically worsening as cones degenerate.

- **Color vision changes (dyschromatopsia)** – Later symptom as cones degenerate; typically blue-yellow deficits first, followed by red-green.

- **Blurred or reduced central vision** – Begins as cones are affected (usually after decades of rod loss). Early central vision loss may be asymmetric or patchy.

### Advanced Symptoms (Adulthood, Age 30–60)

- **Legal blindness** – Visual acuity of 20/200 or worse, or visual field constricted to <20° diameter. Timing depends on inheritance: X-linked RP often by age 30–40; autosomal recessive by 40–50; autosomal dominant by 50–70.

- **Dense central scotoma** – Complete loss of central vision in end-stage disease (like looking through a pinhole or missing the center of a face). Patients rely on eccentric viewing (using remaining peripheral islands).

- **Complete loss of peripheral light perception** – End-stage patients may have only a small central island (1–5°) or light perception only. Complete blindness (no light perception) occurs in <10% of RP patients.

- **Cystoid macular edema (CME)** – Fluid accumulation in the macula affects 10–30% of RP patients, causing additional central blur and metamorphopsia (waviness). CME can occur at any stage and may fluctuate.

- **Posterior subcapsular cataract (PSC)** – Develops earlier (30s–50s) than in the general population, causing glare, halos, and near vision loss (in addition to RP symptoms).

- **Epiretinal membrane (ERM)** – Scar tissue on the macula causing distortion.

- **Optic disc edema (rare)** – In some syndromic forms (e.g., Usher syndrome) or advanced RP.

### Syndromic RP – Additional Symptoms (Beyond the Eye)

- **Usher syndrome**: Sensorineural hearing loss (congenital or progressive) and vestibular dysfunction (balance problems).
- **Bardet-Biedl syndrome**: Obesity, polydactyly (extra digits), renal abnormalities, hypogonadism, learning disability.
- **Alström syndrome**: Childhood obesity, dilated cardiomyopathy, type 2 diabetes, hearing loss.
- **Refsum disease**: Peripheral neuropathy, cerebellar ataxia, elevated phytanic acid (dietary restriction treats).
- **Mitochondrial disorders**: Progressive external ophthalmoplegia (Kearns-Sayre), muscle weakness.

## When to Seek Medical Attention

**Routine (schedule eye exam within weeks to months)** :

- **New difficulty with night vision** – Especially in a child or young adult, warrants comprehensive eye examination, including electroretinography (ERG).
- **Progressive peripheral vision loss** – Bumping into objects, tripping, or difficulty with side vision.
- **Family history of RP** – Even if asymptomatic, baseline examination and genetic counseling recommended.
- **New onset of central blur or metamorphopsia** – Possible CME, which may be treatable.
- **Annual monitoring** – For patients with confirmed RP (every 6–12 months for ERG, visual field, OCT).

**Urgent (same day or within 24–48 hours)** – These symptoms are **not typical of uncomplicated RP** and warrant urgent evaluation:

- **Sudden worsening of central vision** over days (not months or years) – Possible new CME, cataract progression, or macular hole.
- **New metamorphopsia** (wavy distortion) – Suggests CME, ERM, or macular hole.
- **Sudden increase in floaters or flashes of light** – Possible retinal tear or detachment (RP patients have higher risk of retinal detachment due to lattice degeneration).
- **A dark curtain or shadow** across vision – Possible retinal detachment.

**Emergency (go to emergency department immediately)** :

- Sudden, painless vision loss with a "curtain" over vision – Retinal detachment.
- Eye pain with redness and vision loss – Unusual for RP; suggests uveitis, acute glaucoma, or infection.

## Symptoms That May Affect Daily Activities

| Activity | Symptom Impact |
|----------|----------------|
| Night driving | Profound difficulty or impossible due to night blindness; most patients stop night driving by early adulthood |
| Daytime driving | Peripheral field loss (tunnel vision) makes it impossible to see vehicles approaching from side streets or pedestrians stepping from curbs; may meet legal criteria for driving cessation |
| Reading | Preserved until late stages; eventual need for magnification, large print, or audio (if central vision lost) |
| Recognizing faces | Difficult in advanced stages (central scotoma); patients may recognize by voice, gait, or clothing |
| Walking in dim areas | Hallways, stairwells, parking garages, and outdoor areas at dusk become hazardous |
| Navigating crowds | Inability to see people approaching from sides leads to collisions or social anxiety |
| Sports and recreation | Difficulty tracking balls, seeing teammates/opponents, or avoiding obstacles |
| Walking on uneven ground | Poor contrast sensitivity and field loss increase fall risk, especially on stairs, curbs, or loose gravel |
| Cooking | Peripheral loss increases risk of burns, cuts, or spills from unseen hazards at counter edges |
| Taking medications | Difficulty reading small-print labels if central vision is compromised (late stage) |
| Hobbies (sewing, woodworking, painting) | Fine detail work impossible with central vision loss; field loss creates hazard with tools |
| Using digital devices | Large font and high contrast needed if central vision is compromised |
| Social situations | Tunnel vision may make patient appear "rude" (not acknowledging people at the side); anxiety about bumping into things or people |
| Occupational tasks | Driving-based jobs (trucking, taxi) impossible; surgeons, pilots, machinists, artists affected when central vision declines |

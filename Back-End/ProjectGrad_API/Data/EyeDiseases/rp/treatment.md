# Treatment of Retinitis Pigmentosa

## Available Treatments

**There is no cure for retinitis pigmentosa**, and no FDA-approved medical therapy (other than gene therapy for a tiny subset) slows or halts progression. Treatment focuses on:

1. **Managing complications** – Cystoid macular edema (CME), cataracts, and refractive error.
2. **Maximizing remaining vision** – Low vision rehabilitation, assistive technology.
3. **Slowing progression (investigational)** – Gene therapy for specific mutations, neuroprotective agents in trials.
4. **Addressing syndromic features** – Hearing aids (Usher), metabolic management (Refsum disease).

## Medications

### For Cystoid Macular Edema (CME) – 10–30% of RP patients

CME causes additional central vision loss and metamorphopsia that may be reversible.

- **Topical dorzolamide 2% (Trusopt®)** – Carbonic anhydrase inhibitor. First-line. Improves CME and visual acuity in 50–70% of RP patients. Onset: 2–8 weeks. Side effects: bitter taste, local irritation, rare corneal edema.
- **Topical brinzolamide 1% (Azopt®)** – Alternative to dorzolamide (slightly better tolerated).
- **Oral acetazolamide (Diamox®)** – For refractory CME or patients who cannot tolerate topical drops. Dose: 250–500 mg/day. Side effects: paresthesias, fatigue, metallic taste, kidney stones, metabolic acidosis.
- **Intravitreal anti-VEGF (ranibizumab, aflibercept)** – Off-label for CME in RP. Limited evidence; may be used for refractory cases.
- **Intravitreal corticosteroids (triamcinolone, dexamethasone implant)** – Off-label; effective for CME but risk of glaucoma and cataract.

**Note**: Treating CME does not slow the underlying RP progression, but it may improve central vision.

### For Slowing Progression (Controversial/Investigational)

- **High-dose vitamin A palmitate (15,000 IU/day)** – Based on one 1993 trial (Berson) showing 20% slower decline in ERG amplitude. **Current AAO position (2013)**: Does **not** recommend routine supplementation due to:
    - Hepatotoxicity risk (liver damage)
    - Teratogenicity (birth defects)
    - No confirmatory large trial
    - No proven benefit on visual acuity or fields
- **If you take vitamin A** (only with retina specialist approval):
    - Take **vitamin A palmitate** (not beta-carotene)
    - Avoid **high-dose vitamin E** (>400 IU/day) – may antagonize vitamin A
    - Avoid **vitamin A + isotretinoin** (severe liver toxicity)
    - Monitor liver enzymes (ALT, AST) every 6 months
    - Avoid if pregnant or considering pregnancy
- **Do not take vitamin A** if you have liver disease, are on hepatotoxic drugs, or are pregnant.

- **Docosahexaenoic acid (DHA) (1200 mg/day)** – One trial showed slower ERG decline, another found no benefit. Not routinely recommended.

- **Lutein/zeaxanthin** – No proven benefit.

- **Valproic acid** – A 2018 randomized trial showed no benefit and potential retinal toxicity. **Not recommended**.

- **Other investigational agents** (clinical trials only):
    - N-acetylcysteine (NAC) – Antioxidant
    - Ciliary neurotrophic factor (CNTF) – Encapsulated cell implant (phase 3 completed, mixed results)
    - Photobiomodulation (low-level red light) – Phase 2
    - Various gene therapies (see below)

## Surgical Options

### Cataract Surgery

- **Indication**: Visually significant posterior subcapsular cataract (common in RP, often ages 30–50).
- **Benefits**: Improves vision (can be dramatic), reduces glare.
- **Risks** (higher than in non-RP patients):
    - Posterior capsule opacification (PCO) – very common (50–80%)
    - Cystoid macular edema (CME) exacerbation (20–30%)
    - Capsular rupture (weakened zonules in some syndromic RP)
    - Retinal detachment (higher baseline risk)
- **Technique**: Consider combined phacoemulsification + IOL implantation. Use of intraoperative NSAIDs and postoperative steroids may reduce CME risk.
- **YAG laser capsulotomy** – When PCO occurs, performed as outpatient (5 minutes). Risk of CME, retinal detachment.

### Vitrectomy for Macular Hole or ERM

- **Indication**: Epiretinal membrane (ERM) or macular hole (rare in RP, but occurs).
- **Risks**: High due to retinal fragility and possible exacerbation of RP progression? Limited data.

### Gene Therapy (Luxturna® – voretigene neparvovec-rzyl)

- **Indication**: Biallelic RPE65 mutation-associated retinal dystrophy (a specific form of RP or Leber congenital amaurosis).
- **Eligibility**: Genetic confirmation of RPE65 mutations, viable retina on OCT (some remaining photoreceptors).
- **Procedure**: Subretinal injection of AAV2 vector carrying functional RPE65 gene under general anesthesia (one eye at a time).
- **Outcomes** (Phase 3 trial):
    - Improved light sensitivity (multi-luminance mobility test)
    - Improved visual field (Goldmann perimetry)
    - Stable or improved full-field light sensitivity threshold
    - Variable improvement in visual acuity (some patients gain, others stable)
- **Limitations**:
    - Only for RPE65 mutations (~1–2% of arRP)
    - Not a cure; slows progression but does not reverse severe vision loss
    - Requires viable photoreceptors (advanced disease not eligible)
    - Expensive (~$850,000 for both eyes)
    - Durability unknown (follow-up ~4 years shows sustained effect)
- **Availability**: FDA-approved (2017), covered by many insurance plans (prior authorization required).

### Other Gene Therapies (Investigational – Clinical Trials)

- **RPGR gene therapy** (XLRP) – Phase 1/2 trials ongoing (AAV8-RPGR, 4D-110, etc.). Early results show safety and possible improvement.
- **USH2A antisense oligonucleotide** – Phase 1/2 (sepofarsen for CEP290, others for USH2A).
- **RHO gene therapy** – CRISPR-based or antisense for dominant negative mutations (Phase 1/2).
- **MERTK gene therapy** – Phase 1 completed.

### Retinal Implants (Discontinued)

- **Argus II** (bionic eye) – Discontinued by manufacturer (2020). No longer available.
- **Other epiretinal, subretinal, or suprachoroidal implants** – Experimental, no commercial availability.

### Stem Cell Therapy (Investigational, Not Approved)

- **Embryonic stem cell-derived RPE or photoreceptor precursors** – Phase 1/2 trials for RP and AMD. Safety demonstrated in some trials; efficacy not proven.
- **Autologous bone marrow-derived stem cells (unproven)** – Do not pay for unregulated "stem cell clinics" (risk of severe complications, no proven benefit).

## Lifestyle Recommendations

- **UV-protective sunglasses** – May slow macular degeneration in RP (theoretical benefit).
- **Smoking cessation** – Reduces oxidative stress; strongly recommended.
- **Healthy diet** – Rich in antioxidants (fruits, vegetables, leafy greens), omega-3 fatty acids (fish).
- **Avoid high-dose vitamin E** (>400 IU/day) – May antagonize vitamin A (if taking).
- **Vitamin A** – Only if prescribed by retina specialist, with liver enzyme monitoring.
- **Avoid drugs that cause retinal toxicity** – Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil) is relatively contraindicated in RP; deferoxamine, thioridazine, tamoxifen may worsen degeneration.
- **Low vision rehabilitation** – Essential; includes:
    - Orientation and mobility training (white cane, guide dog)
    - Assistive technology training (screen readers, magnification)
    - Eccentric viewing techniques
    - Occupational therapy for daily living skills

## Treatment Limitations

- **No cure or proven therapy to halt progression** (except gene therapy for RPE65, which affects <2%).
- **RPE65 gene therapy** – Limited to specific mutation; does not restore normal vision; requires early intervention; durability unknown.
- **CME treatment** – Improves central vision but does not slow underlying RP progression.
- **Cataract surgery** – Risks higher than in non-RP patients (CME, PCO, capsular rupture).
- **Low vision rehabilitation** – Does not restore vision; maximizes existing vision but requires adaptation.
- **Unregulated stem cell clinics** – Dangerous; report severe vision loss, retinal detachment, orbital inflammation, tumor formation. **Never pay for unapproved stem cell treatments**.

## Prognosis

### Natural History (by Inheritance)

| Inheritance | Typical Onset (nyctalopia) | Legal Blindness Age | Rate of Progression |
|-------------|----------------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **X-linked (XL)** | Early childhood (5–10 years) | 30–40 years | Rapid, severe |
| **Autosomal recessive (ar)** | Late childhood (10–15 years) | 40–50 years | Moderate to severe |
| **Autosomal dominant (ad)** | Variable (10–40 years) | 50–70 years | Slow to moderate |
| **RPE65 mutations** | Early childhood (LCA/RP) | 20–40 years | Severe (but gene therapy may help) |

### Functional Progression (Typical Timeline)

- **Night blindness** – Often by age 10.
- **Peripheral field constriction** – Noticeable by late teens to 20s.
- **Tunnel vision (<20° field)** – By 30–40s (XL), 40–50s (ar), 50–60s (ad).
- **Legal blindness (20/200 or <20° field)** – By 30–40 (XL), 40–50 (ar), 50–70 (ad).
- **Complete blindness (NLP)** – Uncommon (<10%); most maintain light perception.

### Factors Associated with Better Prognosis

- **Autosomal dominant inheritance** (slowest).
- **Preserved central vision** into 60s–70s (some dominant forms).
- **No cystoid macular edema** (preserves central vision).
- **No syndromic features** (Usher, Bardet-Biedl have additional disabilities but not necessarily faster retinal degeneration).

### Factors Associated with Worse Prognosis

- **X-linked inheritance** (most severe, earliest blindness).
- **Early macular involvement** (e.g., CRB1 mutations).
- **Cystoid macular edema** (contributes additional vision loss).
- **Poorly controlled systemic disease** (diabetes, hypertension – not proven but plausible).

**Key message**: RP is a relentlessly progressive disease with no cure, but the rate varies widely. Most patients retain some useful vision (light perception or peripheral islands) until late in life, rarely becoming totally blind. Gene therapy offers hope for a small subset; many other gene-specific and neuroprotective trials are ongoing. Low vision rehabilitation is the most impactful intervention available today.

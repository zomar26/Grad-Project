# Choroideremia

## Definition

Choroideremia is a rare, X-linked recessive inherited retinal disorder characterized by progressive degeneration of the choroid, retinal pigment epithelium (RPE), and photoreceptors. The condition leads to progressive vision loss, beginning with night blindness in childhood and advancing to legal blindness typically by late adulthood (fifth to sixth decade of life) [citation:2][citation:4].

First described by Ludwig Mauthner in 1872, choroideremia affects an estimated 1 in 50,000 to 100,000 males worldwide [citation:1][citation:9]. The disease is caused by mutations in the CHM gene, which encodes Rab escort protein-1 (REP-1), a protein essential for intracellular trafficking within retinal cells [citation:2].

## How the Eye is Affected

Choroideremia causes progressive atrophy (cell death and tissue wasting) of three interconnected layers at the back of the eye:

**1. Choroid** – A vascular layer between the retina and sclera that provides oxygen and nutrients to the outer retina. In choroideremia, the choroid progressively degenerates and eventually disappears, exposing the underlying white sclera.

**2. Retinal Pigment Epithelium (RPE)** – A monolayer of cells beneath the photoreceptors that supports their function by recycling visual pigments, transporting nutrients, and absorbing scattered light. RPE atrophy begins in the mid-periphery and spreads centrally.

**3. Photoreceptors** – The light-sensing cells of the retina:
- **Rods** (responsible for night and peripheral vision) are affected first and most severely.
- **Cones** (responsible for central, high-acuity, and color vision) degenerate later.

**Mechanism**: Mutations in the CHM gene lead to absence or dysfunction of REP-1 protein. REP-1 normally escorts Rab proteins to cell membranes, facilitating intracellular trafficking. Without functional REP-1, Rab proteins cannot properly bind to organelle membranes, leading to premature cell death. The retina has very little of a compensatory protein (REP-2) found elsewhere in the body, making it uniquely vulnerable [citation:2].

## Types or Stages

Choroideremia is typically divided by severity and progression, though staging is not formally standardized:

**Typical (Classic) Choroideremia (Null Phenotype)**
- Complete absence of functional REP-1 due to frameshift, nonsense, or splice-site mutations.
- Early onset of night blindness (first decade of life).
- Progressive peripheral vision loss leading to tunnel vision by young adulthood.
- Legal blindness typically by ages 40–60.

**Mild (Hypomorphic) Choroideremia**
- Caused by splice-site variants (e.g., c.1350-3C>G) that allow production of partially functional REP-1 [citation:8].
- Later onset and slower progression.
- Residual visual function may persist longer.
- May present challenges for gene therapy eligibility due to atypical disease course.

**Carrier State (Female Carriers)**
- Females with one mutated CHM gene copy are typically asymptomatic but show characteristic fundus findings (patchy RPE atrophy, chorioretinal degeneration) on examination [citation:3][citation:4].
- Some carriers experience nyctalopia or visual field changes, particularly after age 40 [citation:3].
- Rare reports exist of fully affected females, usually due to skewed X-inactivation.

## Key Facts

- **Inheritance**: X-linked recessive. Males are affected; females are typically carriers. Fathers cannot pass the condition to sons [citation:2].
- **Prevalence**: 1 in 50,000 to 100,000 males [citation:9].
- **First symptom**: Night blindness (nyctalopia), often noticed in early childhood (ages 5–10).
- **Progression pattern**: Night blindness → peripheral field loss (tunnel vision) → central vision decline → legal blindness.
- **Central vision preservation**: Often maintained until ages 40–60, enabling reading and face recognition for decades after peripheral vision loss [citation:9].
- **Genetic testing**: Confirms diagnosis by identifying pathogenic CHM mutations; required for gene therapy trial eligibility [citation:8].
- **No approved treatments currently** exist, though gene therapy (timrepigene emparvovec) has been investigated in Phase III clinical trials [citation:5].
- **Differential diagnosis**: Often confused with retinitis pigmentosa (RP); about 6% of patients initially diagnosed with RP actually have choroideremia [citation:4].
- **Carrier examination**: Female carriers show characteristic fundus changes (patchy RPE atrophy, granular pigmentation) detectable on dilated eye exam, even without symptoms.

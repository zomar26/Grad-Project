# Treatment of Choroideremia

## Available Treatments

**Currently, no FDA-approved or EMA-approved treatments exist that halt, reverse, or cure choroideremia.** Management focuses on:

1. Monitoring disease progression
2. Treating complications (e.g., cystoid macular edema, choroidal neovascularization)
3. Low vision rehabilitation to maximize functional ability
4. Investigational therapies (gene therapy – not yet approved)

## Medications

**No approved disease-modifying medications** exist for choroideremia.

**Off-label treatments for complications** (not disease-modifying):

- **Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors** (e.g., dorzolamide eye drops, oral acetazolamide) – May reduce cystoid macular edema (CME), which develops in some patients and can worsen central vision. Response varies; not all patients benefit.

- **Anti-VEGF injections** (ranibizumab, aflibercept, bevacizumab) – For the rare complication of choroidal neovascularization (CNV). Intravitreal injections can reduce subretinal fluid and stabilize vision. Not indicated for routine choroideremia without CNV.

- **No proven benefit**: Vitamin A, docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), lutein, or antioxidant supplements. Unlike retinitis pigmentosa, these have not demonstrated efficacy in slowing choroideremia progression.

## Surgical Options

**No curative surgeries exist** for choroideremia.

**Investigational: Subretinal gene therapy (timrepigene emparvovec)**

- **Mechanism**: A recombinant adeno-associated virus (AAV2) delivers a functional copy of the CHM gene (encoding REP-1) to surviving retinal pigment epithelium cells via subretinal injection [citation:5].

- **Trial status**: Phase III randomized controlled trial completed (NCT03496012). The primary endpoint (3-line gain in visual acuity) was not met, though secondary endpoints showed:
    - High-dose group: mean BCVA decline of only 0.3 ETDRS letters at 12 months vs. 2.3 letters decline in control group
    - 83% of high-dose patients maintained ≥1 line of vision vs. 68% of controls [citation:5]

- **Regulatory status**: Not approved by FDA or EMA. The trial did not meet FDA's primary endpoint, though the EMA considers 2-line improvement clinically meaningful. No current plans for commercial approval [citation:5].

- **Candidate selection considerations**: Patients with earlier-stage disease (better baseline visual acuity) may have greater potential to benefit, but trials have largely enrolled advanced patients to allow measurable improvement. Hypomorphic variant patients may have residual REP-1 activity, complicating gene therapy candidacy [citation:8].

- **Risks**: Subretinal injection requires vitrectomy and intentional retinal detachment, which carries risks of cataract, retinal tear/detachment, macular hole, endophthalmitis, and vision loss from surgical trauma.

**Other investigational approaches** (preclinical or early-phase):
- **Stem cell therapy** – RPE replacement strategies; not yet in human trials for choroideremia.
- **Optogenetics** – Viral delivery of light-sensitive proteins to remaining retinal cells; early-stage research.
- **Antisense oligonucleotides** – For splice-site mutations to restore correct mRNA splicing; preclinical.

## Lifestyle Recommendations

- **Protective eyewear** – UV-blocking sunglasses (wraparound style preferred) to reduce potential light toxicity, though benefit unproven.

- **Avoid vitamin A supplementation** – No evidence of benefit for choroideremia; potential liver toxicity at high doses. Do not take without ophthalmologist approval.

- **Optimize general health** – Control blood pressure, blood sugar, and cholesterol; these do not affect choroideremia progression but reduce risk of overlapping age-related eye diseases.

- **Regular low vision assessments** – Every 1–2 years to update magnification devices and rehabilitation strategies.

- **Fall prevention** – Home safety evaluation, physical therapy for balance, and strength training.

## Treatment Limitations

- **No approved therapy** – All interventions are supportive or investigational.
- **Gene therapy challenges**:
    - Requires surgery with inherent risks
    - Advanced disease (severe RPE loss) may limit transduction efficiency – RPE cells must be alive to express the transgene [citation:5]
    - Duration of effect unknown (AAV-mediated expression may decline over years)
    - High cost; uncertain commercial viability
    - Hypomorphic variants may not benefit (residual endogenous REP-1 could limit additional effect) [citation:8]

- **Complication management is imperfect** – Cystoid macular edema may be refractory to carbonic anhydrase inhibitors.

- **Vision loss is irreversible** – Once photoreceptors degenerate, no current intervention can restore them.

## Prognosis

**Natural history (untreated)** :

| Age Range | Typical Visual Status |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Childhood (5–10 years) | Night blindness begins |
| Adolescence | Progressive peripheral field loss; central acuity normal (20/20–20/25) |
| Young adulthood (20–30 years) | Tunnel vision (field <20–30 degrees); may meet driving restrictions |
| Middle adulthood (40–50 years) | Central vision begins declining; acuity 20/40–20/80; reading still possible with magnification |
| Late adulthood (50–70 years) | Legal blindness (20/200 or field <20 degrees) in most patients |
| Advanced age (>70 years) | Severe central vision loss; hand motion or light perception only in some |

**Key prognostic points** :

- **High variability** – Some patients retain 20/40 vision into their 60s; others become legally blind by age 30–40, even within the same family.
- **Central vision is the last to decline** – Reading, face recognition, and color vision are preserved for decades after peripheral loss.
- **No guaranteed progression rate** – Cannot predict individual disease course from genotype alone, though null mutations generally cause more severe disease than hypomorphic variants.
- **Complete blindness (no light perception)** is rare – Some peripheral light perception often persists even in end-stage disease.

**With gene therapy (investigational)** – Phase III data suggest potential for slowing decline, but long-term outcomes unknown [citation:5]. No data yet on whether early intervention (presymptomatic or early symptomatic) could preserve normal vision for decades; such trials have not been conducted.

**Prognosis for carriers** – Excellent. Most remain asymptomatic throughout life. Those with symptomatic findings typically have mild, late-onset night blindness or subtle field defects that do not progress to legal blindness.

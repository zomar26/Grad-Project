# Symptoms of Choroideremia

## Common Symptoms

Symptom onset and progression vary significantly among affected individuals, even within the same family. The following sequence is typical:

**Early Symptoms (Childhood to Early Adolescence)**

- **Night blindness (nyctalopia)** – The earliest and most consistent symptom, often noticed between ages 5 and 10. Difficulty seeing in dim lighting, such as:
    - Navigating a movie theater or restaurant at night
    - Playing outdoor games at dusk
    - Walking from a brightly lit room into a dark hallway
    - Adjusting to oncoming headlights while driving (in later years)

- **Peripheral vision loss (constriction)** – Begins as a ring-like scotoma (blind area) in the mid-periphery that gradually expands outward. Patients may:
    - Bump into door frames, furniture, or people on their sides
    - Trip over objects not directly in front of them
    - Appear clumsy or unaware of their surroundings, especially in unfamiliar environments
    - Have difficulty with sports requiring wide field awareness (e.g., basketball, soccer)

**Intermediate Symptoms (Adolescence to Young Adulthood)**

- **Tunnel vision (concentric field constriction)** – Progressive narrowing of the visual field such that only a small central island of vision remains. Patients describe seeing "as if looking through a paper towel tube" or "a narrow straw."

- **Difficulty with mobility in low-contrast situations** – Steps, curbs, uneven pavement, and dark clothing against dark backgrounds become hard to distinguish.

- **Impaired dark adaptation** – Prolonged time to recover vision after exposure to bright light (e.g., walking indoors on a sunny day, recovering after a camera flash).

## Advanced Symptoms

**Late-Stage Symptoms (Adulthood, Ages 40–60+)**

- **Central vision decline** – Gradual reduction in visual acuity (sharpness). Reading becomes difficult without magnification; recognizing faces across a room becomes challenging. However, many patients retain useful central vision (20/40 to 20/80) well into their 50s.

- **Legal blindness** – By late adulthood (fifth to sixth decade), visual acuity often deteriorates to 20/200 or worse, or the visual field constricts to less than 20 degrees in diameter, meeting legal blindness criteria in most jurisdictions.

- **Complete loss of peripheral light perception** – In end-stage disease, the visual field may narrow to less than 5–10 degrees, severely limiting independent mobility.

- **Choroidal neovascularization (CNV)** – Rarely, abnormal blood vessels grow from the choroid into the subretinal space, causing sudden worsening of central vision, metamorphopsia (wavy distortion), or subretinal hemorrhage [citation:9].

**Carrier Symptoms (Less Common)**

- Most female carriers remain asymptomatic.
- Some carriers (particularly after age 40) experience:
    - Mild night blindness
    - Subtle peripheral field defects detectable by formal perimetry
    - Patchy areas of RPE atrophy visible on fundus exam without corresponding symptoms

## When to Seek Medical Attention

**Urgent (within 24–48 hours)** – Seek immediate ophthalmologic evaluation if you or a family member with choroideremia experiences:

- **Sudden worsening of central vision** over days (not months or years)
- **New distortion (waviness)** of straight lines (suggests possible choroidal neovascularization)
- **Sudden increase in floaters or flashes** of light (could indicate retinal tear or detachment, though less common in choroideremia)

**Routine (schedule within weeks to months)**

- **New difficulty with night vision** – Especially in a child or young adult, warrants comprehensive eye examination including genetic counseling if family history is suggestive.
- **Progressive difficulty with peripheral awareness** – Bumping into objects or tripping more frequently.
- **Change in ability to read or recognize faces** – Even if gradual, document with your retina specialist.
- **Annual monitoring** – Patients with confirmed choroideremia should have annual examinations including visual field testing, optical coherence tomography (OCT), and electroretinography (ERG) to track progression.

**Genetic counseling referral** – Any individual with a family history of X-linked retinal dystrophy or a newly diagnosed affected male should be referred for genetic counseling to discuss inheritance patterns, carrier testing for female relatives, and reproductive options.

## Symptoms That May Affect Daily Activities

| Activity | Potential Symptom Impact |
|----------|--------------------------|
| Night driving | Profound difficulty due to night blindness; most patients voluntarily stop night driving by early adulthood |
| Daytime driving | Peripheral field loss may make it impossible to see vehicles approaching from side streets or pedestrians stepping from curbs; tunnel vision may meet legal requirements for driving cessation |
| Reading | Well-preserved until late stages; eventual need for magnification, large print, or audio formats |
| Walking in dim areas | Hallways, stairwells, parking garages, and outdoor areas at dusk become hazardous |
| Navigating crowds | Inability to see people approaching from sides leads to collisions or social anxiety |
| Sports and recreation | Difficulty tracking balls, seeing teammates, or avoiding obstacles |
| Recognizing faces | Central vision decline in late stages makes social interactions challenging |
| Cooking and household tasks | Peripheral loss increases risk of burns, cuts, or spills from unseen hazards at counter edges |
| Using stairs or curbs | Poor contrast sensitivity and field loss increase fall risk, especially on uneven surfaces |

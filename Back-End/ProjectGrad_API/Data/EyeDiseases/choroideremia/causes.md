# Causes and Risk Factors of Choroideremia

## Main Causes

Choroideremia is caused exclusively by mutations in the **CHM gene** located on the X chromosome (Xq21.2). This gene provides instructions for producing **Rab escort protein-1 (REP-1)** , a protein essential for intracellular trafficking [citation:2].

**Mechanism of disease**:

1. **REP-1 function**: REP-1 attaches to Rab proteins (small GTPases) and escorts them to the membranes of cellular organelles. Rab proteins direct the movement of proteins and vesicles within cells—a process critical for cell survival and function.

2. **Consequence of CHM mutation**: Mutations lead to either complete absence of REP-1 protein or production of a non-functional protein. Without functional REP-1, Rab proteins cannot reach organelle membranes, disrupting intracellular transport.

3. **Selective vulnerability of the retina**: A related protein, REP-2, can perform REP-1's duties in most body tissues. However, the retina contains very little REP-2 and cannot compensate for REP-1 loss. Retinal cells—particularly the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE), photoreceptors, and choroidal vasculature—therefore die prematurely [citation:2].

## Risk Factors

**Primary risk factor: Male sex**
- Because choroideremia follows X-linked recessive inheritance, males (46,XY) with one mutated CHM gene copy are affected.
- Females (46,XX) require mutations in both CHM copies to be affected—an extremely rare event.

**Family history**
- Affected males pass the mutation to all daughters (who become obligate carriers) but to no sons.
- Carrier females have a 50% chance of passing the mutation to each child: sons will be affected, daughters will be carriers.
- Approximately one-third of cases result from de novo (new) mutations without prior family history.

**No environmental or lifestyle risk factors** are known to cause or modify the development of choroideremia. The condition is purely genetic.

## Genetic Factors

**CHM gene mutations** – Over 200 distinct pathogenic mutations have been identified, including:

- **Frameshift mutations** (~50% of cases) – Insertions or deletions that disrupt the reading frame, leading to premature stop codons and complete loss of REP-1.
- **Nonsense mutations** (~20%) – Single nucleotide changes that create a premature stop codon.
- **Splice-site mutations** (~15%) – Disrupt normal mRNA splicing, often leading to exon skipping and non-functional protein [citation:8].
- **Large deletions** (~10%) – Removing entire exons or the whole CHM gene.
- **Missense mutations** (~5%) – Single amino acid substitutions; may allow partial protein function (hypomorphic variants).

**Genotype-phenotype correlations**:
- **Null mutations** (complete loss of REP-1) cause typical, severe choroideremia with early onset.
- **Hypomorphic variants** (e.g., certain splice-site mutations) can allow residual REP-1 activity, leading to milder, later-onset disease with slower progression [citation:8].

**Carrier status** – Females with one CHM mutation are carriers. Approximately 15-20% of carriers show symptomatic findings (night blindness, field defects), likely due to skewed X-inactivation where the normal X chromosome is preferentially inactivated in retinal cells.

## Environmental Factors

**None identified.** Unlike age-related macular degeneration or diabetic retinopathy, choroideremia is not influenced by smoking, diet, sunlight exposure, medications, or systemic diseases. This reflects its nature as a monogenic, cell-autonomous degenerative disorder.

## Prevention Strategies

**Currently, no strategies exist to prevent choroideremia** because the condition is determined entirely by genetics and begins developing prenatally.

**Prevention of transmission (reproductive options)** – For families with known CHM mutations:
- **Preimplantation genetic testing (PGT)** – Embryos created via in-vitro fertilization can be tested for the familial CHM mutation before implantation.
- **Prenatal diagnosis** – Chorionic villus sampling or amniocentesis can determine whether a male fetus carries the mutation.
- **Preconception carrier screening** – Female relatives of affected males can undergo testing to determine carrier status before family planning.
- **Sperm or egg donation** – Using donor gametes from individuals without CHM mutations eliminates transmission risk.

**Secondary prevention (slowing progression)** – While no treatment is approved, the following may preserve remaining vision:
- **Protective eyewear** – UV-blocking sunglasses to reduce light-induced oxidative stress (theoretical benefit; not proven to alter disease course).
- **Avoid vitamin A supplementation** – Unlike some forms of retinitis pigmentosa, vitamin A has no demonstrated benefit for choroideremia and may be hepatotoxic at high doses. Do not supplement without medical supervision.
- **Regular monitoring** – Annual examinations to detect complications such as cystoid macular edema or choroidal neovascularization (rare) that may be treatable.
- **Low vision rehabilitation** – Maximizes functional use of remaining vision but does not alter biological progression.

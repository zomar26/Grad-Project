# Treatment of Age-related macular degeneration (AMD)

## Available Treatments

Treatment is stage-dependent: **no treatment reverses vision loss** from atrophic (dry AMD) changes, but treatments can slow progression (dry AMD) or arrest/wet AMD) vision loss.

| Stage | Treatment Goal | Main Approach |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| Early AMD (small/medium drusen, no vision loss) | Prevent progression | Lifestyle, risk factor control, no supplements |
| Intermediate AMD (large drusen, pigment changes) | Slow progression | AREDS2 supplements + lifestyle |
| Geographic atrophy (late dry) | Slow growth of atrophic lesions | New complement inhibitor intravitreal injections (pegcetacoplan, avacincaptad pegol) |
| Neovascular (wet) AMD | Arrest leakage, preserve vision | Anti-VEGF intravitreal injections |

## Medications

### For Geographic Atrophy (Dry AMD, late stage)

- **Pegcetacoplan (Syfovre®)** – Complement C3 inhibitor. Intravitreal injection monthly or every other month. Slows growth of geographic atrophy by ~20–30% over 2 years. Does not improve vision. Potential adverse effects: ocular inflammation, endophthalmitis (rare), and higher risk of developing wet AMD.
- **Avacincaptad pegol (Izervay™)** – Complement C5 inhibitor. Monthly intravitreal injection. Similar efficacy (~20–35% GA growth reduction). Safety profile similar to pegcetacoplan.

### For Neovascular (Wet) AMD

**Anti-VEGF agents (first-line, highly effective):**
- **Ranibizumab (Lucentis®)** – Monoclonal antibody fragment. Monthly or treat-and-extend (T&E) dosing. Standard efficacy.
- **Aflibercept (Eylea®)** – VEGF receptor-Fc fusion protein. Every 4–8 weeks (typically 8-week after initial loading). Longer duration than ranibizumab.
- **Bevacizumab (Avastin®)** – Full-length antibody. Off-label but equally effective as ranibizumab for most patients at ~1/40th the cost. Monthly or T&E. Used widely due to cost-effectiveness.
- **Faricimab (Vabysmo®)** – Bispecific antibody (anti-VEGF + anti-Ang-2). Dosing every 4–16 weeks (most patients can extend to 12–16 weeks after loading). Newest agent; improves durability.

**Treatment protocol:**
- **Loading phase**: 3 monthly injections.
- **Maintenance**: T&E approach – injection interval extended by 2 weeks if stable (up to 12–16 weeks), shortened if fluid recurs.
- **Outcomes**: ~95% of patients stabilize vision; ~30–40% gain 3 or more lines of vision. Best results with early initiation (when vision is still good).

## Surgical Options

Surgery plays a **minimal role** in AMD:
- **Submacular surgery** (removal of CNV membrane) – Historically performed but no better than observation; abandoned after anti-VEGF era.
- **Macular translocation surgery** – Moving fovea over healthier RPE. High complication rate; rarely performed.
- **Retinal pigment epithelium transplantation** – Experimental; not standard of care.

### For severe complications of wet AMD:
- **Pneumatic displacement of submacular hemorrhage** – Intravitreal gas ± tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) to move blood away from fovea; limited evidence.
- **Pars plana vitrectomy with subretinal hemorrhage drainage** – For massive hemorrhages threatening fovea; vision outcomes remain poor.

## Lifestyle Recommendations

- **Smoking cessation** – Slows progression (benefit seen within months to a year).
- **AREDS2 supplements** – Only for intermediate AMD or late AMD in one eye; not for early AMD. *Caution: Zinc can cause GI upset (take with food).*
- **Heart-healthy diet** – Mediterranean pattern; leafy greens, fatty fish (salmon, mackerel) twice weekly.
- **Control cardiovascular risk factors** – BP, cholesterol, blood glucose.
- **Protect from sunlight** – Wraparound sunglasses with UV400 and blue-blocking lenses.
- **Low vision rehabilitation** – Maximizes remaining vision (see Daily Life file).

## Treatment Limitations

- **No cure** – Treatments slow progression (dry) or stabilize (wet) but do not restore lost photoreceptors.
- **Geographic atrophy treatments** – Slow lesion growth but do not reverse atrophy; vision continues to decline over years.
- **Anti-VEGF requires chronic injections** – Often every 4–12 weeks indefinitely; non-adherence leads to irreversible vision loss.
- **Treatment burden** – Monthly clinic visits can be difficult for elderly patients or those with transportation issues.
- **Wet AMD may convert to atrophy** – After years of anti-VEGF, some eyes develop subretinal fibrosis or geographic atrophy despite fluid control.
- **Non-responders** – ~5–10% of wet AMD patients do not improve or stabilize despite maximal anti-VEGF.

## Prognosis

| AMD Subtype | Typical Course with Treatment | Without Treatment |
|-------------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| Early AMD | No vision loss; ~10–15% progress to intermediate over 5 years | Same |
| Intermediate AMD | ~20% progress to late AMD (dry or wet) over 5 years | ~30% progress over 5 years |
| Geographic atrophy | Slow central vision loss over 5–10 years; AREDS2 slows progression ~20%; complement inhibitors slow ~25% | Faster loss; reading ability lost after ~6–8 years from diagnosis |
| Neovascular (wet) AMD | 90–95% stable or improved at 2 years; average VA ~20/40 to 20/80 depending on starting VA | 50% progress to legal blindness (20/200 or worse) within 2 years |

**Key prognostic factors:**
- **Better outcomes**: Early treatment (within 2 weeks of symptom onset), smaller lesion, lower baseline vision (paradoxically more room to improve).
- **Worse outcomes**: Large submacular hemorrhage, fibrotic scar, advanced age, poor response after 3 loading doses.
- **Second eye risk**: About one-third of patients with unilateral wet AMD develop wet AMD in the fellow eye within 5 years; annual monitoring essential.

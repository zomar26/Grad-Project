# Living with Age-related macular degeneration (AMD)

## Reading and Computer Use

- **Magnification devices** – Handheld lighted magnifiers (3× to 10×), stand magnifiers, or electronic video magnifiers (CCTV) that project text onto a large screen.
- **Smartphone apps** – Magnifying apps (e.g., Magnifier on iOS, Magnifying Glass on Android). Text-to-speech apps (Seeing AI, Voice Dream Reader).
- **Large-print materials** – Many publishers offer large-print books (18–20 point font); local libraries often have large-print sections.
- **Computer accessibility settings** – Enable high-contrast mode, invert colors, use screen readers (JAWS, NVDA, VoiceOver on Mac/iOS, Narrator on Windows). Zoom function (Ctrl + / Cmd +).
- **E-readers** – Amazon Kindle allows adjustable font size (up to 14 point) and boldness; iPad with Apple Books has similar flexibility.
- **Bold-line paper and pens** – For writing checks or notes.
- **Audiobooks** – Audible, Libby (library audiobooks), Bard (National Library Service for the Blind and Print Disabled – free in US).
- **Lighting** – Use gooseneck lamps or clip-on LED task lights directed at reading material. Avoid glare from glossy paper (use matte or antiglare overlays).

## Mobility and Navigation

- **White cane** – Standard long cane or folding cane for detecting curbs, steps, and obstacles. Not just for total blindness; low vision patients benefit from cane use.
- **Sighted guide technique** – Hold the elbow of a sighted companion; the guide walks half a step ahead.
- **Orientation and mobility (O&M) training** – Taught by certified O&M specialists; includes cane use, route planning, and use of auditory cues.
- **Talking GPS apps** – Seeing Assistant, Lazarillo, Blindsquare. Provide turn-by-turn voice navigation.
- **Contrast enhancements** – Paint edges of steps or stair risers with high-contrast yellow or white paint; apply tactile tape (bumpy strips) on handrails.
- **Nighttime precautions** – AMD patients have poor dark adaptation; avoid walking in dim areas without a bright flashlight (LED 200+ lumens).

## Driving Considerations

**Generally, driving is not safe** once AMD reaches intermediate or late stages, due to:
- Central scotoma (cannot read street signs or see traffic lights)
- Metamorphopsia (misjudges distances or lane position)
- Poor contrast sensitivity (difficulty seeing pedestrians or debris in low light)

**Legal status varies by jurisdiction:**
- United States: Most states require visual acuity of 20/40 or better in at least one eye (with or without correction) and a horizontal visual field of at least 140 degrees. Patients with geographic atrophy or wet AMD in the better eye rarely meet these standards.
- UK (DVLA): Must stop driving if VA worse than 20/40 (6/12) in both eyes or if there is a central scotoma.
- **Recommendation**: Discuss driving with your ophthalmologist; many patients voluntarily stop before legal requirements force them. Transition planning (family rides, paratransit, ride-sharing) should begin early.

## Home Adaptations

- **Lighting** – Use bright, even lighting (LED daylight bulbs 5000–6500K). Install under-cabinet lights in kitchen. Avoid single bright spotlights that cause glare.
- **Contrast labeling** – Use black markers on white labels for light switches; tactile dots (bump dots) on microwave keypads, washer/dryer settings.
- **Kitchen safety** – Use contrasting cutting boards (white board on dark counter, dark board on light counter). Pour liquids with finger over rim to feel level. Use audible timers and talking kitchen scales.
- **Medication management** – Pill organizers with large compartments and tactile markings (e.g., different shapes for morning/evening). Use a talking pill reminder alarm.
- **Bathroom** – Use colored toilet seats (red or blue) to contrast with white porcelain. Install grab bars in shower. Use liquid soap dispensers (bar soap is easy to drop).
- **Furniture and tripping hazards** – Remove throw rugs, run cords along walls under cord covers. Keep furniture in fixed positions; avoid rearranging without informing the patient.

## Emotional and Psychological Impact

**Common emotional responses:**
- **Grief and denial** – Loss of ability to read, drive, or see faces is comparable to losing a loved one. Denial (refusing low vision tools, skipping appointments) is common initially.
- **Anxiety** – Fear of progressing to wet AMD or becoming more dependent. Fear of Charles Bonnet hallucinations (patients worry they are “going crazy”).
- **Depression** – Prevalence of major depression in AMD patients is ~25–33% (higher than general elderly population). Risk factors: recent vision loss, living alone, prior depression.
- **Social isolation** – Difficulty recognizing faces → avoids gatherings; can’t read cards or menus → avoids restaurants; can’t drive → fewer outings.
- **Loss of independence** – Need for help with finances, medications, transportation.

**Mental health strategies:**
- **Low vision rehabilitation counseling** – Combined occupational therapy and psychological support.
- **Support groups** – In-person (often at low vision clinics) or virtual (Hadley, VisionAware, MD Support – online groups for AMD).
- **Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)** – Effective for adjusting to vision loss; challenge catastrophic thinking (“I’m useless” → “I need new ways to do tasks”).
- **Treat depression** – SSRIs (e.g., sertraline, escitalopram) are safe in elderly and may improve quality of life even if vision doesn’t improve.
- **Charles Bonnet syndrome** – Reassurance is the main treatment (hallucinations are not mental illness). Reduce anxiety by explaining they occur in up to 30% of advanced AMD. No medication needed unless distressing.

## Tips for Family Members and Caregivers

**Communication:**
- **Identify yourself** – “Hi Mom, it’s Sarah” (don’t assume patient recognizes you by silhouette).
- **Describe what you’re doing** – “I’m putting a cup of coffee on your right side, near the plate.”
- **Use the clock face method** – “Your fork is at 12 o’clock, the spoon is at 3 o’clock.”
- **Don’t rearrange furniture** – AMD patients use memorized layouts; moving items disorients them.
- **Ask before guiding** – “Would you like me to guide you?” Offer your elbow; don’t grab their arm.

**Practical assistance:**
- **Sort medications** – Use weekly pill organizers; read labels aloud while patient watches if possible.
- **Organize bills** – Use large-print checks; consider automatic bill pay.
- **Accompany to eye injections** – Patients cannot drive home after anti-VEGF (pupil dilation and possible vision fluctuation). Offer transportation and reassurance (injections are quick but anxiety-provoking).
- **Learn the Amsler grid** – Help patient test monthly and report changes.

**Caregiver self-care:**
- **Caregiver burnout is common** – Respite care, adult day programs for low vision, or in-home aides for a few hours weekly.
- **Join a caregiver support group** – Many offered by local low vision centers or Alzheimer’s groups (even though AMD is not dementia, caregiving challenges overlap).
- **Adjust expectations** – Patient may not be able to read to grandchildren anymore, but can listen to audiobooks together or go for walks. Focus on preserved abilities.

**Emergency preparedness:**
- Keep a list of emergency contacts in large print and on voice-activated device (Alexa, etc.).
- Ensure smoke alarms have strobe lights (patient may not see smoke but will see flashing light).
- Practice fire evacuation routes with cane or guide.

**Key message for families**: AMD is not a cognitive disease. Your loved one’s mind is intact; they simply need adapted communication and environment. Patience and creativity go further than sympathy.

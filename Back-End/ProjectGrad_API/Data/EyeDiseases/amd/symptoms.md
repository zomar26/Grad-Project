# Symptoms of Age-related macular degeneration (AMD)

## Common Symptoms

*Note: Early AMD is often asymptomatic. Symptoms typically begin in intermediate or late stages.*

- **Blurred or fuzzy central vision** – Difficulty seeing fine details such as print size on a phone or a street sign from a normal distance.
- **Increased difficulty reading** – Words may appear broken, missing letters, or fade out; need for brighter light or magnification.
- **Diminished color intensity** – Colors seem less vibrant or faded, particularly blues and yellows.
- **Metamorphopsia (visual distortion)** – Straight lines (e.g., door frames, telephone poles, window blinds) appear wavy, bent, or crooked. This is a key symptom of fluid from wet AMD or from drusen-induced photoreceptor displacement.
- **Scotomas (blind spots)** – A dark, empty, or blurry spot in the center of vision. Patients often report “a hole in my vision” or missing the middle of a face when looking directly at someone.
- **Delayed dark adaptation** – Prolonged time to adjust when moving from bright light to dim light (e.g., entering a movie theater or driving at dusk). This is one of the earliest functional changes.
- **Glare sensitivity** – Bothersome glare from oncoming headlights or bright reflections.

## Advanced Symptoms

- **Central scotoma becomes dense and stable** – Patient cannot read any words, recognize faces directly ahead, or see fine details; peripheral vision remains intact.
- **Charles Bonnet syndrome (CBS)** – Complex visual hallucinations (geometric patterns, animals, faces, or whole scenes) occur in 10–30% of patients with significant central vision loss. Patients are aware the images are not real and often hesitate to report them due to fear of psychiatric illness.
- **Rapid vision decline over days to weeks** – Suggests conversion to wet AMD with subretinal fluid or hemorrhage. May also cause sudden increase in floaters or a dark curtain in vision (though less common than with retinal detachment).
- **Subretinal hemorrhage** – In wet AMD, a large bleed can cause a dramatic, sudden “blackout” of central vision, sometimes with a visible dark area on examination.

## When to Seek Medical Attention

**Urgent (within 24–48 hours) – Call your ophthalmologist immediately:**
- Sudden worsening of central vision over days (not months or years)
- New or increased waviness of straight lines (positive Amsler grid change)
- A new blind spot in or near the center of vision
- Sudden increase in floaters OR a shadow/curtain in vision (though less common in AMD, requires rule-out of retinal tear/detachment)

**Routine (schedule within weeks):**
- Gradual difficulty reading or recognizing faces
- Need for progressively brighter reading light
- Colors appearing less vivid over months to years
- Annual comprehensive eye exam if over age 50, even without symptoms (early AMD is asymptomatic)

## Symptoms That May Affect Daily Activities

| Activity | Potential Symptom Impact |
|----------|--------------------------|
| Reading | Words break, letters disappear, need for magnification or large-print books |
| Recognizing faces | Missing the central details of a face (eyes, nose); may mistake strangers or not see expressions |
| Driving | Difficulty reading street signs, seeing traffic lights clearly, or judging distances (legal driving may become impossible) |
| Watching TV | Missing details like subtitles, scores in sports, or actors’ expressions |
| Cooking | Inability to see dial settings on stove/oven, difficulty chopping food safely |
| Using digital devices | Inability to see cursor, icons, or small text on phones/tablets |
| Walking on uneven ground | Central blind spot may cause missteps on stairs, curbs, or cracks (compounded by poor contrast sensitivity) |
| Taking medications | Difficulty reading prescription labels or distinguishing similar-looking pills |

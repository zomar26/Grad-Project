# Age-related macular degeneration (AMD)

## Definition

Age-related macular degeneration (AMD) is a progressive eye disease that affects the macula, the small central portion of the retina responsible for sharp, straight-ahead vision. AMD is the leading cause of severe vision loss in people aged 50 and older in developed countries. It does not cause complete blindness (peripheral vision remains intact) but can severely impair central vision needed for reading, driving, recognizing faces, and performing close-up tasks.

## How the Eye is Affected

The macula is a yellowish, oval-shaped area near the center of the retina (the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye). It contains a high density of cone photoreceptor cells that enable high-acuity vision.

In AMD, progressive damage occurs to the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE)—a monolayer of cells beneath the photoreceptors that nourishes and supports them. Key structural changes include:

- **Drusen**: Yellowish extracellular deposits that accumulate between the RPE and Bruch’s membrane (a thin layer separating the retina from the choroid). Small, few drusen are common with aging, but medium to large, numerous drusen are hallmarks of early AMD.
- **Geographic atrophy (GA)**: In late-stage non-neovascular (dry) AMD, areas of RPE and photoreceptor cell death lead to smooth, well-demarcated atrophic patches.
- **Choroidal neovascularization (CNV)**: In neovascular (wet) AMD, abnormal blood vessels grow from the choroid through Bruch’s membrane into the subretinal space, leaking fluid, blood, and lipid, causing rapid damage to photoreceptors.

These changes result in progressive loss of central vision while peripheral (side) vision remains preserved.

## Types or Stages

AMD is classified into two main types and several stages:

### 1. Non-neovascular (Dry) AMD – 85–90% of cases
- **Early AMD**: Medium-sized drusen (63–125 μm) without pigment changes or vision loss.
- **Intermediate AMD**: Large drusen (>125 μm) and/or retinal pigment abnormalities (hyperpigmentation or hypopigmentation). Some patients notice mild blurring or difficulty with low light.
- **Late-stage dry AMD (Geographic atrophy, GA)**: Well-defined areas of RPE and photoreceptor loss. Central vision slowly and progressively declines over years.

### 2. Neovascular (Wet) AMD – 10–15% of cases
- Can develop from any stage of dry AMD.
- Characterized by choroidal neovascularization (CNV) with leakage, exudation, or hemorrhage.
- Subtypes (based on fluorescein angiography/optical coherence tomography):
  - **Type 1 CNV**: Grows beneath the RPE (less aggressive).
  - **Type 2 CNV**: Penetrates into the subretinal space (more aggressive).
  - **Type 3 (retinal angiomatous proliferation, RAP)**: Originates from deep retinal capillaries.
- Without treatment, can cause rapid, severe central vision loss over weeks to months.

### 3. Age-related maculopathy (early changes without significant vision loss) is an older term; current preferred terminology uses the Beckman Initiative classification (no AMD, early, intermediate, late AMD).

## Key Facts

- **Prevalence**: Affects approximately 11 million people in the United States and 170 million globally. Prevalence rises sharply with age: ~2% of people 50–59, ~30% of those over 75.
- **Vision impact**: Dry AMD typically progresses over years; wet AMD can cause severe central vision loss in weeks if untreated.
- **Unilateral vs bilateral**: About one-third of patients with unilateral wet AMD develop wet AMD in the second eye within 5 years.
- **No cure exists**, but treatments can slow progression (dry AMD) or arrest vision loss (wet AMD).
- **Amsler grid** home monitoring is recommended for early detection of wet AMD conversion.
- **Genetic and lifestyle factors** strongly influence risk; modifiable factors include smoking, hypertension, high BMI, and diet.
- **Anti-VEGF injections** are first-line therapy for wet AMD and have reduced legal blindness rates by ~50% over the past 15 years.
- **Peripheral vision remains intact** even in late stages—total blindness from AMD alone does not occur.

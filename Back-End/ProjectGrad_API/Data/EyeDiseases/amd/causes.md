# Causes and Risk Factors of Age-related macular degeneration (AMD)

## Main Causes

AMD is a multifactorial disease with no single cause. The primary pathologic cascade involves:

1. **Oxidative stress** – Cumulative damage from free radicals (reactive oxygen species) to retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) cells, which have high metabolic rates and are exposed to light.
2. **Chronic inflammation** – Dysregulation of the complement system (part of innate immunity) leads to chronic low-grade inflammation, Drusen formation, and RPE damage.
3. **Lipofuscin accumulation** – Incomplete digestion of photoreceptor outer segments by RPE cells leads to lipofuscin, a toxic byproduct that further damages RPE.
4. **Impaired extracellular matrix turnover** – Age-related thickening and cross-linking of Bruch’s membrane impair nutrient/waste exchange between choroid and retina.

These processes lead to drusen deposition, RPE degeneration (dry AMD), and, in some patients, upregulation of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), causing choroidal neovascularization (wet AMD).

## Risk Factors

### Non-modifiable (Cannot Change)
- **Advanced age** – Strongest risk factor. Prevalence increases from ~2% at age 50–59 to ~30% after age 75.
- **Family history** – First-degree relative with AMD increases risk 3- to 6-fold.
- **Genetics** – See section below.
- **Race/ethnicity** – More common in Caucasians than African Americans, Hispanics, or Asian Americans.
- **Light iris color** – Some studies suggest lighter iris color (less melanin) is associated with modestly increased risk, possibly due to less retinal light absorption.
- **Female sex** – Slightly higher prevalence in women, partly due to longer life expectancy.

### Modifiable (Can Change) – **Focus prevention efforts here**
- **Cigarette smoking** – Increases risk 2–4 fold; dose-dependent. Toxins cause oxidative stress, reduce choroidal blood flow, and lower antioxidant levels. Smoking may accelerate AMD onset by 5–10 years.
- **Hypertension** – Poorly controlled high blood pressure increases risk of both dry AMD progression and conversion to wet AMD.
- **Atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease** – Shared risk factors (inflammation, endothelial dysfunction).
- **Obesity (BMI ≥30)** – Increases risk of progression from early to late AMD.
- **Diet** – Low intake of omega-3 fatty acids (fish), lutein/zeaxanthin (leafy greens), and vitamins C/E is associated with higher risk.
- **High glycemic index diet** – Refined carbohydrates may exacerbate oxidative stress.
- **Excessive sunlight exposure** – Lifetime cumulative exposure to UV/blue light may increase risk, though evidence is less strong than for smoking.

## Genetic Factors

- **CFH gene (chromosome 1)** – Y402H variant (single nucleotide polymorphism) accounts for ~40–50% of genetic risk. Complement factor H regulates the alternative complement pathway; risk variants lead to overactive complement, chronic inflammation, and drusen formation.
- **ARMS2/HTRA1 gene (chromosome 10)** – Accounts for ~20–30% of genetic risk. Function not fully understood but linked to mitochondrial dysfunction and extracellular matrix remodeling.
- **Other genes** – C2, CFB, C3 (complement pathway), APOE (lipid metabolism), and TIMP3 (extracellular matrix).
- **Polygenic risk scores** – Combining multiple risk variants can identify individuals with >50-fold relative risk, but genetic testing is not routinely recommended for clinical management as lifestyle modifications apply to all.

## Environmental Factors

- **Air pollution (PM2.5)** – Emerging evidence suggests long-term exposure to fine particulate matter increases AMD risk (mechanism: systemic inflammation and oxidative stress).
- **Occupational exposure to bright light** – Welders, outdoor workers without eye protection may have modestly increased risk.
- **Low socioeconomic status** – Associated with worse access to eye care, higher smoking rates, poorer nutrition.

## Prevention Strategies

### Primary prevention (preventing onset)
- **Smoking cessation** – Single most effective intervention. Risk declines after quitting but remains elevated for decades.
- **Mediterranean-style diet** – High in leafy greens (spinach, kale), fish (omega-3s), fruits, nuts, and whole grains.
- **Dietary supplements for prevention** – Not recommended for individuals with no AMD or only early AMD (no proven benefit). Instead, obtain nutrients from food.
- **Regular exercise** – Improves cardiovascular health and may reduce oxidative stress.
- **Control blood pressure and lipids** – Target BP <130/80 mmHg (per general guidelines).
- **UV/blue light protection** – Wear sunglasses with UV400 rating and blue-blocking lenses when outdoors.
- **Routine eye exams** – Starting at age 50, then every 1–2 years; sooner if symptoms.

### Secondary prevention (slowing progression after diagnosis)
- **AREDS2 formula supplements** – For **intermediate AMD or late AMD in one eye only** (not for early AMD). Contains: vitamin C 500 mg, vitamin E 400 IU, lutein 10 mg, zeaxanthin 2 mg, zinc 80 mg (with copper 2 mg to prevent copper deficiency anemia). *Do not substitute with standard multivitamins.*
- **Avoid beta-carotene** – AREDS2 removed beta-carotene after it was linked to increased lung cancer risk in smokers. Patients who have smoked should not take original AREDS formula.
- **Amsler grid monitoring** – Monthly testing in each eye separately to detect metamorphopsia or new scotomas (signs of wet conversion).
- **Ophthalmology follow-up** – Every 6–12 months for intermediate AMD; every 1–4 months for wet AMD.

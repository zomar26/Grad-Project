# Symptoms of Cortical Cataract

## Common Symptoms

Symptoms of cortical cataracts are distinctive due to the light-scattering nature of the opacities. Patients may maintain good visual acuity (20/20–20/40) while experiencing significant functional problems in certain lighting conditions.

- **Glare and light sensitivity (photophobia)** – The most characteristic symptom. Patients report being "dazzled" or "blinded" by bright lights, including:
    - Sunlight reflecting off windshields, water, or snow
    - Overhead fluorescent lights in stores or offices
    - Oncoming headlights while driving at night
    - Bright computer screens or smartphones in dark rooms

- **Halos around lights** – Colored or white rings surrounding car headlights, streetlights, or decorative lights. Halos are caused by light diffraction through the wedge-shaped opacities.

- **Difficulty with night driving** – Combined effects of glare from oncoming headlights and halos around traffic lights make night driving challenging and eventually unsafe.

- **Blurred or hazy vision** – Less prominent than in nuclear cataracts, but develops as spokes extend into the central visual axis. Vision may be described as "looking through a foggy or dusty window."

- **Reduced contrast sensitivity** – Trouble distinguishing objects from their background, particularly in low-contrast situations:
    - Seeing white steps against a white wall
    - Reading gray text on a white page
    - Differentiating curbs or pavement changes
    - Seeing facial expressions in dim lighting

- **Monocular diplopia or polyopia** – Seeing double or multiple images with one eye (unlike binocular double vision caused by eye misalignment). The multiple images may overlap or appear as "ghost images."

- **Subjective visual fluctuation** – Symptoms may vary from day to day or with lighting conditions. Patients sometimes report "good days and bad days" or that "some days my glasses work better than others."

## Advanced Symptoms

As cortical opacities progress and spokes coalesce in the central lens, additional symptoms develop:

- **Dense central blurring** – Visual acuity declines to 20/70–20/200 or worse. Reading, face recognition, and watching television become difficult or impossible without cataract surgery.

- **Complete loss of contrast** – Objects, particularly those of similar color to their background, become nearly invisible. Patients may trip over curbs or steps they cannot see.

- **Color desaturation** – Colors appear faded or washed out, though less pronounced than with nuclear cataracts (which cause yellow-brown browning).

- **Difficulty in bright light paradox** – Some patients paradoxically see better in dim light because pupil dilation allows light to pass around peripheral opacities. They may prefer dim restaurants or wearing sunglasses indoors.

- **Inability to perform near tasks** – Reading fine print (pill bottles, receipts, phone screens) becomes impossible despite adequate distance vision.

- **Frequent prescription changes** – Patients may notice their eyeglass prescription changes every 6–12 months as the cataract progresses.

## When to Seek Medical Attention

**Schedule an eye exam (within weeks to months) if you experience:**

- **New or worsening glare or halos** that interfere with driving, reading, or daily activities.
- **Difficulty with night driving** – Especially if you find yourself avoiding night driving altogether.
- **Blurred vision** that is not corrected with a glasses prescription change.
- **Need for brighter light to read** – A common early sign of cataract progression.
- **Changes in color perception** – Colors appearing yellowed or faded.

**Seek urgent ophthalmologic evaluation (within 24–48 hours) if you experience:**

- **Sudden vision loss** over hours or days (not weeks or months) – Cortical cataracts progress slowly; sudden change suggests another cause (retinal detachment, macular degeneration, vitreous hemorrhage, acute glaucoma).
- **Pain with vision changes** – Cataracts are painless. Pain + vision change = possible acute angle-closure glaucoma or uveitis.
- **Flashes of light or new floaters** – Not typical of cataracts; could indicate posterior vitreous detachment or retinal tear.
- **A curtain or shadow across vision** – Emergency; possible retinal detachment.

**Annual comprehensive eye exams** are recommended for adults aged 65 and older, regardless of symptoms, to detect cataracts and other age-related eye diseases.

## Symptoms That May Affect Daily Activities

| Activity | Symptom Impact |
|----------|----------------|
| Daytime driving | Glare from sun reflecting off windshields or road surfaces; difficulty reading road signs in bright sun |
| Night driving | Severe glare from oncoming headlights; halos around traffic lights; unsafe after moderate progression |
| Reading | Spokes reaching center cause blur; need for magnification or bright task lighting; ghost images of text lines |
| Computer work | Glare from bright screen; halos around text or icons; difficulty with light mode (prefers dark mode) |
| Watching television | Reduced contrast makes dark scenes difficult to interpret; halos around bright objects |
| Cooking | Difficulty seeing dial settings on stoves; trouble distinguishing raw meat from cutting board; glare from stainless steel surfaces |
| Walking outdoors on sunny days | Disabling glare forces squinting or wearing sunglasses constantly; difficulty seeing steps or curbs |
| Playing sports (golf, tennis) | Reduced contrast makes tracking balls against sky or grass difficult; glare off equipment |
| Recognizing faces | Less impacted early (central vision preserved), but faces may appear hazy or ghosted in bright light |
| Walking in dim environments | Paradoxically easier than bright environments for some patients; however, reduced contrast sensitivity still impairs depth perception |
| Taking medications | Difficulty reading prescription labels (small print, low contrast) |
| Using smartphones/tablets | Glare from bright screens; halos around icons; prefers dark mode and large text |

# Treatment of Cortical Cataract

## Available Treatments

The only definitive treatment for cortical cataract is **surgical removal of the opacified lens** with implantation of an artificial intraocular lens (IOL). There are no medications, eye drops, or supplements that reverse or halt cataract progression.

Management is divided into:
- **Non-surgical management** (early stages): Optical correction and symptom management while monitoring progression.
- **Surgical management** (visually significant stages): Phacoemulsification with IOL implantation.

## Medications

**No FDA-approved medications** for treating cortical cataract. The following have been investigated but are **not recommended** due to lack of efficacy or safety concerns:

- **Aldose reductase inhibitors** – Theoretical benefit for diabetic cortical cataracts (sorbitol pathway). Clinical trials showed no significant benefit or unacceptable side effects.

- **N-acetylcarnosine eye drops** – Sold as a supplement, not FDA-approved. Randomized controlled trials have not demonstrated efficacy. Professional ophthalmology organizations (AAO, ASCRS) advise against use.

- **Lanosterol or other protein-aggregation inhibitors** – Preclinical studies only; no human efficacy data. Not recommended.

- **Antioxidant supplements** (vitamins C/E, lutein/zeaxanthin) – AREDS/AREDS2 trials found no reduction in cataract progression or surgery rates. Not recommended for cataract treatment.

**Off-label medications for symptom management**:
- **Tinted lenses** – Yellow, amber, or orange-tinted glasses (not medications) can reduce glare and improve contrast sensitivity. Prescription or over-the-counter.

## Surgical Options

**Phacoemulsification with intraocular lens (IOL) implantation** is the standard of care.

### Procedure Overview

1. **Anesthesia** – Topical (eye drops) or local anesthetic (peribulbar/retrobulbar block). General anesthesia rarely needed.
2. **Corneal incision** – 2–3 mm self-sealing incision.
3. **Capsulorhexis** – Circular opening in the anterior lens capsule.
4. **Phacoemulsification** – Ultrasonic probe breaks cortical and nuclear material into fragments, which are aspirated.
5. **IOL implantation** – Foldable IOL inserted through the small incision and unfolded in the capsular bag.
6. **Incision hydration** – No sutures required in most cases.

### Timing of Surgery

Indications for surgery (based on AAO Preferred Practice Pattern guidelines):

- **Visual symptoms** affecting daily activities (reading, driving, working, hobbies)
- **Visual acuity** typically 20/40 or worse, but symptoms (glare, contrast loss) may justify surgery with 20/20–20/30 acuity
- **Patient desire** for improved vision; patient-driven, not acuity-driven alone

**Special considerations**:
- Cortical cataracts with disabling glare but good acuity (20/20–20/30) are appropriate surgical candidates if patient reports functional impairment.
- Cataract surgery should not be performed "just because" the cataract exists; it must be visually significant to the patient.

### Intraocular Lens (IOL) Options

| IOL Type | Features | Considerations |
|----------|----------|----------------|
| **Monofocal IOL** | Single focus (distance). Best distance vision; requires glasses for near (reading) or intermediate (computer). Most common, covered by insurance/Medicare. | Excellent contrast sensitivity; no glare/halos. |
| **Multifocal IOL** | Multiple rings for distance, intermediate, near. Reduced or eliminated need for glasses. | Increased glare/halos (ironic given cortical cataract patients already have glare). May not suit patients with pre-existing glare sensitivity. |
| **Extended depth of focus (EDOF) IOL** | Continuous range from distance to intermediate; some near. Fewer halos than multifocal. | Intermediate vision good; near may still need glasses. |
| **Toric IOL** | Corrects pre-existing corneal astigmatism. | Reduces need for post-op glasses for distance. |
| **Accommodating IOL** | Designed to shift position with ciliary muscle contraction. Limited range of accommodation. | Less commonly used; efficacy variable. |

**Shared decision-making**: Discuss lifestyle needs (night driving, computer use, reading) and tolerance for glasses.

### Postoperative Course

- **Day of surgery**: Eye patched or shielded; vision blurry.
- **Days 1–7**: Rapid vision improvement. Use prescribed antibiotic and anti-inflammatory drops (typically 4 weeks). Avoid rubbing eye, heavy lifting (>20 lbs), swimming, or getting water in eye.
- **Weeks 1–4**: Vision stabilizes. Glasses prescription can be updated after 4 weeks.
- **Complication rates**: Serious complications (endophthalmitis, retinal detachment) occur in <0.5% of healthy eyes.

## Lifestyle Recommendations

**Before surgery (non-surgical management)** :

- **Tinted lenses** – Yellow (F42), amber (F43), or orange-tinted glasses reduce glare and improve contrast. Can be fit over prescription glasses (fit-over sunglasses) or prescription tinted lenses.

- **Polarized sunglasses** – Reduce glare from reflections (windshields, water, snow). UV400 protection.

- **Anti-reflective (AR) coating** – On prescription glasses to reduce internal reflections and glare from lights.

- **Brighter task lighting** – Use gooseneck LED lamps (5000–6500K) for reading and close work.

- **Computer adjustments** – Use dark mode, increase contrast, reduce screen brightness, and use matte screen filters.

- **Night driving avoidance** – If glare from headlights is disabling, avoid night driving or limit to well-lit, familiar routes.

- **Maximize contrast** – Use black markers on white labels, white dishes on dark placemats, high-contrast tape on steps.

**After surgery** :

- **Use prescribed eye drops** – Antibiotics (prevent infection) and anti-inflammatories (prevent cystoid macular edema). Complete full course.
- **Wear eye shield at night** – First week to prevent inadvertent rubbing.
- **Avoid strenuous activity** – First week (walking allowed; no heavy lifting, bending with head below waist).
- **No swimming or hot tubs** – For 2 weeks (infection risk).
- **Attend follow-up appointments** – Day 1, week 1, month 1 (typical schedule).

## Treatment Limitations

- **Surgery does not reverse pre-existing retinal or optic nerve damage** – If concurrent glaucoma or macular degeneration is present, vision may remain limited post-surgery.

- **Posterior capsule opacification (PCO)** – Occurs in 20–30% of patients within 2–5 years. The posterior lens capsule becomes cloudy (fibrosis or regenerated lens cells). Treated with YAG laser capsulotomy (painless, 5-minute outpatient procedure). PCO is NOT a cataract recurrence (cataract cannot recur because the lens is removed).

- **Cystoid macular edema (CME)** – Occurs in 1–3% of uncomplicated surgeries. Fluid accumulates in the macula, causing blurred vision. Usually resolves with NSAID or steroid drops; rarely persists.

- **IOL limitations** – No IOL replicates the natural lens's dynamic accommodation perfectly. Most patients require glasses for some tasks (reading, computer, or distance, depending on IOL choice).

- **Progression of other cataract types** – If mixed cataract (nuclear + cortical), removing the cataract addresses all opacities because the entire lens is removed.

- **No pharmacologic prevention** – No drops to slow progression. Surgery remains the only cure.

## Prognosis

**Without surgery** :

- Cortical cataracts progress slowly over 5–10 years from early to severe.
- Visual acuity declines to 20/70–20/200 or worse.
- Glare becomes disabling; night driving impossible.
- Eventually interferes with basic activities (reading, face recognition, mobility).
- Does NOT cause permanent blindness if surgery is performed before severe vision loss.

**With cataract surgery** :

- **Visual acuity**: >95% of healthy eyes achieve 20/40 or better; >90% achieve 20/25 or better with monofocal IOLs (targeting distance).
- **Contrast sensitivity**: Returns to near-normal levels; glare and halos resolve.
- **Patient satisfaction**: >90% report improved quality of life and ability to perform daily activities.
- **Second eye surgery**: Often performed within weeks to months if both eyes have significant cataracts.

**Long-term prognosis**: Excellent. IOLs do not degrade over time. The eye may develop other age-related conditions (macular degeneration, glaucoma, diabetic retinopathy) unrelated to the cataract, requiring ongoing eye exams every 1–2 years.

**Special populations**:

- **Diabetic patients**: Good glycemic control improves surgical outcomes, but risk of diabetic retinopathy progression exists. Preoperative retinal evaluation recommended.
- **Glaucoma patients**: Cataract surgery may lower intraocular pressure slightly; however, pre-existing field loss limits visual potential.
- **Age >90 years**: Surgery is safe and effective if medically fit for local anesthesia.

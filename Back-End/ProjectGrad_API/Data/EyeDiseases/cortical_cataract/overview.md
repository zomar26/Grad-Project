# Cortical Cataract

## Definition

A cortical cataract is a type of age-related cataract that forms in the lens cortex—the outer layer of the eye's natural lens, surrounding the central nucleus. It appears as wedge-shaped or spoke-like opacities that extend from the peripheral lens toward the center, resembling the spokes of a wheel. Cortical cataracts are one of the three main types of age-related cataracts, along with nuclear sclerotic cataracts (central clouding) and posterior subcapsular cataracts (opacity at the back of the lens).

Cortical opacities affect vision primarily by scattering light rather than simply blocking it, leading to distinctive symptoms such as glare and difficulty with bright lights. They tend to progress slowly over years and are highly treatable with cataract surgery.

## How the Eye is Affected

**Affected structure**: The lens cortex—the soft, outer layer of the crystalline lens located between the lens capsule and the central nucleus. The cortex is composed of elongated, tightly packed lens fiber cells that are normally transparent due to precise cellular organization and high concentrations of crystallin proteins.

**Mechanism of opacification**:

1. **Oxidative stress** – With aging, oxidative damage accumulates in lens proteins and cell membranes. The cortex is particularly vulnerable because it is farther from the lens center and has lower antioxidant capacity than the nucleus.

2. **Disruption of lens fiber cell architecture** – Damage to cell membranes causes fluid imbalances. Water accumulates between lens fibers, forming vacuoles and clefts.

3. **Protein aggregation** – Oxidized crystallin proteins unfold and clump together, forming light-scattering complexes.

4. **Progressive spoke formation** – These opacities begin at the lens equator (periphery) and extend centrally along the planes between lens fiber cells, creating the characteristic radial, wedge-shaped spokes.

**Effect on vision**:
- Cortical opacities **scatter** incoming light rather than simply blocking it.
- Light scatter reduces contrast sensitivity and causes glare—particularly disabling under bright conditions (sunlight, oncoming headlights at night).
- Central vision may remain relatively preserved until spokes reach the visual axis.
- As opacities progress, the central lens becomes involved, causing blurred vision similar to other cataract types.

## Types or Stages

Cortical cataracts are typically classified by severity using the Lens Opacities Classification System (LOCS III), a standardized clinical grading system:

| Grade | LOCS III Standard | Clinical Appearance | Visual Impact |
|-------|------------------|---------------------|---------------|
| **C1** | Trace | Isolated vacuoles or small wedges confined to far periphery | Usually none |
| **C2** | Mild | Wedge-shaped opacities extending <1/3 of the distance from equator to center | Mild glare in bright light |
| **C3** | Moderate | Opacities extending 1/3 to 2/3 of the distance; early spoke formation | Noticeable glare, halos; vision 20/30–20/50 |
| **C4** | Marked | Opacities extending >2/3 but not involving central 3mm zone | Significant glare, reduced contrast, vision 20/40–20/70 |
| **C5** | Severe | Central 3mm zone involved; spokes may coalesce | Dense central blur, vision often 20/70–20/200 or worse |

**Progression staging for patient communication**:
- **Early**: Peripheral spokes only; no symptoms or mild glare.
- **Moderate**: Spokes reach near center; glare disabling; reading still possible.
- **Advanced**: Central vision blurred; may need surgery for daily activities.

**Note**: Cortical cataracts often coexist with nuclear sclerotic or posterior subcapsular cataracts (mixed cataracts). Pure cortical cataracts are less common than mixed types in older adults.

## Key Facts

- **Prevalence**: Cortical opacities are present in approximately 20–30% of adults aged 65–75 and increase to over 50% in those over 80. Pure cortical cataracts are less common than mixed forms.

- **Pathognomonic symptom**: Disabling glare and light scatter—patients report being "blinded" by sunlight or oncoming headlights, sometimes more than they complain of blurred vision.

- **Progression rate**: Generally slow, taking 5–10 years to progress from early to visually significant opacity. Some rapid progressors (especially with diabetes) may worsen over 1–3 years.

- **Contrast sensitivity affected early**: Visual acuity may be 20/20–20/30, but patients struggle with low-contrast tasks (reading gray print, navigating in fog, seeing steps in dim light).

- **Associated with diabetes**: People with diabetes develop cortical cataracts at younger ages and with faster progression due to osmotic stress from sorbitol accumulation in lens fibers.

- **Surgery is curative**: Cataract surgery with intraocular lens (IOL) implantation completely removes the opacified lens material and restores vision. Success rate exceeds 95% in healthy eyes.

- **UV-B exposure link**: Lifetime cumulative exposure to ultraviolet B (UV-B) sunlight is a major risk factor for cortical cataracts, more so than for nuclear cataracts.

- **Aspirin controversy**: High-dose aspirin was once thought to protect against cortical cataracts, but large randomized trials have not confirmed this association.

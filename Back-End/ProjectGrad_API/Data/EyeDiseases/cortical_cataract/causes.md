# Causes and Risk Factors of Cortical Cataract

## Main Causes

The primary cause of cortical cataract is **oxidative damage** to lens fiber cell membranes and crystallin proteins, leading to water accumulation, protein aggregation, and light-scattering opacities. Unlike nuclear cataracts (which involve protein cross-linking and yellowing), cortical cataracts predominantly result from membrane damage and fluid imbalance.

**Key pathophysiologic mechanisms**:

1. **Membrane lipid peroxidation** – Free radicals (reactive oxygen species) attack unsaturated fatty acids in lens fiber cell membranes, disrupting membrane integrity and ion transport.

2. **Calcium dysregulation** – Damaged membranes allow calcium influx. Elevated intracellular calcium activates proteases (calpains) that degrade lens proteins.

3. **Water accumulation (vacuoles)** – Disrupted sodium-potassium ATPase pumps lead to intracellular water accumulation, forming vacuoles and clefts between lens fibers.

4. **Sorbitol pathway activation (in diabetes)** – Excess glucose is converted to sorbitol by aldose reductase. Sorbitol accumulates in lens fibers (poorly permeable), creating osmotic stress and water influx.

5. **Crystallin aggregation** – Oxidized crystallins unfold and aggregate, forming insoluble light-scattering complexes.

## Risk Factors

### Non-modifiable Risk Factors

- **Advanced age** – The strongest risk factor. Cortical opacities increase from approximately 10% at age 55 to over 50% by age 80.

- **Female sex** – Women have higher rates of cortical cataracts than men, possibly due to longer life expectancy and hormonal factors (estrogen may have protective effects).

- **Race/ethnicity** – Cortical cataracts are more common in African Americans and Hispanic Americans than in Caucasians, unlike nuclear cataracts which are more common in Caucasians.

- **Family history** – Having a first-degree relative with cortical cataracts increases risk 2- to 3-fold. Twin studies suggest heritability of 35–50% for cortical cataracts.

### Modifiable Risk Factors

- **Ultraviolet B (UV-B) light exposure** – Lifetime cumulative exposure to sunlight UV-B is a major, modifiable risk factor. Risk increases with outdoor occupation, residence at lower latitudes, and lack of sunglass or hat use. UV-B damages lens epithelial cells and generates free radicals.

- **Diabetes mellitus** – Increases risk 2–5 fold, with stronger association for cortical than nuclear cataracts. Poor glycemic control (elevated HbA1c) accelerates progression. Younger age at diabetes onset increases lifetime risk.

- **Cigarette smoking** – Increases cortical cataract risk 1.5–2.5 fold, dose-dependent. Smoking reduces systemic antioxidants and increases oxidative stress. Risk declines after quitting but may remain elevated for decades.

- **Heavy alcohol consumption** – >2 drinks/day increases risk; moderate intake (1 drink/day) shows no consistent association.

- **Hypertension** – Chronic high blood pressure is associated with a 1.5–2 fold increased risk, possibly through vascular damage and oxidative stress.

- **Obesity (BMI ≥30)** – Increases risk through oxidative stress, systemic inflammation, and diabetes association.

- **Poor nutritional status** – Low dietary intake of antioxidants (vitamins C and E, carotenoids) and omega-3 fatty acids is associated with higher risk.

- **Corticosteroid use** – Systemic or high-dose inhaled corticosteroids increase cataract risk (all types, including cortical). Dose-dependent effect; longer duration increases risk.

- **Dehydration** – Severe or recurrent dehydration (e.g., from cholera, heat stroke) is associated with increased cortical cataract risk in some studies, though rare in developed countries.

### Medications and Treatments

- **Statins** – Mixed evidence; some studies suggest protective effects, others no association.
- **Aspirin** – No consistent protective effect; high-dose aspirin not recommended for cataract prevention.
- **Postmenopausal hormone therapy** – Unclear association; some studies suggest decreased risk, others no effect.

## Genetic Factors

- **Heritability** – Cortical cataracts have a heritability estimate of 35–50%, higher than nuclear cataracts but lower than posterior subcapsular cataracts.

- **Candidate genes** – Variants in genes related to:
    - **Antioxidant defense**: GSTM1, GSTT1 (glutathione S-transferases), SOD2 (superoxide dismutase)
    - **Lens crystallins**: CRYAA, CRYAB (alpha-crystallin chaperone function)
    - **Membrane transport**: ATP1A1 (sodium-potassium ATPase)
    - **Aldose reductase**: AKR1B1 (sorbitol pathway, particularly in diabetes)

- **Age-related cataract risk loci** – Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified multiple loci associated with cortical cataracts, including regions near CRYAA, CRYGS, and EPHA2.

- **Monogenic cataracts (rare)** – Cortical cataracts can present as part of early-onset (childhood or young adult) inherited cataract syndromes, often with mutations in lens crystallin genes (CRYAA, CRYAB, CRYGC, CRYGD).

## Environmental Factors

- **Sunlight exposure** – High cumulative UV-B exposure (e.g., outdoor workers, residents near equator) increases risk. UV-A exposure shows weaker association. Protective eyewear (sunglasses blocking 99–100% UV) reduces risk.

- **Air pollution** – Limited evidence; some studies suggest fine particulate matter (PM2.5) may increase cataract risk through systemic oxidative stress.

- **Occupational radiation exposure** – Infrared radiation (glassblowers, steel workers) and ionizing radiation (radiologists without adequate protection) increase risk, though modern safety standards have reduced this.

## Prevention Strategies

**Primary prevention (preventing onset)** :

- **UV-B protection** – Wear wraparound sunglasses labeled "99–100% UV protection" or "UV400" when outdoors. Wide-brimmed hats add protection. Most effective during childhood and young adulthood (cumulative exposure matters).

- **Smoking cessation** – Reduces risk over time; benefit increases with years since quitting.

- **Diabetes control** – Maintain HbA1c <7% (or as individually targeted). Tight glycemic control slows cataract progression and reduces risk of developing new opacities.

- **Antioxidant-rich diet** – High intake of fruits and vegetables, particularly dark leafy greens (spinach, kale), citrus fruits (vitamin C), and colorful vegetables (carotenoids: lutein, zeaxanthin).

- **Maintain healthy weight** – BMI 18.5–24.9 reduces diabetes and oxidative stress risk.

- **Control blood pressure** – Target <130/80 mmHg (per general guidelines).

- **Moderate alcohol** – Limit to ≤1 drink/day for women, ≤2 for men.

- **Routine eye exams** – Every 1–2 years after age 65, earlier if risk factors present.

**Secondary prevention (slowing progression)** :

- **Address modifiable risk factors** – Smoking cessation, diabetes control, UV protection remain important after diagnosis.

- **No proven pharmacologic treatments** – No eye drops, supplements, or medications have been shown to reverse or halt cortical cataract progression in rigorous trials. AREDS2 supplements (for AMD) do not prevent cataracts.

- **Manage glare with tinted lenses** – Yellow-tinted or amber-tinted glasses reduce glare and improve contrast in early to moderate cortical cataracts, but do not slow progression.

- **Avoid unnecessary corticosteroid use** – Use lowest effective dose, shortest duration, and consider non-steroidal alternatives when possible.

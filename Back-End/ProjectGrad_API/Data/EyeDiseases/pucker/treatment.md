# Treatment of Macular Pucker

## Available Treatments

The only definitive treatment for symptomatic macular pucker (epiretinal membrane, ERM) is **pars plana vitrectomy with epiretinal membrane peeling**. No medications, eye drops, or supplements reverse or halt ERM progression.

**Management algorithm**:

- **Asymptomatic ERM (cellophane maculopathy, no vision loss or distortion)** : Observation only. Annual dilated exam with OCT.
- **Mildly symptomatic ERM (vision 20/25–20/40, mild metamorphopsia)** : Observation preferred. Surgery only if symptoms are disabling to the patient (e.g., affects occupation, driving, reading).
- **Moderate to severe symptomatic ERM (vision ≤20/50–20/80, significant metamorphopsia)** : Consider vitrectomy with ERM peeling. Surgery improves metamorphopsia in 80–90% and visual acuity in 60–80%.
- **ERM with macular edema, lamellar hole, or vitreomacular traction** : Strongly consider surgery to prevent progression to macular hole or permanent vision loss.

## Medications

**No FDA-approved medications for ERM.** Off-label uses:

- **Anti-VEGF injections (ranibizumab, aflibercept, bevacizumab)** – For ERM-associated **cystoid macular edema (CME)** . May temporarily reduce edema and improve vision, but does not treat the underlying membrane. Used as a temporizing measure before vitrectomy or in patients who decline surgery.

- **Intravitreal corticosteroids (dexamethasone implant [Ozurdex], triamcinolone, fluocinolone [Iluvien])** – For refractory CME from ERM. More effective than anti-VEGF for inflammatory CME, but increase intraocular pressure (glaucoma risk) and accelerate cataract. Used rarely and off-label.

- **Topical NSAIDs or corticosteroids** – No benefit for ERM.

- **Ocriplasmin (Jetrea)** – An intravitreal enzyme that dissolves vitreous adhesions. **Not effective for ERM** (indicated for vitreomacular traction without ERM). Do not use for ERM.

**Limitations of medical therapy**:
- Does not remove or halt the membrane.
- Effects are temporary (weeks to months).
- Repeated injections carry risks (endophthalmitis, cataract progression, retinal detachment).
- Surgery is the only definitive treatment.

## Surgical Options

### Pars Plana Vitrectomy with ERM Peeling (Standard of Care)

**Indications for surgery** (AAO Preferred Practice Pattern guidelines):

1. **Vision loss** – Typically ≤20/50 or worse, but earlier (20/30–20/40) if symptoms are disabling (e.g., occupation requires fine vision, driving unsafe due to distortion).
2. **Disabling metamorphopsia** – Even with good visual acuity, significant distortion affecting quality of life is a valid indication.
3. **ERM with vitreomacular traction (VMT)** – To prevent progression to macular hole.
4. **ERM with lamellar macular hole or foveal detachment** – To prevent full-thickness hole.
5. **Progressive ERM** – Documented worsening over 6–12 months on OCT.

**Procedure details** (same-day outpatient surgery, 30–60 minutes):

1. **Anesthesia** – Local (retrobulbar or peribulbar block) or topical with intracameral anesthesia. General anesthesia for anxious patients or children (rare).
2. **Vitrectomy** – 23, 25, or 27 gauge pars plana vitrectomy. Removal of the vitreous gel (especially the posterior hyaloid if still attached).
3. **Staining the ERM** – Dye (brilliant blue G, triamcinolone, or MembraneBlue) applied to visualize the thin, transparent membrane.
4. **ERM peeling** – Fine forceps used to grasp and peel the membrane from the retinal surface. Often performed with **internal limiting membrane (ILM) peeling** (peeling an additional layer beneath the ERM) to reduce recurrence risk.
5. **Tamponade (gas or air)** – In uncomplicated ERM, air or balanced salt solution (no tamponade) is used. Gas (SF6 or C3F8) used if a macular hole is present or retinal detachment occurs. Patients with gas must avoid air travel and high altitude (gas expands) and cannot have subsequent cataract surgery until gas resolves (2–8 weeks).
6. **Closure** – Sutureless (valved) incisions; no sutures required.

**Outcomes**:
- **Metamorphopsia improvement**: 80–90% of patients experience significant improvement (often within days to weeks).
- **Visual acuity improvement**: 60–80% gain ≥2 lines (10 ETDRS letters) at 6–12 months. Mean improvement: 2–4 lines (10–20 letters).
- **Final vision**: 70–80% achieve 20/40 or better. However, vision rarely improves to 20/20 if baseline was worse than 20/40 preoperatively.
- **Time to maximal improvement**: 3–6 months (vision continues to improve for up to 12 months).

**Complications** (rates per 1000 cases):

| Complication | Rate | Notes |
|--------------|------|-------|
| Cataract progression | 100% at 12–24 months (phakic eyes) | Nearly all phakic eyes develop significant cataract within 2 years; cataract surgery required. |
| Posterior capsule opacification (PCO) | 20–30% (after cataract surgery) | YAG laser capsulotomy. |
| Retinal detachment | 1–3% | Rhegmatogenous; treatable with additional surgery. |
| Cystoid macular edema (CME) | 1–5% | Usually responds to NSAID/steroid drops; persistent cases may require injections. |
| Endophthalmitis | 0.05% (1 in 2000) | Severe intraocular infection; rare but sight-threatening. |
| Intraocular pressure elevation | 2–10% | Transient (gas or steroid response); treat with drops. |
| ERM recurrence | 5–10% (within 2–5 years) | Repeat vitrectomy successful in most cases. |
| Subretinal hemorrhage | <1% | During peeling; usually self-limited. |
| Macular hole formation | <1% | Intraoperative; repaired with gas tamponade. |

**Special consideration: Combined vitrectomy + cataract surgery**:

- In patients with significant nuclear sclerosis (cataract) and ERM, **combined surgery** (phacoemulsification + IOL + vitrectomy + ERM peeling) is performed.
- Advantages: Single surgery, avoids two separate procedures, faster visual recovery.
- Disadvantages: Slightly longer surgery, higher risk of postoperative inflammation and CME (but acceptable).
- For patients with clear lenses: Vitrectomy alone accelerates cataract formation (100% within 2 years). These patients will need cataract surgery later. Combined surgery is often recommended for patients aged ≥60 with any lens opacity.

### Alternative/Historically Used Procedures (Not Recommended)

- **Laser photocoagulation** – Not effective; causes retinal scarring and worsens vision.
- **Subretinal membrane surgery** – For submacular membranes (different entity); not for epiretinal membranes.
- **Medical therapy** (anti-VEGF, steroids) – No long-term benefit as monotherapy.

## Lifestyle Recommendations

**Before surgery (non-surgical management)** :

- **Amsler grid monitoring** – Monthly testing in each eye separately to detect progression of metamorphopsia.
- **Optimize refraction** – Updated glasses may improve vision slightly (but cannot correct metamorphopsia).
- **Tinted lenses (yellow/amber)** – May improve contrast and reduce glare (though ERM does not typically cause disabling glare like cataracts).
- **Magnification devices** – Handheld or electronic magnifiers for reading.
- **Large-print materials** – Books, e-readers with adjustable font.
- **Dark mode on screens** – May reduce perceived distortion for some patients.
- **Avoid unnecessary cataract surgery** – If both cataract and ERM are present, consider combined surgery rather than cataract surgery alone (which may worsen ERM symptoms by improving clarity of the distortion).

**After surgery** :

- **Postoperative drops** – Antibiotics (1 week) and steroids/NSAIDs (4 weeks).
- **Positioning** – If gas tamponade was used (rare for uncomplicated ERM), face-down or strict positioning for 1–14 days depending on gas type and indication (macular hole repair). For routine ERM without hole: no positioning required.
- **No air travel or high altitude** – If gas tamponade (SF6: avoid for 2–3 weeks; C3F8: avoid for 6–8 weeks). Gas expands at altitude, causing dangerously high intraocular pressure.
- **Avoid heavy lifting (>20 lbs), strenuous exercise, swimming** – For 1–2 weeks.
- **No eye rubbing** – For 2–4 weeks.
- **Expect gradual improvement** – Vision and distortion improve over 3–12 months. Do not expect immediate perfection.

## Treatment Limitations

- **No medical therapy** – Surgery is the only definitive treatment.
- **Surgery risks** – Cataract, retinal detachment, infection, CME, recurrence.
- **Cataract inevitability** – 100% of phakic eyes develop significant cataract within 2 years after vitrectomy. Requires subsequent cataract surgery (or combined surgery upfront).
- **Incomplete symptom resolution** – 10–20% of patients have residual metamorphopsia despite successful ERM peeling (due to permanent photoreceptor displacement or retinal scarring).
- **Vision rarely returns to 20/20** if baseline was worse than 20/40; mean final vision 20/30–20/40.
- **Recurrence** – 5–10% require repeat vitrectomy.
- **Quality of life** – Most patients are satisfied, but unrealistic expectations (e.g., "perfect vision") lead to disappointment. Discuss realistic outcomes preoperatively.

## Prognosis

### Natural History (without surgery)

| ERM Severity | Progression Rate | Spontaneous Improvement | Vision Outcomes |
|--------------|------------------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Cellophane (asymptomatic) | 10–20% progress over 5 years | <1% | 80–90% remain stable, vision 20/20–20/30 |
| Mild symptomatic (20/25–20/40) | 20–30% progress over 5–10 years | 2–5% (if PVD completes) | 70% stable; 30% worsen to 20/50–20/80 |
| Moderate (20/50–20/80) | 30–50% progress over 5 years | <1% | 50–70% worsen to 20/100 or worse |
| Severe with CME or lamellar hole | 50–70% progress over 3–5 years | Rare | 80% worsen; macular hole may develop |

### With Vitrectomy + ERM Peeling

| Outcome | Percentage | Timeframe |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Metamorphopsia improvement | 80–90% | Days to 3 months |
| Visual acuity gain (≥2 lines) | 60–80% | 6–12 months |
| Final vision 20/40 or better | 70–80% | 12 months |
| Final vision 20/20 or better | 20–30% | 12 months (if baseline good) |
| No improvement (stable) | 10–20% | 12 months |
| Vision worse than pre-op | 2–5% | Due to complications |
| ERM recurrence | 5–10% (at 5 years) | Requires repeat surgery |
| Cataract surgery required (phakic eyes) | 100% | Within 2 years |

**Factors associated with better prognosis**:
- Preoperative vision ≥20/50 (better baseline)
- Shorter symptom duration (<12–24 months)
- No pre-existing macular edema or lamellar hole
- Complete ERM removal with ILM peeling
- Younger age (<70 years)
- No other ocular comorbidities (glaucoma, AMD, diabetic retinopathy)

**Factors associated with worse prognosis**:
- Preoperative vision ≤20/100
- Long-standing ERM (>2–3 years) with chronic retinal changes
- ERM with lamellar macular hole or macular edema
- Age >80 years
- Concurrent macular degeneration or glaucoma
- Surgical complications (retinal detachment, CME, hemorrhage)

**Key message**: Macular pucker surgery is highly effective for improving distortion and vision, but it is not without risks (cataract, retinal detachment, recurrence). Surgery is elective and should be considered when symptoms significantly impact quality of life. Most patients with mild symptoms do not require surgery. Discuss realistic expectations with your retina specialist.

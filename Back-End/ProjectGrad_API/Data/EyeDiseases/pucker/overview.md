# Macular Pucker

## Definition

A macular pucker, also known as epiretinal membrane (ERM) or cellophane maculopathy, is a thin, semi-transparent layer of scar tissue that forms on the surface of the macula—the central part of the retina responsible for sharp, detailed vision. As this membrane contracts, it wrinkles, puckers, or distorts the underlying retinal tissue, leading to metamorphopsia (visual distortion) and reduced central vision.

The term "cellophane maculopathy" refers to early, mild membranes that appear like wrinkled cellophane wrap on the macula without significant distortion or vision loss. "Macular pucker" implies a thicker, more contractile membrane that causes visible retinal wrinkling and functional impairment. The condition is usually unilateral (affecting one eye) but can occur bilaterally in 10–30% of cases.

## How the Eye is Affected

**Affected structures**: The inner retinal surface (internal limiting membrane, or ILM) and the underlying neurosensory retina (particularly the macula).

**Mechanism of membrane formation**:

1. **Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD)** – With aging, the vitreous gel shrinks and peels away from the retina (PVD). This is a normal process in most people, but in some cases, residual vitreous cortical cells (hyalocytes) or glial cells remain attached to the retina.

2. **Cell migration and proliferation** – These residual cells (astrocytes, Müller cells, fibroblasts, and hyalocytes) migrate across the retinal surface and begin to proliferate, forming a cellular membrane.

3. **Collagen deposition** – The cells produce extracellular matrix components, including collagen types I, II, and IV, and fibronectin, creating a fibrous membrane.

4. **Contraction** – Myofibroblast-like cells within the membrane contract, similar to scar tissue elsewhere in the body. This contraction exerts tangential traction (pulling sideways) on the underlying retina.

5. **Retinal distortion** – The tangential traction causes:
    - **Retinal wrinkling**: Fine folds in the retinal surface (like crumpled cellophane)
    - **Vascular distortion**: Retinal blood vessels become tortuous, straightened, or pulled toward the membrane
    - **Foveal ectopia**: Displacement of the fovea (central pit) from its normal position
    - **Macular edema**: Fluid accumulation from mechanical stress on retinal capillaries
    - **Foveoschisis (rare)**: Splitting of retinal layers from chronic traction

**Effect on vision**:

- **Metamorphopsia**: Straight lines appear wavy, bent, or crooked—the most characteristic symptom. Caused by mechanical distortion of photoreceptor distribution.
- **Reduced visual acuity**: When the membrane involves the fovea (central pit), sharpness decreases. Mild: 20/25–20/40; moderate: 20/50–20/100; severe: 20/200 or worse (rare).
- **Micropsia or macropsia**: Objects may appear smaller (micropsia) due to retinal crowding or larger (macropsia) from foveal ectopia.
- **Reduced contrast sensitivity**: Difficulty distinguishing objects from background.

## Types or Stages

Macular pucker is classified by clinical appearance (on fundoscopy and optical coherence tomography) and visual impact.

### By Clinical Appearance (Fundoscopy / OCT)

| Stage | OCT Findings | Fundus Appearance | Visual Symptoms |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Stage 0: Cellophane maculopathy** | Thin, flat membrane; no retinal distortion; no foveal involvement | Glinting, translucent membrane without wrinkles | None or minimal |
| **Stage 1: Mild ERM** | Thin membrane with mild retinal surface irregularities; foveal contour preserved | Wrinkled cellophane appearance; mild vascular tortuosity | Mild metamorphopsia; vision 20/20–20/30 |
| **Stage 2: Moderate ERM** | Thicker membrane; retinal striae (wrinkles) and foveal flattening; possible foveal detachment | Opaque or semi-opaque membrane; visible retinal folds; vascular dragging | Metamorphopsia prominent; vision 20/30–20/70 |
| **Stage 3: Severe ERM (with macular edema)** | Dense membrane; significant retinal thickening (edema); foveal pseudohole possible | Dense, white-ish membrane; marked vascular tortuosity; blurred foveal reflex | Metamorphopsia severe; vision 20/50–20/200 |
| **Stage 4: ERM with lamellar macular hole** | ERM with partial-thickness foveal defect; intact outer retina | ERM with central "schitic" or "split" appearance | Vision 20/80–20/200 |

### By Etiology

- **Primary (idiopathic)** – No identifiable cause (most common, 70–80%). Usually occurs after age 50, with peak incidence 65–75 years. Associated with posterior vitreous detachment (PVD).

- **Secondary** – Associated with underlying ocular conditions:
    - **Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD)** – Most common secondary cause
    - **Retinal tear or detachment** – Post-surgical or post-laser
    - **Uveitis** – Chronic inflammation
    - **Retinal vascular disease** – Diabetic retinopathy, retinal vein occlusion
    - **Ocular trauma** – Blunt or penetrating injury
    - **Intraocular surgery** – Cataract surgery, vitrectomy, retinal detachment repair
    - **Retinal laser photocoagulation** – Scarring response
    - **Retinitis pigmentosa** – Associated in some cases

### By OCT-based Morphology (Contemporary Classification)

- **ERM with foveal sparing** – Membrane does not cover the fovea; good prognosis.
- **ERM with foveal involvement** – Membrane covers the fovea; vision more affected.
- **ERM with foveal pseudohole** – Membrane contracts, pulling retinal tissue peripherally, creating a central "hole" that is not a true full-thickness hole.
- **ERM with lamellar macular hole** – Partial-thickness defect from chronic traction.
- **ERM with cystoid macular edema (CME)** – Fluid-filled cysts from capillary leakage.

## Key Facts

- **Prevalence**: Macular pucker is common, especially with aging. Present in 2–7% of people aged 50–59, increasing to 10–20% of those aged 75–85 (autopsy and OCT-based studies). The majority (60–80%) remain stable or progress very slowly.

- **Bilaterality**: 10–30% of patients develop ERM in both eyes, often asymmetric.

- **Progression**: Most ERMs (50–70%) do not progress significantly. Only 10–20% progress to vision loss (worse than 20/40) over 5–10 years. Spontaneous resolution is rare (<1%) but can occur after posterior vitreous detachment completion.

- **Metamorphopsia > acuity**: The most bothersome symptom is usually distortion, not blur. Patients may have 20/20–20/30 vision but significant waviness that interferes with reading, driving, and face recognition.

- **No medical treatment**: No eye drops, medications, or supplements reverse or halt macular pucker progression.

- **Vitrectomy surgery is effective**: Pars plana vitrectomy with ERM peeling improves metamorphopsia in 80–90% and visual acuity in 60–80% of symptomatic patients. Surgery is elective and typically delayed unless vision is significantly impaired or distortion is disabling.

- **Risk factors**: Age, posterior vitreous detachment, cataract surgery (3–5% risk of developing ERM within 2–5 years post-operatively), retinal tears/detachments, uveitis, and trauma.

- **Spontaneous improvement**: Some mild ERMs (cellophane maculopathy) may remain stable or even improve if the vitreous completely detaches and releases traction.

- **Post-operative outcomes**: After vitrectomy with ERM peeling, 80–90% of patients experience improved metamorphopsia, and 60–80% gain ≥2 lines of visual acuity. However, cataract formation occurs in nearly 100% of phakic (natural lens) eyes within 6–24 months, requiring subsequent cataract surgery.

- **Recurrence rate**: ERM recurs in 5–10% of eyes after successful peeling, usually within 2–5 years. Repeat vitrectomy is effective.

# Symptoms of Macular Pucker

## Common Symptoms

The hallmark symptom of macular pucker is **metamorphopsia** (visual distortion). Unlike many retinal diseases that cause primarily blur, macular pucker distorts the shape of objects. Symptoms typically develop gradually over months to years, though some patients report sudden onset associated with acute posterior vitreous detachment.

- **Metamorphopsia (wavy or crooked vision)** – The most characteristic and bothersome symptom. Straight lines appear:
    - Wavy, bent, or crooked (e.g., door frames, window blinds, telephone poles, text lines)
    - Curved inward or outward (like looking into a funhouse mirror)
    - Irregular or "jagged" (particularly with higher magnification)
- **Testing**: Patients often first notice this on an Amsler grid (a checkerboard-like pattern). Lines that should be straight appear wavy, missing, or broken.

- **Micropsia (objects appear smaller)** – Due to retinal wrinkling and crowding. Patients report:
    - Text on a page looks compacted or "squeezed together"
    - A coffee cup appears miniature
    - Icons on a phone screen look smaller than normal
- **Macropsia (objects appear larger)** – Less common; occurs when traction pulls the fovea, stretching photoreceptors apart.

- **Blurred or reduced central vision (unilateral)** – Typically mild to moderate (20/25–20/80) in most cases. Severe vision loss (20/200 or worse) is uncommon (<10% of symptomatic cases) and suggests complications (macular edema, lamellar hole, or underlying macular disease).

- **Monocular diplopia or polyopia** – Seeing double, triple, or multiple images with one eye. The ghost images may be overlapping or separated. Caused by irregular retinal contour creating multiple focal points.

- **Tortuous or distorted vision of straight edges** – Door frames, window frames, rulers, and tile lines appear crooked. Reading text: letters may look misshapen, and lines of text may appear wavy or to "dip" in the middle.

- **Reduced contrast sensitivity** – Difficulty distinguishing objects from background, particularly in low light or low-contrast situations (e.g., seeing steps against a dark floor, reading gray text on white paper).

- **Central or paracentral scotoma (rare)** – A dark, gray, or hazy spot in the central or slightly off-center vision. Suggests significant retinal thinning, lamellar hole, or macular edema.

- **Photopsia (flashing lights)** – Brief flashes in the peripheral vision, often associated with acute posterior vitreous detachment (the underlying cause of many ERMs). If new, warrants urgent evaluation to rule out retinal tear.

- **Floaters (common in early stage)** – New floaters (spots, cobwebs, or strands) often accompany the posterior vitreous detachment that triggers ERM formation. **A sudden shower of many floaters with flashes** suggests retinal tear—an emergency.

- **Asymptomatic (very common)** – Many patients (especially those with thin "cellophane" membranes) have no symptoms and are diagnosed incidentally on routine eye exam. Up to 70% of ERMs found on screening OCT are asymptomatic.

## Advanced Symptoms

As the epiretinal membrane thickens, contracts further, or causes complications:

- **Progressive worsening of metamorphopsia** – Distortion becomes more pronounced, affecting central vision. Straight lines may appear dramatically wavy or "pulled" toward the center.

- **Worsening visual acuity** – Declines from 20/40 to 20/80–20/200 over years. Reading becomes impossible without magnification.

- **Macular edema (fluid accumulation)** – Causes additional blur, reduced contrast, and metamorphopsia. Vision often fluctuates (worse in the morning, improving during the day? Not typical; CME is usually constant).

- **Lamellar macular hole** – Partial-thickness foveal defect from chronic traction. Vision typically 20/60–20/200 with severe metamorphopsia.

- **Full-thickness macular hole (rare)** – Complete foveal defect from persistent tangential traction. Sudden worsening of central vision to 20/200 or worse, often with a dense central scotoma. Requires surgical repair (vitrectomy with internal limiting membrane peeling and gas tamponade).

- **Foveal detachment (rare)** – Serous detachment of the fovea from traction. Vision drops to 20/100–20/400. May progress to macular hole if untreated.

- **Chronic cystoid macular edema (CME)** – Persistent fluid-filled cysts; vision 20/50–20/200. May require anti-VEGF or corticosteroid injections (off-label).

- **Epiretinal membrane with vitreomacular traction (VMT)** – Adherent vitreous pulling on the macula, causing severe distortion, edema, and reduced vision. May require vitrectomy or ocriplasmin injection (though ocriplasmin is rarely used today due to side effects).

## When to Seek Medical Attention

**Routine (schedule eye exam within weeks to months)** :

- **New or worsening metamorphopsia** (wavy vision) – The most common reason for ERM diagnosis
- **Gradual blurring of central vision** in one eye (not both)
- **Micropsia or macropsia** (objects appearing smaller or larger)
- **Difficulty reading** small print or seeing fine details, especially if you have had prior posterior vitreous detachment or cataract surgery
- **Asymptomatic but at risk** – Annual dilated exam for patients with known ERM to monitor progression

**Urgent (same day or within 24–48 hours)** – These symptoms are **not typical of ERM alone** and warrant urgent evaluation to rule out retinal tear, detachment, or other emergencies:

- **Sudden increase in floaters**, especially a "shower" of many small floaters (like pepper or soot)
- **New flashes of light** (photopsia) that persist or recur
- **A dark curtain, shadow, or veil** across any part of the visual field (peripheral or central)
- **Sudden, painless vision loss** over hours or days (not months)
- **New dense central scotoma** (black spot in central vision)

**Emergency (go to emergency department immediately)** :

- Vision loss accompanied by eye pain (unusual for ERM; suggests uveitis, acute glaucoma, or endophthalmitis)
- Trauma to the eye with vision changes

**Special note**: If you have known ERM and develop **sudden worsening** of vision or distortion, this could indicate:
- Acute posterior vitreous detachment (with or without retinal tear)
- Development of macular hole
- Vitreomacular traction worsening

Do not wait for a routine appointment—call your ophthalmologist immediately.

## Symptoms That May Affect Daily Activities

| Activity | Potential Symptom Impact |
|----------|--------------------------|
| Reading | Words break or bend; lines of text appear wavy; micropsia makes text look smaller; need for magnification or large print; monocular diplopia creates ghost images of letters |
| Recognizing faces | Distortion may cause facial features to appear misshapen; mild ERM may have minimal effect, but moderate ERM impairs recognition |
| Driving | Metamorphopsia distorts distances (e.g., judging distance to car ahead); wavy road markings; micropsia makes road signs appear smaller; night driving less affected (unless glare from other causes) |
| Computer work | Lines of text or grid lines (spreadsheets) appear wavy; icons look misshapen; micropsia makes fonts appear smaller; eye strain from trying to "straighten" wavy lines |
| Watching television | Subtitles may be wavy; faces appear distorted; horizontal or vertical lines (e.g., goal lines, net cords) appear bent |
| Using smartphones/tablets | Icons and text wavy or shrunken; viewing photos (straight edges like buildings or horizons appear bent) |
| Reading analog clocks | The hands and numbers may appear distorted; difficult to tell exact time |
| Playing sports (golf, tennis) | Wavy vision affects depth perception and ball tracking; micropsia makes the ball or hole appear smaller |
| Walking on uneven ground | Metamorphopsia may cause misjudgment of step heights or distances; reduced contrast sensitivity worsens depth perception |
| Home repair/DIY (measuring, leveling) | Inability to trust that lines (e.g., level, ruler, chalk line) are straight; measurement errors |
| Taking medications | Difficulty reading small-print labels (especially if micropsia present) |
| Occupational tasks | Surgeons, machinists, artists, pilots, drivers, and anyone requiring fine visual discrimination may be significantly affected |
| Hobbies (sewing, painting, woodworking) | Distortion makes straight cuts, straight seams, or precise lines impossible; micropsia alters perceived size of workpieces |
| Reading street signs while driving | Wavy vision and micropsia make signs difficult to read at distance |
| Walking in unfamiliar environments | Wavy floors, curbs, or steps may be disorienting; increased fall risk |

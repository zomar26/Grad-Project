# Causes and Risk Factors of Macular Pucker

## Main Causes

Macular pucker (epiretinal membrane, ERM) results from **proliferation and contraction of fibrocellular tissue on the inner retinal surface**. The initiating event is almost always a **posterior vitreous detachment (PVD)** —the age-related separation of the vitreous gel from the retina—or trauma/surgery that disrupts the vitreoretinal interface.

**Primary (idiopathic) ERM** (70–80% of cases):
- Caused by **incomplete or anomalous PVD**. As the vitreous separates, residual cortical vitreous cells (hyalocytes) and glial cells remain attached to the retina.
- These cells migrate onto the retinal surface, proliferate, and lay down collagen, forming a membrane.
- The membrane contracts over months to years, wrinkling the underlying retina.

**Secondary ERM** (20–30%):
- Associated with conditions that cause breakdown of the blood-retinal barrier and release of growth factors (TGF-β, PDGF, VEGF), stimulating cell migration and proliferation.

**Common secondary causes**:
1. **Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD)** – The most common overall cause (both primary and secondary). However, secondary ERM implies an abnormal or complicated PVD.
2. **Retinal tear or detachment** – Inflammation and proliferation from retinal breaks.
3. **Intraocular surgery** – Cataract surgery (3–5% risk of ERM within 2–5 years), vitrectomy, retinal detachment repair, glaucoma surgery.
4. **Ocular trauma** – Blunt or penetrating injury disrupts the vitreoretinal interface.
5. **Uveitis (intraocular inflammation)** – Chronic inflammation releases cytokines that stimulate ERM formation.
6. **Retinal vascular disease** – Diabetic retinopathy, retinal vein occlusion (hemorrhage and inflammation).
7. **Retinal laser photocoagulation** – Scarring response triggers cell migration.
8. **Retinitis pigmentosa** – Associated in some cases (10–20%).
9. **Ocular tumors** – Rare; choroidal melanoma or retinoblastoma may cause secondary ERM.

## Risk Factors

### Non-modifiable Risk Factors

- **Advanced age** – The strongest risk factor. Prevalence increases from 2–7% at age 50–59 to 10–20% at age 75–85. Age-related PVD is the driver.

- **Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD)** – Present in 80–90% of eyes with idiopathic ERM. Most PVDs occur without complication, but 5–10% lead to ERM formation.

- **Female sex** – 2–3 times higher risk than males. Reason unknown, possibly hormonal (estrogen effects on vitreous and inflammation) or longer life expectancy.

- **Caucasian race** – Higher prevalence in Caucasians than in African Americans or Asians (in some studies).

- **Myopia (nearsightedness)** – High myopia (> -6D) increases risk of PVD and ERM, but low to moderate myopia may be protective (no clear consensus).

### Modifiable Risk Factors (Limited)

- **Previous cataract surgery** – 3–5% risk of developing ERM within 2–5 years post-operatively. Risk is higher after complicated surgery (capsular rupture, vitreous loss). Modern small-incision phacoemulsification has lower risk than older extracapsular techniques.

- **Previous retinal tear or detachment** – 10–20% risk of ERM following retinal tear (even without surgery) and up to 30–50% following retinal detachment repair (especially with vitrectomy and gas tamponade).

- **Ocular trauma** – Blunt trauma (e.g., sports injury, fall, assault) causes PVD and retinal edema, leading to ERM in 5–10% of cases.

- **Uveitis** – Chronic anterior, intermediate, or posterior uveitis (especially sarcoidosis, JIA-associated uveitis) increases ERM risk.

- **Diabetes mellitus** – Weak association; ERM is more common in diabetic patients with diabetic retinopathy (especially after laser or vitrectomy).

- **Retinal vein occlusion** – Branch or central retinal vein occlusion leads to ERM in 10–20% of eyes within 1–2 years (due to hemorrhage and inflammation).

### Weak or Unconfirmed Risk Factors

- **Hypertension** – No consistent association.
- **Smoking** – Not clearly linked to ERM (unlike AMD or nuclear cataract).
- **Alcohol use** – No association.
- **Diet** – No proven dietary risk factors.

## Genetic Factors

- **Familial ERM** – Rare (<1% of cases). Autosomal dominant inheritance described in a few families with mutations in:
    - **TGFB2** (transforming growth factor beta 2) – Promotes fibrosis and cell proliferation.
    - **FZD4** (frizzled-4) – Associated with familial exudative vitreoretinopathy (FEVR), which can cause ERM.
    - **LRP5, NDP** – Other FEVR-associated genes.

- **Polymorphisms** – Common variants in TGFB1, MMP2, and TIMP2 genes may influence susceptibility to ERM formation after PVD, but not clinically useful.

- **Retinitis pigmentosa genes** – Many RP genes (RHO, RPGR, USH2A) can cause secondary ERM due to chronic inflammation and vitreous changes.

**Clinical genetic testing**: Not recommended for routine ERM. Only indicated for early-onset (<40 years) or familial cases with syndromic features.

## Environmental Factors

- **Occupational eye trauma risk** – Boxers, martial artists, military combat, construction workers (high-velocity projectile risk) have higher rates of ocular trauma and secondary ERM.

- **High altitude** – Not a risk factor.

- **UV light exposure** – Not linked to ERM (unlike cataracts and AMD).

## Prevention Strategies

### Primary prevention (preventing onset)

**No proven strategies** to prevent idiopathic ERM, as PVD is an age-related physiological process.

However, for secondary ERM:

- **Minimize surgical trauma** – Modern small-incision cataract surgery reduces ERM risk compared to older techniques.
- **Prophylactic laser for retinal tears** – Treating retinal breaks may reduce ERM risk by preventing chronic vitreous traction and inflammation, though evidence is limited.
- **Control uveitis aggressively** – Topical or systemic immunosuppression (steroids, steroid-sparing agents) reduces chronic inflammation, lowering ERM risk.
- **Prevent ocular trauma** – Use protective eyewear for high-risk activities (sports, construction, military).

### Secondary prevention (slowing progression in established ERM)

- **Observation (most cases)** – Since 50–70% of ERMs do not progress, initial management is observation with serial OCTs every 6–12 months.
- **Treat underlying inflammation** – If uveitis or diabetic retinopathy is present, controlling the primary disease may slow ERM progression.
- **No proven medical therapy** – No eye drops, oral medications, or injections prevent ERM progression.

### Tertiary prevention (preventing vision loss)

- **Vitrectomy with ERM peeling** – For symptomatic ERM causing vision loss (usually <20/40) or disabling metamorphopsia. Surgery prevents further progression and improves vision in most cases.
- **Treat macular edema** – If ERM causes cystoid macular edema, intravitreal anti-VEGF or corticosteroid injections (off-label) may temporarily improve vision, but definitive treatment is vitrectomy.
- **Monitor for macular hole** – Patients with ERM and vitreomacular traction are at risk for macular hole; early vitrectomy may prevent hole formation.

**Key message**: Macular pucker is largely unpreventable because posterior vitreous detachment is an age-related process. However, most ERMs are asymptomatic or mild, and observation is safe. For progressive or symptomatic ERM, vitrectomy is highly effective. Do not undergo surgery for asymptomatic ERM; the risks of surgery outweigh benefits in stable, mild cases.

# Nuclear Cataract

## Definition

A nuclear cataract is a type of age-related cataract that forms in the central nucleus (core) of the eye's natural lens. It is characterized by progressive hardening (sclerosis) and yellow-to-brown discoloration of the lens nucleus, leading to gradual, painless vision loss. Nuclear cataract is the most common type of age-related cataract and is strongly associated with cumulative ultraviolet B (UV-B) light exposure and oxidative stress.

Unlike cortical cataracts (which cause glare from peripheral spoke-like opacities) or posterior subcapsular cataracts (which cause disabling glare and near vision loss), nuclear cataracts primarily cause **gradual blurring of distance vision**, **reduced color perception** (especially blues and greens), and **improved near vision** (second sight) in early stages—a phenomenon called "myopic shift."

## How the Eye is Affected

**Affected structure**: The lens nucleus—the central, oldest part of the crystalline lens, located deep to the lens cortex and surrounded by the lens capsule. The nucleus is composed of densely packed lens fiber cells that are laid down embryonically and never shed; they accumulate damage over a lifetime.

**Mechanism of opacification**:

1. **Oxidative stress accumulation** – Over decades, reactive oxygen species (from UV light, smoking, and normal metabolism) damage lens crystallin proteins. The nucleus is particularly vulnerable because it has no capacity for protein turnover (new proteins are not synthesized in the mature nucleus).

2. **Protein cross-linking and aggregation** – Damaged crystallin proteins form disulfide bonds and other cross-links, creating high-molecular-weight protein aggregates that scatter light.

3. **Pigment accumulation** – Oxidized proteins undergo non-enzymatic browning (similar to the Maillard reaction in cooking), accumulating yellowish-brown pigments that absorb blue light.

4. **Increased refractive index** – The aggregated, pigmented proteins increase the lens's refractive index (light-bending power). This causes **myopic shift** (increased nearsightedness), temporarily improving near vision—the "second sight" phenomenon.

5. **Progressive hardening (nuclear sclerosis)** – The nucleus becomes increasingly dense and hard, making it more difficult to phacoemulsify (break up) during cataract surgery.

**Effect on vision**:

- **Light absorption**: Yellow-brown pigments absorb short-wavelength (blue) light, causing colors to appear yellowish or brownish (xanthopsia and browning) and reducing blue-green discrimination.
- **Light scatter**: Protein aggregates scatter light, reducing contrast sensitivity and causing glare (though less severe than in cortical cataracts).
- **Increased refractive power**: Myopic shift improves near vision temporarily but worsens distance vision.
- **Decreased light transmission**: Dense nuclear sclerosis blocks light, causing dim, hazy vision.

## Types or Stages

Nuclear cataracts are classified by severity using the **Lens Opacities Classification System (LOCS III)** , which grades nuclear opalescence (NO) and nuclear color (NC) on a scale from 1 to 6.

| LOCS III Grade | Clinical Description | Visual Acuity (Typical) | Surgical Difficulty |
|----------------|----------------------|-------------------------|---------------------|
| **NO1 / NC1** | Clear or trace yellow; minimal nuclear sclerosis | 20/20–20/25 | Easy (soft nucleus) |
| **NO2 / NC2** | Mild yellow discoloration; early nuclear hardening | 20/25–20/40 | Easy to moderate |
| **NO3 / NC3** | Moderate yellow; definite nuclear sclerosis | 20/40–20/70 | Moderate |
| **NO4 / NC4** | Deep yellow to amber; dense nucleus | 20/70–20/150 | Moderate to difficult |
| **NO5 / NC5** | Brownish-amber; very dense nucleus | 20/150–20/400 | Difficult |
| **NO6 / NC6** | Dark brown to black (brunescent cataract); rock-hard nucleus | Counting fingers to hand motion | Very difficult (requires higher ultrasound energy) |

**Simplified patient staging**:

- **Early (NO1–2)**: Mild blurring; colors slightly faded; "second sight" (improved reading without glasses) may occur.
- **Moderate (NO3–4)**: Noticeable distance blur; difficulty driving at night; colors appear yellow-brown; near vision may still be good.
- **Advanced (NO5–6)**: Severe distance blur (cannot see road signs); difficulty recognizing faces; reading may become difficult as cataract progresses; lens may appear dark brown or black on examination.

**Mixed cataracts**: Nuclear cataracts often coexist with cortical and/or posterior subcapsular cataracts (mixed cataract). Pure nuclear cataracts are less common in older adults than mixed forms.

## Key Facts

- **Prevalence**: Nuclear cataract is the most common cataract type, present in approximately 60–80% of patients undergoing cataract surgery. Prevalence increases from ~5% at age 50–59 to >80% at age 80+.

- **"Second sight" phenomenon**: In early to moderate nuclear cataracts, increased refractive power causes myopic shift (more nearsighted). Patients who previously needed reading glasses may find they can read without them. This temporary improvement typically lasts 1–3 years before distance and near vision both decline.

- **Color vision changes**: Patients often report that colors appear "yellowed," "browned," or "faded." Blue-green discrimination is most affected. Post-surgery, patients are often amazed at how "blue" the sky appears.

- **Progression rate**: Slow, typically taking 5–10 years from early to advanced. However, progression can accelerate in patients with diabetes, high UV exposure, or smoking.

- **Risk factors**: Age (universal), UV-B sunlight exposure (strongest environmental risk), smoking, diabetes, and family history.

- **Surgery is curative**: Phacoemulsification with intraocular lens (IOL) implantation completely removes the opacified nucleus and restores vision. Success rate >95% in healthy eyes.

- **Brunscent cataract**: An advanced nuclear cataract that has turned dark brown or black (NO6). These are rock-hard and require higher ultrasound energy during surgery, increasing risk of complications.

- **No medical treatment**: No eye drops, supplements, or medications reverse or halt nuclear cataract progression. AREDS2 vitamins (for AMD) do not prevent nuclear cataracts.

- **Contrast sensitivity affected early**: Patients may have 20/25–20/30 acuity but poor contrast sensitivity—difficulty seeing steps, curbs, or faces in low light.

- **Driving difficulty**: Night driving is affected early due to glare and reduced contrast. Daytime driving becomes difficult in moderate to advanced stages due to distance blur.

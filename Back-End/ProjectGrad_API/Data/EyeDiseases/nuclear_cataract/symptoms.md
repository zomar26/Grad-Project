# Symptoms of Nuclear Cataract

## Common Symptoms

Symptoms develop gradually over years and are often bilateral (both eyes), though one eye may be more advanced.

- **Gradual, painless blurring of distance vision** – The most common symptom. Patients report that "everything looks hazy" or "like looking through a dirty windshield." Unlike cortical cataracts (glare-dominant), nuclear cataracts cause progressive loss of sharpness.

- **"Second sight" (temporary improvement in near vision)** – A classic early sign. Patients who required reading glasses may find they can read without them. This occurs because increased refractive power of the lens creates myopia (nearsightedness), which compensates for presbyopia. Lasts 1–3 years before declining.

- **Frequent eyeglass prescription changes** – Patients need stronger minus (myopic) lenses every 1–2 years as the lens nucleus hardens and refractive index increases.

- **Dim, darkened vision** – Patients describe that "colors are not as bright" or "the world looks like it's fading." Due to yellow-brown pigment blocking light transmission.

- **Yellowing or browning of colors (xanthopsia)** – Whites appear yellowish; blues and greens look muted or faded. Patients may not notice until after surgery (when they are shocked by how blue the sky or white a wall appears).

- **Difficulty driving at night** – Glare from oncoming headlights (less severe than cortical cataracts but still problematic). Reduced contrast sensitivity makes seeing road markings, pedestrians, and curbs difficult.

- **Reduced contrast sensitivity** – Trouble distinguishing objects from background, especially in low light or low-contrast situations:
    - Seeing white steps against a white wall
    - Reading gray text on a white page
    - Navigating in fog or twilight
    - Seeing facial expressions in dim lighting

- **Difficulty with outdoor activities in bright sunlight** – Glare and reduced contrast make golfing (tracking white ball against sky/grass), tennis, and cycling hazardous.

- **Monocular diplopia or polyopia (rare)** – Seeing double or multiple images with one eye. Less common in pure nuclear cataracts than in cortical or posterior subcapsular types.

- **No glare or halos as prominent feature** – Unlike cortical cataracts (severe glare) and posterior subcapsular cataracts (disabling glare with near vision loss), nuclear cataracts cause primarily blur and color changes.

## Advanced Symptoms

As nuclear sclerosis progresses to NO4–NO6 (dense brown to black cataract), additional symptoms develop:

- **Severe distance vision loss** – Visual acuity declines to 20/200 or worse (counting fingers to hand motion). Patients cannot read road signs, recognize faces across a room, or watch television.

- **Loss of "second sight"** – Once the cataract becomes dense, near vision also declines. The patient can no longer read without glasses, even with the myopic shift.

- **Dense central blur** – Vision is uniformly hazy; no improvement with pinhole or refraction.

- **Complete loss of color discrimination** – Colors appear uniformly brownish or gray. Patients may confuse blue and green, red and brown.

- **Inability to see in dim light** – Dense nuclear opacity blocks what little light is available. Patients report "I can't see at night at all" or "I need very bright light to read."

- **"Brunscent cataract" (NO6)** – The lens nucleus turns dark brown or black. On examination, the pupil may appear black or dark brown (instead of the normal red reflex). These are rock-hard and require higher ultrasound energy during surgery.

- **Functional blindness** – Vision reduced to hand motion or light perception in the affected eye(s). Activities of daily living (reading, cooking, walking safely) become impossible without cataract surgery.

- **Risk of angle-closure glaucoma (rare)** – As the lens swells and thickens (intumescent cataract), it can push the iris forward, narrowing the anterior chamber angle and causing acute angle-closure glaucoma. Symptoms: sudden eye pain, headache, nausea, vomiting, halos, and blurred vision—an emergency.

- **Phacolytic glaucoma (very rare)** – Leakage of lens proteins through the capsule causes inflammation and elevated intraocular pressure in advanced hypermature cataracts.

## When to Seek Medical Attention

**Routine (schedule eye exam within weeks to months)** :

- Gradual blurring of distance vision not corrected by new glasses
- Difficulty driving at night or in bright sunlight
- Colors appearing yellowed or faded
- Frequent changes in eyeglass prescription (every 1–2 years)
- Needing brighter light to read (despite "second sight")
- Annual exam for patients with known cataract (to monitor progression)

**Urgent (same day or within 24–48 hours)** – Nuclear cataracts themselves do NOT require urgent care, but these symptoms suggest complications:

- **Sudden, painless vision loss** over hours or days – Not typical of nuclear cataract (which progresses over years). Suggests retinal detachment, vitreous hemorrhage, or macular degeneration.
- **New flashes of light or sudden increase in floaters** – Possible posterior vitreous detachment or retinal tear.
- **A curtain or shadow across vision** – Possible retinal detachment.

**Emergency (go to emergency department immediately)** :

- **Sudden eye pain with nausea/vomiting, headache, and blurred vision** – Possible acute angle-closure glaucoma from an intumescent (swollen) cataract. Requires immediate treatment to prevent permanent vision loss.
- **Eye pain with redness and vision loss** – Possible phacolytic glaucoma, uveitis, or endophthalmitis.
- **Trauma to the eye** – Can rupture the lens capsule, causing rapid cataract progression and inflammation.

**Important**: Nuclear cataracts are painless. Any eye pain with vision changes requires immediate evaluation.

## Symptoms That May Affect Daily Activities

| Activity | Symptom Impact |
|----------|----------------|
| Daytime driving | Distance blur makes reading road signs impossible; reduced contrast sensitivity makes seeing pedestrians at crosswalks difficult; glare from sun off windshields. Many patients stop driving voluntarily at moderate stages (20/50–20/70). |
| Night driving | Glare from headlights; halos around traffic lights; poor contrast makes seeing road edges and pedestrians very difficult. Usually impossible by moderate stage. |
| Reading (distance) | Not affected (near vision may be preserved or improved by "second sight"). |
| Reading (small print) | In early stages, improved (second sight). In advanced stages, dense blur blocks both near and distance. |
| Recognizing faces | In moderate to advanced stages, faces appear hazy or featureless beyond 5–10 feet. |
| Watching television | Subtitles and small details become difficult to see; colors look yellow-brown (e.g., blue sky looks gray-green). |
| Computer work | Distance blur not an issue (monitor at near distance); color shifts (blue light filtering reduces color accuracy). |
| Cooking | Difficulty seeing dial settings (distance); cannot read small-print measurements without bright light. |
| Walking outdoors on sunny days | Glare and reduced contrast make steps, curbs, and uneven pavement hazardous. |
| Golf/tennis/sports | Difficulty tracking white ball against sky or grass; reduced depth perception. |
| Recognizing traffic lights | Colors may appear yellow-brown (red and green can be confused in advanced stages). |
| Nighttime navigation | Severe difficulty; patients often refuse to go out after dark. |
| Taking medications | Difficulty reading small-print labels (near vision may still be good in early stages). |
| Occupational tasks | Surgeons, pilots, drivers, and others requiring sharp distance vision may be unable to work. |

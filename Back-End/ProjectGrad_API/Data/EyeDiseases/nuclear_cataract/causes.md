# Causes and Risk Factors of Nuclear Cataract

## Main Causes

Nuclear cataract is primarily caused by **cumulative oxidative damage** to lens crystallin proteins over decades, leading to protein aggregation, cross-linking, and pigment accumulation. Unlike cortical cataracts (which involve membrane damage and fluid imbalance), nuclear cataracts involve **protein modification** within the lens nucleus—the oldest, most metabolically inactive part of the lens.

**Key pathophysiologic mechanisms**:

1. **Oxidative stress** – Reactive oxygen species (ROS) from UV light, normal metabolism, and environmental toxins (cigarette smoke) oxidize methionine and cysteine residues in crystallin proteins, leading to unfolding and aggregation.

2. **Advanced glycation end-products (AGEs)** – Glucose (and other reducing sugars) non-enzymatically binds to lens proteins, forming AGEs. These cross-linked proteins are yellow-brown, accumulate with age, and are accelerated in diabetes. AGEs contribute to the characteristic browning of nuclear cataracts.

3. **Glutathione depletion** – Glutathione (GSH) is the lens's primary antioxidant. Nuclear GSH levels decline with age (by ~50% by age 70), reducing protection against oxidative damage. The lens nucleus cannot synthesize new GSH; it must be transported from the cortex, a process that becomes less efficient with age.

4. **Calpain activation** – Oxidative stress activates calpains (calcium-dependent proteases), which cleave crystallins, generating light-scattering fragments.

5. **Decreased chaperone function** – Alpha-crystallin normally acts as a molecular chaperone, preventing aggregation of damaged proteins. Oxidative damage impairs alpha-crystallin's chaperone function, accelerating aggregation.

6. **Increased refractive index** – Protein aggregation and cross-linking increase the packing density of lens fibers, raising the refractive index (myopic shift).

## Risk Factors

### Non-modifiable Risk Factors

- **Advanced age** – The strongest risk factor. Nuclear cataract prevalence increases from ~5% at age 50–59 to >80% by age 80–89. Age-related decline in antioxidants (glutathione) and accumulation of damaged proteins are universal.

- **Female sex** – Women have higher rates of nuclear cataract than men, even after adjusting for age and UV exposure. Possible mechanisms: longer life expectancy, hormonal factors (estrogen decline after menopause), and smaller lens size (higher protein concentration).

- **Race/ethnicity** – Nuclear cataracts are more common in Caucasians than in African Americans, Hispanics, or Asians (the opposite of cortical and posterior subcapsular cataracts, which are more common in non-Caucasians).

- **Family history** – Having a first-degree relative with nuclear cataract increases risk 2–3 fold. Twin studies show heritability of 40–50% for nuclear cataract.

### Modifiable Risk Factors

- **Ultraviolet B (UV-B) light exposure** – Cumulative lifetime exposure to sunlight UV-B is a major modifiable risk factor (stronger for nuclear than cortical or PSC). Risk increases with outdoor occupation, residence at lower latitudes, and lack of sunglass/hat use. UV-B generates free radicals that damage lens proteins.

- **Cigarette smoking** – Increases nuclear cataract risk 2–3 fold, dose-dependent. Smokers develop cataracts 5–10 years earlier than non-smokers. Smoking depletes systemic antioxidants (vitamin C, glutathione) and directly exposes the lens to oxidative toxins. Risk declines after quitting but remains elevated for years.

- **Diabetes mellitus** – Increases nuclear cataract risk 1.5–3 fold, with stronger association than for cortical cataracts. Hyperglycemia leads to:
    - Advanced glycation end-product (AGE) formation (brown pigments)
    - Sorbitol accumulation (osmotic stress)
    - Increased oxidative stress
- **Poor glycemic control** (elevated HbA1c) accelerates progression. Type 2 diabetes of long duration (>10 years) carries higher risk.

- **Obesity (BMI ≥30)** – Increases risk through oxidative stress, systemic inflammation, and diabetes association. Central obesity (high waist-to-hip ratio) may be particularly detrimental.

- **Hypertension** – Associated with 1.5–2 fold increased risk, possibly through vascular damage and oxidative stress. Untreated hypertension carries higher risk than treated.

- **Low dietary antioxidant intake** – Low levels of vitamins C and E, lutein/zeaxanthin, and carotenoids (found in fruits and vegetables) increase nuclear cataract risk.

- **Heavy alcohol consumption** – >2 drinks/day increases risk; moderate intake (1 drink/day) may be neutral or slightly protective (red wine polyphenols?).

- **Corticosteroid use** – Systemic corticosteroids (e.g., prednisone) increase cataract risk (all types, including nuclear). Dose-dependent; longer duration increases risk. Inhaled corticosteroids (for asthma/COPD) have lower but detectable risk.

- **Statins** – Some studies suggest statins may modestly reduce nuclear cataract risk (anti-inflammatory effects), but evidence is mixed. Not recommended for cataract prevention.

### Medications and Supplements

- **Estrogen (postmenopausal hormone therapy)** – Mixed evidence; some studies suggest reduced risk, others no effect.
- **Aspirin** – No consistent protective effect.
- **AREDS/AREDS2 supplements** – No benefit for nuclear cataract (tested in large randomized trials).

## Genetic Factors

- **Heritability** – 40–50% for nuclear cataract (twin studies). Several genes influence susceptibility.

- **Crystallin genes** – Mutations in CRYAA, CRYAB, CRYBA1, CRYBB2, CRYGC, CRYGD cause rare early-onset autosomal dominant cataracts (childhood or young adult). Common variants in these genes may modify age-related nuclear cataract risk.

- **Glutathione S-transferase (GST) genes** – GSTM1 and GSTT1 null genotypes (lack functional enzyme) are associated with increased nuclear cataract risk, especially in smokers. These enzymes detoxify oxidative compounds.

- **EPHA2 gene** – Variants in EPHA2 (ephrin receptor) are associated with age-related cataract (all types) in multiple populations.

- **DNA repair genes** – Variants in OGG1 (oxidative DNA damage repair) and XRCC1 are associated with cataract risk.

- **Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) variants** – Accumulation of mtDNA deletions with age impairs oxidative phosphorylation, increasing ROS production.

**Clinical genetic testing** – Not recommended for age-related nuclear cataract. Only indicated for childhood or early-onset cataract with strong family history.

## Environmental Factors

- **Sunlight exposure** – High cumulative UV-B exposure increases risk. Protective factors: wide-brimmed hats, sunglasses with 99–100% UV blocking, living at higher latitudes.

- **Occupational exposure** – Outdoor workers (farmers, construction, lifeguards, fishermen) have higher risk. Welders (UV and infrared radiation) also at increased risk.

- **Air pollution** – Limited evidence; fine particulate matter (PM2.5) may increase oxidative stress and cataract risk.

- **Radiation exposure** – Ionizing radiation (therapeutic, occupational) increases cataract risk, including nuclear type. Astronauts (space radiation) have higher cataract rates.

## Prevention Strategies

### Primary prevention (preventing onset)

- **UV-B protection** – Wear wraparound sunglasses labeled "100% UV protection" or "UV400" when outdoors. Wide-brimmed hats. Most effective when started in childhood/adolescence (cumulative exposure matters).

- **Smoking cessation** – Single most effective modifiable intervention. Risk decreases gradually after quitting but may take 10–20 years to approach non-smoker levels.

- **Diabetes control** – Maintain HbA1c <7% (or as individually targeted). Tight glycemic control reduces AGE formation and oxidative stress.

- **Antioxidant-rich diet** – High intake of fruits and vegetables, particularly:
    - Vitamin C: citrus fruits, bell peppers, broccoli, strawberries
    - Vitamin E: nuts, seeds, vegetable oils, spinach
    - Lutein/zeaxanthin: dark leafy greens (spinach, kale, collards), eggs
    - Carotenoids: carrots, sweet potatoes, tomatoes
- **Limited evidence** that supplements are beneficial; whole foods preferred.

- **Maintain healthy weight** – BMI 18.5–24.9.

- **Control blood pressure** – Target <130/80 mmHg.

- **Moderate alcohol** – Limit to ≤1 drink/day (women) or ≤2 drinks/day (men). No recommendation to start drinking for eye health.

- **Regular eye exams** – Every 1–2 years after age 60 (sooner if symptoms).

### Secondary prevention (slowing progression)

- **Address modifiable risk factors** – Smoking cessation, diabetes control, UV protection remain important after diagnosis.

- **No proven pharmacologic therapies** – No eye drops, supplements, or medications halt or reverse nuclear cataract progression. AREDS2 vitamins (for AMD) do not reduce cataract progression or surgery rates.

- **Manage myopic shift** – Update glasses prescription every 1–2 years to maintain best-corrected vision.

- **Optimize lighting** – Use bright task lighting for near work; yellow-tinted lenses may improve contrast (early cataracts).

- **Consider earlier surgery** – If cataract is affecting quality of life (driving, work, hobbies), proceed with surgery rather than waiting for "maturity." Modern surgery is safe even in early cataracts.

**Key message**: Nuclear cataract is nearly universal with extreme old age, but lifestyle modifications (UV protection, smoking cessation, diabetes control, healthy diet) can delay onset and progression by years.
